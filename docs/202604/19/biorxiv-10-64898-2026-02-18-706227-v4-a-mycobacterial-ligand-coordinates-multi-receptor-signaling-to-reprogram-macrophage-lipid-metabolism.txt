Abstract. How innate immune receptors integrate signals from complex microbial ligands 33 

remains poorly understood. Here, we show that a single microbial component can encode 34 

coordinated engagement of two pattern-recognition receptors to reprogram macrophage lipid 35 

metabolism and drive foam cell formation, a hallmark of necrotizing tuberculosis lesions. These 36 

lipid-laden macrophages are defined by the accumulation of cytosolic lipid droplets enriched in 37 

neutral lipids. The mycobacterial lipoglycan mannose-capped lipoarabinomannan (ManLAM) 38 

drives macrophage lipid droplet accumulation through combined activation of Toll-like receptor 2 39 

(TLR2) and Dectin-2, with distinct structural moieties selectively mediating recognition by each 40 

receptor. Dual receptor engagement induces lipid metabolic reprogramming and enhances NF-41 

kB-mediated inflammatory signaling, with lipid accumulation proceeding through an mTORC1-42 

PPAR γ-dependent pathway that is largely independent of NF-kB activation. ManLAM-induced 43 

lipid metabolic changes closely mirror those elicited during Mycobacterium tuberculosis 44 

infection, both in neutral lipid composition and in dependence on the mTORC1-PPAR γ axis. 45 

These findings identify ManLAM as a major mycobacterial driver of foam cell-associated lipid 46 

metabolism and suggest that the architecture of complex microbial ligands can organize multi-47 

receptor signaling. 48

Introduction 67 

> 68

Tuberculosis (TB) remains the leading cause of death worldwide from a single infectious 69 

agent 1 . Infection with Mycobacterium tuberculosis drives the formation of lung granulomas, 70 

which, under sustained immune stimulation, can progress from protective structures to 71 

necrotizing lesions that underlie lung tissue destruction and transmission 2-4 . A defining feature 72 

of this transition is the emergence of lipid-droplet-laden macrophage foam cells, which exhibit 73 

impaired antimicrobial activity, amplify pro-inflammatory and pro-necrotic signaling, and directly 74 

contribute to the necrotic core 4,5 . Foam cells may also provide a lipid-rich environment 75 

supporting M. tuberculosis persistence and sequester lipophilic antibiotics such as bedaquiline 76  

> 5,6

. Despite their central role at the interface of immunity, metabolism, and pathology, the 77 

mechanisms driving foam cell biogenesis in TB remain poorly defined. 78 

Several mycobacterial components, most of them associated with the cell envelope, have 79 

been linked to macrophage lipid droplet accumulation and engage multiple pattern-recognition 80 

receptors. How signals from these receptors are coordinated to drive macrophage lipid 81 

remodeling remains unclear. Mannose-capped lipoarabinomannan (ManLAM) is the best 82 

characterized of these factors. ManLAM is a ~17-kDa structurally heterogeneous lipoglycan 83 

composed of a mannosyl-phosphatidylinositol anchor, a mannan core, and an arabinan domain 84 

that is capped with terminal mannose residues in pathogenic mycobacteria, including the M. 85 

tuberculosis complex 7-9 . In addition to its structural role in cell wall integrity 10 , ManLAM is a 86 

major determinant of mycobacterial virulence, disrupting macrophage phagosome maturation 87 

and modulating adaptive immune responses , including CD1-restricted T cell activation, CD4 ⁺ T88 

cell function, and cytokine and antibody production by B cells 8,9 . Such pleiotropic effects reflect 89 

ManLAM’s capacity to engage multiple pattern-recognition receptors on macrophages, including 90 

C-type lectin receptors (CLRs) such as the mannose receptor and Dectin-2, as well as Toll-like 91 

receptor (TLR) 2. Notably, ManLAM binds Dectin-2 with high avidity 11,12 , whereas its interaction 92 

with TLR2 occurs with comparatively low affinity 13,14 . Seminal work by D’Avila et al. 15 

> 93

demonstrated that TLR2 is required for ManLAM-induced lipid droplet accumulation, yet 94 

activation of TLR2 alone by a potent agonist fails to induce this response 16 . These observations 95 

indicate that TLR2 signaling is necessary but not sufficient to promote foam cell formation, 96 

leaving unresolved the receptor mechanism by which ManLAM drives lipid droplet biogenesis. 97 

We identify coordinated engagement of TLR2 and Dectin-2 as the mechanistic principle 98 

underlying ManLAM-driven foam cell formation. Distinct structural moieties within ManLAM 99 

engage these pattern-recognition receptors to activate an mTORC1-PPAR γ axis that drives 100

Results 106 

> 107

M. tuberculosis ManLAM induces macrophage lipid droplet accumulation through TLR2 108 

and Dectin-2. 109 

Previous studies showed that the vaccine strain Mycobacterium bovis Bacillus Calmette-110 

Guérin (BCG), a member of the M. tuberculosis complex, induces lipid droplet (LD) 111 

accumulation in murine macrophages in a TLR2-dependent manner, yet activation of TLR2 112 

alone by the synthetic agonist Pam3CSK4 is insufficient to induce LD formation 15,16 . Similar 113 

results were obtained with BCG-derived ManLAM 15,16 . These observations left unresolved how 114 

BCG bacilli – and specifically ManLAM – promote LD biogenesis in macrophages. Because 115 

ManLAM is a relatively weak TLR2 agonist compared to bacterial lipoproteins (EC 50 is in the 116 

μg/ml vs ng/ml range) 13,17 , it was important to exclude the possibility that the observed TLR2-117 

stimulating activity of the ManLAM preparation resulted from trace contamination with highly 118 

active lipopeptides 18 . To address this, purified ManLAM was treated with 1% H 2 O2 , which 119 

inactivates lipoproteins by converting the N-terminal cysteine-thioether substructure required for 120 

TLR2 activation into sulfoxide derivatives 19 . Untreated and H 2 O2 -treated ManLAM were tested 121 

with a TLR2 reporter HEK293 cell line engineered to express alkaline phosphatase activity as a 122 

read-out of TLR2-dependent NF-κB activation (see Methods ). H 2 O2 treatment had little effect on 123 

the TLR2 agonist activity of M. tuberculosis ManLAM ( Fig. 1A ) while completely abrogating 124 

TLR2 activation by the sentinel lipoprotein LrpG ( SI Fig. 1 ). This result demonstrates that our 125 

ManLAM preparations are highly purified and express bona fide TLR2 agonism. 126 

Next, to investigate the mechanisms underlying ManLAM-induced LD accumulation in 127 

macrophages, we used immortalized murine bone marrow-derived macrophages (iBMDMs), 128 

which are well suited for genetic manipulations and closely recapitulate primary macrophage 129 

phenotypes 20 . Gene knockdown in iBMDMs was achieved by CRISPR-interference (CRISPRi), 130 

which employs a nuclease-inactivated Cas9 (dCas9) and promoter-targeting guide RNAs 131 

(gRNAs) to block transcription of target genes. We generated TLR2 knockdown (KD) iBMDMs 132 

by stably expressing dCas9 and two different gRNAs targeting the Tlr2 promoter (see Methods ). 133 

Control cells expressed gRNAs against an irrelevant target gene (bacterial luciferase, lux A). 134

ManLAM induced LD accumulation in control iBMDMs but failed to do so in TLR2 KD cells ( Fig. 138

previous reports 16 , stimulation of control iBMDMs with Pam3CSK4 alone failed to induce LD 140 

accumulation at any of the doses tested ( Fig. 1B-E ). The observed insufficiency of TLR2 141 

signaling prompted us to investigate whether ManLAM engages other host receptors to drive 142 

macrophage LD accumulation. 143 

Previous work has shown that M. tuberculosis ManLAM is also an agonist of Dectin-2 11,12 , a144 

surface CLR that recognizes microbial carbohydrates and has been studied primarily in the 145 

context of fungal infections 21 . Consistent with these reports, M. tuberculosis ManLAM activated 146 

Dectin-2 signaling in a reporter HEK293 cell line engineered to express alkaline phosphatase 147 

activity as a read-out of Dectin-2-dependent NF-κB activation (see Methods ) ( Fig. 2A ). H 2 O2148 

treatment had no significant effect on the Dectin-2 agonist activity of M. tuberculosis ManLAM 149 

(Fig. 2A ). To assess whether Dectin-2 signaling contributes to ManLAM-induced LD 150 

accumulation, we generated Dectin-2 knockdown (KD) and control cells in iBMDM using the 151 

same strategy described for TLR2. Efficient Dectin-2 knockdown was validated by reduced 152 

Dectin2 transcript levels measured by RT-qPCR ( SI Fig. 2D ) and by impaired induction of the 153 

downstream genes Il6 and Tnfa following stimulation with the Dectin-2 agonist furfurman ( SI 154 

Fig. 2E,F ). When LD induction was assessed, both ManLAM and H 2 O2- treated ManLAM 155 

induced LD accumulation in control iBMDM but failed to do so in Dectin-2 KD cells ( Fig. 2B ;156 

representative imaging flow cytometry data are in Fig. 2C ). In contrast, knockdown of other 157 

CLRs, including Dectin-1 and Mincle, had no effect on ManLAM-induced LD accumulation ( Sl 158 

Fig. 3A ). Moreover, treating parental iBMDMs with increasing doses of furfurman up to 10 μg/ml 159 

failed to induce LD accumulation ( Fig. 2D ; representative imaging flow cytometry data are in 160 

Fig. 2E ). Thus, as observed with TLR2, Dectin-2 activation is necessary but not sufficient to 161 

drive ManLAM-induced LD accumulation. 162

Dectin-2 signaling to induce LD accumulation in macrophages. To determine whether co-164 

engagement of these two receptors is sufficient, we treated iBMDMs with the TLR2 agonist 165 

Pam3CSK4 and the Dectin-2 agonist furfurman, alone and in combination. While neither agonist 166 

induced LD accumulation when applied alone, they did so when used in combination ( Fig. 3A ;167 

representative imaging flow cytometry data are in Fig. 3B ). These results demonstrate that co-168

We next asked whether specific ManLAM structural features are required for lipid droplet 172 

accumulation mediated by TLR2 and Dectin-2. Prior work showed that acylation of the 173 

phosphatidyl-myo-inositol anchor of mycobacterial lipoglycans is required for TLR2 activation 174  

> 14,22

(dManLAM, see Methods ) and mannose-cap-deficient ManLAM by enzymatic removal with α-177

cell lines, dManLAM failed to activate TLR2 while retaining Dectin-2 activation ability ( Fig. 3C ). 179 

In contrast, αtManLAM retained TLR2 activation but lost the ability to activate Dectin-2 ( Fig. 180 

3C ). We next assessed the ability of these ManLAM derivatives to induce LD accumulation in 181 

iBMDMs. In contrast to intact ManLAM, neither dManLAM nor αtManLAM induced LD 182 

accumulation ( Fig. 3D ; representative imaging flow cytometry data are in Fig. 3E ). These 183 

findings were recapitulated in human monocyte-derived macrophages (MDM) ( SI Fig. 3B ). 184 

Together, these results demonstrate that engagement of both TLR2 and Dectin-2 by ManLAM is 185 

required for macrophage LD accumulation , identify the structural moieties that mediate this dual 186 

receptor recognition, and establish its relevance in human macrophages .187

Structural integrity of ManLAM is required for full NF-κB activation. 189 

Because ManLAM is also known to activate the transcription factor NF-κB 11 , we next used 190 

NF-κB activation as a quantitative readout to compare signaling elicited by intact ManLAM with 191 

that induced by the structural derivatives used in the lipid droplet assays. NF-κB activation 192 

occurs downstream of multiple innate immune receptors, including TLRs and CLRs 23 . Upon 193 

receptor engagement, a series of signaling events results in the translocation of NF-κB from the 194 

cytoplasm to the nucleus (schematic in Fig. 4A ), where it induces expression of 195 

proinflammatory cytokines such as TNF-α and IL-1 β 24 . We therefore quantified NF-κB196 

activation by measuring nuclear translocation of the RelA (p65) subunit of NF-κB and 197 

downstream cytokine gene expression in macrophages treated with ManLAM or its structural 198 

derivatives. 199

fluorescence intensity in RAW 264.7 macrophages stably expressing green-fluorescence-201

TLR2 and Dectin-2 contribute to macrophage lipid droplet accumulation during M. 224 

tuberculosis infection 225 

To assess the relevance of our findings with purified ManLAM to live M. tuberculosis 226 

infection, we tested whether TLR2 and Dectin-2 signaling contribute to macrophage LD 227 

accumulation during infection. Macrophages infected with M. tuberculosis and subjected to 228 

knockdown of TLR2 or Dectin-2 exhibited a ~25% reduction in LD content compared with 229 

parental cells ( Fig. 5A,B ). This reduction indicates that both receptors contribute to maximal LD 230 

accumulation during infection. In light of our findings with purified ManLAM, these results also 231 

suggest that ManLAM represents a substantial, though not exclusive, driver of the LD-inducing 232 

capacity of the pathogen. 233 234

We next investigated whether ManLAM-induced macrophage LD accumulation resembles 237 

that observed during live M. tuberculosis infection. To define the chemical nature of the 238 

ManLAM-induced neutral lipids, untreated and ManLAM-treated iBMDMs were exposed to 239 

chemical inhibitors of diacylglycerol acyltransferase (DGAT), which converts di- into tri-240 

glycerides, or acyl-CoA:cholesterol acyltransferase (ACAT), which esterifies free cholesterol. 241 

Inhibition of DGAT, but not ACAT, reduced ManLAM-induced LD accumulation in a dose-242 

dependent manner ( Fig. 5C,D ), indicating that ManLAM induces triglyceride-enriched LDs, 243 

consistent with our prior findings with M. tuberculosis infection 26 .244 

We next examined the signaling pathways underlying ManLAM-induced LD formation. 245 

Previous work showed that LD accumulation during tuberculous mycobacterial infection requires 246 

the protein kinase Mechanistic Target of Rapamycin Complex 1 (mTORC1) and the downstream 247 

transcription factor peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma (PPAR γ) 16,26,27 . Both are 248 

key regulators of cellular lipid metabolism 28-30 . Inhibition of mTORC1 with rapamycin or of 249 

PPAR γ with GW9662 markedly reduced ManLAM-induced LD accumulation in a dose-250 

dependent manner ( Fig. 5E ), recapitulating findings in M. tuberculosis infection 16,26,27 . In 251 

contrast, although ManLAM activates NF-κB, LD accumulation was unaffected by inhibition of 252 

RelA (p65) nuclear translocation by JSH-23 ( Fig. 5E ). Similar results were obtained with a 253 

second NF-κB inhibitor, QNZ ( SI Fig. 6A ), indicating that Man-LAM-induced LD formation is 254 

largely independent of NF-kB activity. 255 

To validate this pathway separation at the transcriptional level, we examined how inhibition 256 

of mTORC1 or NF-κB affected expression of representative target genes following ManLAM 257 

stimulation. Rapamycin selectively inhibited ManLAM-mediated upregulation of the PPAR γ258 

target gene Plin2 , which encodes a lipid droplet-associated protein 31 , but did not affect 259 

induction of the NF-κB-regulated genes Tnfa and Il1b 32 (Fig. 5F ). Conversely, the NF-κB260 

inhibitor JSH-23 suppressed ManLAM-mediated induction of Tnfa and Il1b but not Plin2 . Both 261 

rapamycin and JSH-23 reduced ManLAM-induced upregulation of Hif1a , a gene controlled by 262 

both NF-κB and mTORC1 33,34 . Comparable results were obtained with the NF-κB inhibitor QNZ 263 

(Fig. S6B ). Together, these findings indicate that Man-LAM-induced LD accumulation proceeds 264 

through an mTORC1-PPAR γ-dependent metabolic program that operates independently of NF-265 

κB signaling, mirroring the pathway architecture observed during M. tuberculosis infection 26,35 .266

Discussion 268 

> 269

Foam cells are the cellular hallmark of necrotizing TB lesions 26 , yet how mycobacterial 270 

signals integrate to reprogram macrophage lipid metabolism remains unresolved. Here, we 271 

identify ManLAM as a key structural determinant of tuberculous foam cell formation and show 272 

that it drives this process by coordinating engagement of TLR2 and Dectin-2. Distinct structural 273 

features of ManLAM -- acylation and mannose capping -- selectively mediate activation of TLR2 274 

and Dectin-2, respectively 11,12,14,22 , and are jointly required for triglyceride-rich lipid droplet 275 

accumulation . These findings explain earlier observations that TLR2 signaling is necessary but 276 

insufficient for lipid droplet formation. Lipid remodeling proceeds through an mTORC1-PPAR γ277 

axis that is largely independent of inflammatory signaling. Together, these results support a 278 

model in which ManLAM co-engages two macrophage surface receptors through distinct 279 

molecular moieties, reprogramming lipid metabolism and achieving full inflammatory activation 280 

via separable pathways ( Fig. 6 ). We further show that, as the only M. tuberculosis -derived 281 

agonist of Dectin-2 11 , ManLAM contributes to full lipid droplet accumulation during infection and 282 

that the lipid droplets it induces mirror those elicited by live bacilli in both triglyceride 283 

composition and dependence on the mTORC1-PPAR γ axis. Collectively, these findings link 284 

ManLAM architecture to dual receptor recognition and macrophage lipid metabolic 285 

reprogramming during infection, providing a molecular basis for coordinated receptor 286 

engagement. 287 

> 288

Activation of multiple receptors by distinct structural features within a microbial ligand 289 

defines a mode of signal integration at the ligand level. In contrast, prevailing models of innate 290 

immune signaling emphasize receptor crosstalk, in which distinct microbial ligands engage 291 

separate receptors and signal integration occurs downstream of receptor activation – for 292 

example, cooperative signaling between CLRs and TLRs in fungal infections 36 . Embedding 293 

ligands for multiple receptors within a single microbial component may confer an intrinsic 294 

advantage by promoting spatial proximity of engaged receptors. This idea is supported by prior 295 

studies showing that productive crosstalk between Dectin-1 and TLR2 depends on spatial 296 

coordination of their ligands, often requiring scaffolded presentation at nanometer-scale 297 

proximity 37,38 . Such spatial organization promotes receptor clustering, increases effective 298 

avidity, and converts weak interactions into productive signaling 39 . By analogy, recognition of 299 

ManLAM by Dectin-2 and TLR2 may facilitate integration of parallel signaling cascades, 300 

enabling synergistic signaling even when individual receptor-ligand interactions are of low 301

ManLAM-driven lipogenic and inflammatory programs in macrophages appear to be 306 

functionally separable. This is consistent with established signaling biology, as TLR2 and 307 

Dectin-2, in addition to activating NF-kB 23 , can independently engage the mTORC1-PPAR γ308 

axis through PI3K/Akt signaling downstream of MYD88 or Syk, respectively 40,41 . At the same 309 

time, the synergistic effects of TLR2 and Dectin-2 co-engagement on both lipogenic and 310 

inflammatory outputs suggest convergence at shared regulatory nodes. One candidate is the 311 

PI3K/Akt axis, which lies upstream of both NF-kB and mTORC1–PPAR γ signaling 42 . ManLAM 312 

may therefore promote foam cell formation by activating parallel signaling pathways that 313 

convergence at common control points while preserving functional independence of 314 

downstream outputs. 315 

> 316

The lipid-inducing activity of ManLAM closely mirrors, but does not fully recapitulate, the 317 

response elicited by live M. tuberculosis . ManLAM drives triglyceride-rich lipid droplet 318 

accumulation in macrophages through an mTORC1-PPAR γ-dependent pathway that is largely 319 

insensitive to NF-κB inhibition, a signaling profile also observed during infection 26 . Lipid droplet 320 

accumulation during infection is reduced in Dectin-2- or TLR2-deficient macrophages, 321 

suggesting that ManLAM contributes to this response in vivo, particularly given that it is the only 322 

known M. tuberculosis -derived agonist of Dectin-2 11 . However, whereas TLR2 or Dectin-2 323 

deficiency fully abrogates ManLAM-induced lipid droplet formation, it only partially reduces lipid 324 

accumulation during infection, indicating that ManLAM contributes substantially -- but not 325 

exclusively -- to foam cell formation. Additional microbial and host-derived inputs, including 326 

trehalose dimycolate and mycolic acids 43,44 , cGAS–STING activation 45 , and TNF signaling 26 ,327 

likely cooperate in this process. Together, these observations support a hierarchical model in 328 

which ManLAM-dependent surface receptor signaling acts as an upstream driver of lipid 329 

metabolic reprogramming, while additional pathways shape the magnitude and persistence of 330 

foam cell formation during infection. 331 

> 332

By identifying mTORC1 and PPAR γ as downstream effectors of dual receptor engagement, we 333 

define key nodal control points within the lipid metabolic program that may be targeted 334 

pharmacologically to disrupt pathogenic foam cell formation. Because these regulators lie 335

Materials and Methods 342 

> 343

Mycobacterial strains and purified components 344 

Mycobacterium tuberculosis Erdman (TMC#107) was cultured in complete 7H9 medium, 345 

which consists of Middlebrook 7H9 base (BD Difco, Franklin Lakes, NJ) supplemented with 10% 346 

(v/v) oleic acid-albumin-dextrose-catalase (OADC) obtained from oleic acid (0.5 g/L, Thermo 347 

Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA), albumin (50 g/L), dextrose (20 g/L), and catalase (0.04 g/L all 348 

from Fisher Scientific, Hampton, NH). The medium also contained sodium chloride (8 g/L), 349 

glycerol (0.5% v/v), and was supplemented with 0.05% tyloxapol (all from Thermo Fisher 350 

Scientific, Hampton, NH). 351 

Mannosylated lipoarabinomannan (ManLAM) was extracted from M. tuberculosis Erdman 352 

and purified by a previously described method 46,47 . Deacylated ManLAM (dManLAM) was 353 

prepared as described previously 46 . Briefly, 200 μg of ManLAM was incubated in 200 μl of 354 

NaOH 0.1 M for 2 h at 37°C. After neutralization with 200 μl of HCl 0.1 M, the reaction products 355 

were dialyzed against water. ManLAM devoid of mannose caps ( αtManLAM) was prepared as 356 

described previously 48 . Briefly, 200 μg of ManLAM was incubated for 6 h at 37°C in 30 μl of α-357 

mannosidase solution (2 mg/ml, 0.1 M sodium acetate buffer, pH 4.5, 1 mM ZnSO 4 ) followed by 358 

a second addition of 50 μl of the same enzyme solution for overnight incubation at 37°C. The 359 

reaction products were dialyzed against 50 mM NH 4 CO 3 , pH 7.6. Elimination of α-mannosidase 360 

was achieved by denaturation (2 min at 110°C) followed by overnight tryptic digestion with 361 

trypsin/ α-mannosidase (2% by weight) at 37°C. αtManLAM was recovered after dialysis against 362 

water. Removal of mannose caps was controlled by capillary electrophoresis as described 48 .363 

dManLAM and αtManLAM were suspended in 1x phosphate buffer saline (PBS) pH 7.4 and 364 

stored at -20°C. To inactivate putative lipoprotein contaminating ManLAM and its derivatives, 365 

samples were incubated with 1% H 2 O 2 for 3h at 37°C, a treatment that converts the N-terminal 366 

cysteine-thioether substructure of lipoproteins into TLR-2-inactive sulfoxide derivatives 19 . At the 367
