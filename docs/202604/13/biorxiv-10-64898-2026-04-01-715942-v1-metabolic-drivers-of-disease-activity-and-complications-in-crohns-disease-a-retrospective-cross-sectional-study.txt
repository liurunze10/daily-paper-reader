36 Abstract 

37 Background : This study aims to examine the independent relationships 

38 between individual components of metabolic syndrome (MetS) and two key 

39 clinical outcomes in patients with Crohn's disease (CD): disease activity, as 

40 quantified by the Crohn's Disease Activity Index (CDAI), and the occurrence 

41 of complications. 

42 Methods : This retrospective cross-sectional study included 376 adults with 

43 newly diagnosed Crohn’s disease. Multiple linear regression was used to 

44 examine associations between metabolic parameters and CDAI scores, while 

45 multivariate logistic regression assessed links to complications. Analyses 

46 were also based on clinical CDAI cut-offs. Predictive nomograms were 

47 developed and internally validated via bootstrap resampling. 

48 Results : Multiple linear regression indicated that higher CDAI scores were 

49 independently associated with lower BMI (B = -5.866, P < 0.001), lower HDL-

50 C levels (B = -81.770, P < 0.001), higher triglycerides (B = 15.618, P = 0.001), 

51 and lower ESR (B = -0.375, P = 0.03). Multivariate logistic regression 

52 established low HDL-C (OR = 0.042, P < 0.001), low BMI (OR = 0.915, P = 

53 0.034), and high triglycerides (OR = 1.792, P = 0.007) as significant 

54 independent risk factors for complications. The developed nomograms 

55 demonstrated strong predictive performance, with an adjusted R ² of 0.207 

56 for the CDAI model and an AUC of 0.765 for the complication model. For both 

57 predictive tasks, the model incorporating separate TG and HDL-C 

58 measurements significantly outperformed the TG/HDL-C ratio model. 

59 Conclusion: Metabolic disturbances demonstrate a significant association 

60 with increased disease severity and a higher risk of complication 

61 development in Crohn's disease.

63 Index; Intestinal complications; Linear models; Logistic models

80 1. Introduction 

81 Crohn's disease (CD) is a chronic condition driven by immune dysfunction, manifesting as 

82 transmural inflammation of the intestinal tract. Its etiology is multifaceted, and patient 

83 outcomes often vary considerably 1. Persistent transmural injury results in irreversible 

84 intestinal fibrosis. Over time, the combined effects of chronic inflammation and fibrotic 

85 remodeling contribute to progressive bowel wall thickening, the development of strictures, 

86 and complications such as obstruction or penetration 2. Clinically, this heterogeneity is 

87 observed across two distinct sides: short-term disease activity, typically measured by tools 

88 such as the Crohn's Disease Activity Index (CDAI), and long-term structural sequelae, 

89 including strictures, fistulas, and abscesses. Despite the therapeutic advances brought by 

90 biologic agents, significant variability in patient outcomes persists. A subset of individuals 

91 continues to develop refractory inflammatory responses, fibrotic strictures, and penetrating 

92 disease complications 3. We urgently need to find a new biomarker that can predict both 

93 Disease activity and risk of complications. 

94 Over recent decades, the prevalence of both metabolic syndrome (MetS) and inflammatory 

95 bowel disease (IBD) has increased in parallel. This trend is especially evident in 

96 populations characterized by lifestyles associated with Westernization 4. A Western-style 

97 dietary pattern can promote intestinal inflammation by activating innate immune receptors 

98 and disrupting the gut microbiota 5. The state of obesity represents an established risk factor 

99 for inflammatory bowel disease (IBD). This association is mediated in part by underlying 

100 metabolic dysfunction, where chronic inflammatory processes serve as a key mechanistic 

101 pathway 6. The conclusions of existing studies on how obesity affects the natural course of 

102 Crohn's disease (CD) are inconsistent, reflecting a complex association between the two 

103 that has not been fully elucidated 7. No studies have really elucidated the underlying 

104 pathophysiological mechanisms.. 

105 Circulating metabolic factors, especially high-density lipoprotein (HDL), are regarded as 

106 passive lipid transporters and are indispensable dynamic platforms in the immune 

107 regulatory signaling network 8. Under normal physiological conditions, high-density 

108 lipoprotein (HDL) displays robust anti-inflammatory and antioxidant capabilities. It 

109 functions by suppressing the production of pro-inflammatory mediators from monocytes

111 within lipid rafts of antigen-presenting cells. This activity leads to the downregulation of 

112 major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class II molecules, thereby restraining excessive 

113 activation of T-lymphocytes 8. During states of chronic inflammation, the composition and 

114 functionality of HDL are subject to substantial remodeling. Specific alterations include the 

115 incorporation of acute-phase proteins such as serum amyloid A1 and a marked reduction 

116 in its intrinsic antioxidant enzymatic activity. As a result of these changes, HDL transitions 

117 into a dysfunctional state and may even acquire distinct pro-inflammatory properties 8. In 

118 CD, systemic metabolic disturbances may disrupt intestinal immune homeostasis by 

119 inducing dysfunction of immunometabolic products like HDL, thereby exacerbating 

120 inflammation and promoting complications. But the direct clinical evidence supporting 

121 this hypothesis is currently lacking. Therefore, this study aims to focus on circulating 

122 immunomodulatory metabolic components, integrating clinical cohort data to evaluate 

123 associations between metabolic disturbances and core CD outcomes and to construct risk 

124 prediction models. This may provide novel prognostic biomarkers and empirical evidence 

125 for understanding metabolic-immune-tissue repair crosstalk in intestinal inflammation, 

126 suggesting potential therapeutic targets.

127 2. Subjects and Methods 

128 2.1 Study Design 

129 This study used a retrospective cross-sectional study and was reported according to the 

130 STROBE Statement for observational studies. 

131 2.2 Study Population 

132 We included 376 patients first diagnosed and treated for CD at the First Affiliated Hospital 

133 of Guangxi Medical University between April 1, 2014, and June 30, 2025. Diagnosis 

134 complied with the "Chinese Guidelines for the Diagnosis and Treatment of Crohn’s 

135 Disease (2023, Guangzhou)" 9. Inclusion criteria: age ≥ 18 years; first diagnosis of CD at 

136 our hospital; availability of complete baseline MetS indicators and clinical data. Exclusion 

137 criteria: severely incomplete data; concomitant severe comorbidities( malignancy, liver 

138 cirrhosis, end-stage renal disease); pregnancy or lactation. The study was approved by the 

139 Medical Ethics Committee of First Affiliated Hospital of Guangxi Medical University 

140 (Approval No.:2026-E0012). 

141 2.3 Outcome Definitions 

142 This study used a dual-outcome analysis. The primary outcome variable 1 categorized 

143 patients based on CDAI score: clinical remission (CDAI < 150), mild activity (CDAI 150-

144 220), and moderate-to-severe activity (CDAI > 220) 10,11 . The primary outcome variable 2 

145 categorized patients based on the presence of complications. Complications, defined per 

146 guidelines 9, included fistula, abscess, stricture/obstruction, perianal disease, hemorrhage, 

147 perforation, and malignancy, confirmed by computed tomography enterography (CTE), 

148 magnetic resonance enterography (MRE), or endoscopy. 

149 2.4 Data Collection and Variable Definitions 

150 Data were collected from the hospital information system. Baseline characteristics: age, 

151 sex, height, weight, smoking history, time to diagnosis (symptom onset to diagnosis), 

152 disease location (Montreal classification) 9, disease behavior (B1/B2/B3/B2+B3) 9,

153 perianal disease, surgical history, current medications (5-ASA, corticosteroids, 

154 immunomodulators, biologics). Metabolic indicators: BMI, blood pressure(BP), fasting

160 2.5 Diagnostic Definitions for Metabolic Indicators 

161 Definitions followed the Chinese Diabetes Society guidelines 12 : Hyperglycemia: FPG ≥

162 6.1 mmol/L or 2-hour postprandial glucose ≥ 7.8 mmol/L, or diagnosed diabetes. 

163 Hypertension: BP ≥ 130/85 mmHg or diagnosed hypertension. 

164 2.6 Statistical Analysis 

165 Analyses were performed using SPSS 25.0 and R 4.5.1. Continuous variables were 

166 assessed for normality; skewed variables are reported as median (IQR) and compared using 

167 Mann-Whitney U or Kruskal-Wallis H tests. Categorical variables are reported as 

168 frequency (%) and compared using chi-square or Fisher's exact tests. Minimal missing data 

169 (e.g., disease duration in 15 cases, 4.0%) were handled via median imputation. All analyses 

170 were performed on the imputed dataset. 

171 Main Analysis 1 (CDAI score): Hierarchical multiple linear regression. Model 1 adjusted 

172 for clinical factors (time to diagnosis, disease behavior, location, and current treatment). 

173 Model 2 added metabolic indicators (BMI, HDL-C, UA, etc.) to Model 1; ΔR² reported. 

174 Main Analysis 2 (Complications): Hierarchical multivariate logistic regression. Model 1 

175 adjusted for clinical factors (disease behavior, time to diagnosis, treatment). Model 2 added 

176 metabolic indicators (BMI, HDL-C, etc.) to Model 1; the change in model χ ² was 

177 reported. 

178 Model Development and Validation: Nomograms were based on the final models. Internal 

179 validation was performed via bootstrap resampling (1000 repetitions). Performance was 

180 assessed by discrimination (AUC/adjusted R²), calibration (calibration curve, Hosmer-

181 Lemeshow test), and clinical utility (Decision Curve Analysis, DCA).

185 3. Results 

186 3.1 Baseline Patient Characteristics 

187 Among 376 CD patients, 255 (67.8%) had complications. The median CDAI score was 

188 257 (IQR: 220-297.48). According to clinical cut-offs, 5 patients (1.3%) were in clinical 

189 remission, 98 (26.1%) had mild activity, and 273 (72.6%) had moderate-to-severe activity. 

190 Table 1 (stratified by CDAI groups) showed significant differences (P < 0.05) for BMI, 

191 HDL-C, TC, LDL-C, smoking history, and TG/HDL-C ratio. No significant differences 

192 were observed for age, sex, time to diagnosis, disease behavior/location, surgical history, 

193 hypertension, hyperglycemia, TG, UA, ESR, hs-CRP, thyroid function, or use of 

194 biologics/corticosteroids. The proportion of patients with complications increased with 

195 higher disease activity (P < 0.001), while HDL-C (P < 0.001) and BMI (P < 0.001) 

196 decreased. 

197 Table 2 (stratified by complication status) showed significant differences (P < 0.05) for 

198 sex, use of biologics/corticosteroids, TG, HDL-C, TC, and TG/HDL-C ratio. No significant 

199 differences were found for age, smoking history, time to diagnosis, disease 

200 behavior/location, surgical history, hypertension, hyperglycemia, BMI, LDL-C, UA, ESR, 

201 hs-CRP, or thyroid function. The complication group had a higher median CDAI (272.70 

202 vs. 220.47, P < 0.001), lower HDL-C (P < 0.001), and a higher TG/HDL-C ratio (P < 

203 0.001). Male sex was more frequent in the complication group (76.6% vs. 62.8%, P = 

204 0.001).

212 ratio, etc.) to Block 1. The incremental explanatory value of metabolic indicators was

220 3.2.2 Independent Risk Factors for Complications 

221 Using complication status (no=0, yes=1) as the dependent variable, hierarchical binary 

222 logistic regression was used. Due to issues of perfect separation for "disease location" 

223 identified in preliminary analysis and multicollinearity among variables, a streamlined 

224 multivariate logistic regression model was constructed. Block 1 forced entry of known 

225 important clinical variables (disease behavior, time to diagnosis, treatment) and variables 

226 with a P-value of less than 0.1 in the univariate analysis (sex). Block 2 added five 

227 metabolic/inflammatory indicators (BMI, HDL-C, TG, ESR, UA) to Block 1, focusing on 

228 changes in pseudo R², likelihood ratio tests, and the ORs with 95% CIs of metabolic 

229 indicators. Hierarchical logistic regression revealed that HDL-C (OR=0.042, 95%CI: 

230 0.015-0.116, P<0.001) and BMI (OR=0.915, 95%CI: 0.842-0.993, P=0.034) were 

231 independent protective factors, while TG (OR=1.792, 95%CI: 1.170-2.745, P=0.007) was 

232 an independent risk factor (Table 4). Sex, time to diagnosis, disease behavior, treatment, 

233 UA, and ESR showed no independent predictive value after adjusting for metabolic factors. 

234 3.3 ROC Curves and Nomogram Models 

235 Based on the multivariate logistic regression results, an ROC curve was constructed for 

236 complication status. The prediction model's AUC was 0.765 (95%CI: 0.712~0.818). Based 

237 on the Youden index, the optimal cut-off was 0.612 (sensitivity 85.9%, specificity 60.3%), 

238 with a maximum Youden index of 0.462 (Figure 1). R software validation showed 

239 consistency with SPSS results(HDL-C: SPSS=0.042 vs R=0.037; TG: SPSS=1.792 vs 

240 R=1.805; BMI: SPSS=0.915 vs R=0.922). The nomogram was constructed using R results 

241 (Figure 2). The calibration curve indicated good calibration, with a mean absolute error of 

242 0.04. After bias correction via 1000 bootstrap resamples, the predicted probabilities 

243 remained largely consistent with observed probabilities across most ranges (Figure 3).

250 3.4 Sensitivity Analysis 

251 To evaluate whether using the TG/HDL-C ratio as a composite metabolic indicator could 

252 replace separate TG and HDL-C measurements, a sensitivity analysis was performed. For 

253 the CDAI outcome, the original model's R² (0.204) was higher than the ratio model's R² 

254 (0.165; absolute difference ΔR² = 0.039, 95% CI: -0.0093-0.0942). For complications, the 

255 original model's AUC (0.765) was higher than the ratio model's (0.698; absolute difference 

256 ΔAUC = 0.0675, 95% CI: 0.0326-0.1168) (Figure 5). Comparing ROC curves stratified by 

257 complication status confirmed the superior discriminative ability of the original model 

258 (Figure 6). The original model also had lower AIC/BIC values, indicating better statistical 

259 fit and parsimony (Figure 7). Bootstrap repetition (1000 times) further confirmed the 

260 performance advantage of the original model (mean AUC difference: 0.071, 95% CI: 

261 0.033-0.116). We found that the variation in the TG/HDL-C ratio primarily reflected TG 

262 levels (Figure 8). The original model was significantly better than the model using the TG 

263 / HDL-C ratio.

264 3. Discussion 

265 This study has delineated specific associations between metabolic disturbances and two 

266 pivotal clinical dimensions of CD. Regarding the clinical outcome reflected by CDAI 

267 scores, dyslipidemia —characterized by low HDL-C, low BMI, and a high triglyceride-to-

268 HDL-C ratio —constituted a central driving factor, alongside the non-metabolic contributor 

269 of ESR. For the outcome of structural complication development, low HDL-C emerged as 

270 the strongest risk predictor, with undernutrition (low BMI) and lipotoxicity (high TG) also 

271 playing significant roles. The complication prediction model developed based on these 

272 insights demonstrated excellent discriminatory power (AUC=0.765) and calibration, 

273 providing a practical tool for the early identification of high-risk patients, with its 

274 robustness confirmed via bootstrap validation. As a common influencing factor, HDL-C 

275 plays an important role in both inflammatory activity and structural progression in CD. 

276 We found that a Mendelian randomization study confirmed our conclusion that an elevated 

277 concentration of HDL-C with a genetic basis was significantly associated with a reduced 

278 risk of Crohn's disease, with an odds ratio of 0.85 13 .High-density lipoprotein (HDL) plays 

279 a dual role in physiological systems. The significant anti-inflammatory effect of HDL is 

280 mainly mediated through two different pathways. It can directly bind to and neutralize 

281 endotoxins in the circulation, and can also alter the membrane composition of immune cells 

282 by extracting cholesterol from specialized lipid raft domains, thereby weakening the pro-

283 inflammatory signal cascade. This latter action effectively suppresses signaling pathways 

284 associated with both Toll-like receptors (TLR) and T-cell receptor activation 14 . The 

285 HDL/S1P (sphingosine-1-phosphate) axis modulation of TGF-β might inhibit fibroblast 

286 activation, offering a potential mechanistic explanation for its influence on intestinal 

287 fibrogenesis and its predictive value for complication risk 14 . HDL exhibits a variety of 

288 anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. An important part of this work further 

289 identified sphingosine-osteoporosis ( S1P ), a bioactive lipid medium carried on HDL, as 

290 the main molecular component responsible for these protective effects 15-17 . HDL3 

291 subspecies synthesized by the intestinal epithelium (particularly in the ileum) are 

292 transported via the portal vein, bind to LPS-binding protein (LBP), and effectively 

293 sequester endotoxin, preventing TLR4 pathway activation in macrophages and thereby

299 Recent studies indicate that elevating HDL-C not only alleviates intestinal inflammation 

300 via ATF3-dependent anti-inflammatory reprogramming of macrophages but also directly 

301 enhances intestinal epithelial barrier function 19 . This indicates the protective potential of 

302 HDL-C in anti-inflammatory and structural damage. They also found that HDL-C levels 

303 inversely correlate with IBD inflammation and predict severity better than CRP 19 .

304 Relevant mechanisms have been previously reported; for instance, deletion of MCPIP1 in 

305 macrophages promotes M1 polarization and inhibits maturation into an anti-inflammatory 

306 phenotype via the ATF3-AP1S2 axis, exacerbating intestinal mucosal inflammation 20 .

307 Given that intestinal fibrosis involves fibroblasts and microbiota 21 , and our finding links 

308 HDL-C to complications, a connection to fibrotic processes is plausible, warranting further 

309 investigation. Whether and how HDL directly regulates intestinal fibrosis by influencing 

310 fibroblasts or epithelial-mesenchymal transition awaits future investigation. 

311 As a risk factor for complications, male gender may be related to the effect of sex hormones 

312 on the number, composition, and function of HDL 8, which explains why gender has lost 

313 its independent association after metabolic adjustment. 

314 High TG, also an independent risk factor for both outcomes, highlights "lipotoxicity." 

315 Research by a team from Peking University found that excess free fatty acids (e.g., 

316 palmitate) generated from hydrolysis in a high-TG environment can act as endogenous 

317 danger signals, directly activating the NLRP3 inflammasome in macrophages, leading to 

318 the release of key pro-inflammatory cytokines like IL-1β 22 , causing systemic and even 

319 widespread inflammation. A high-fat diet pattern induces oxidative stress in the colon, 

320 enhances the permeability of the intestinal epithelial barrier, and promotes inflammatory 

321 responses 23 . Even short-term, acute high-fat intake impairs gut homeostasis, rapidly 

322 disrupting the intestinal barrier, altering gut microbiota, and significantly suppressing the 

323 secretion of IL-22—a key cytokine for barrier maintenance by group 3 innate lymphoid

325 in the direct contact between the mucosal surface and pro-inflammatory mediators. This 

326 exposure aggravates localized inflammation and concurrently disrupts the delicate 

327 microenvironment required for effective tissue regeneration, thereby establishing a

328 foundation conducive to fibrotic complications. Separately, saturated fatty acids, including 

329 palmitate, inflict direct cytotoxic damage upon intestinal epithelial cells. Their mechanism 

330 involves the induction of endoplasmic reticulum stress coupled with compromised 

331 mitochondrial function, a sequence of events that culminates in programmed cell death and 

332 the consequent emergence of structural impairments within the physical barrier 25 .

333 Autophagy alleviates ER stress 26 , but high-fat diets can impair barrier stress response via 

334 TLR4/NF- κ B-mediated autophagy 27 . This reflects the inflammation-damage-repair 

335 failure cycle in high TG environments. Intestinally derived HDL protects the barrier 28 ,

336 linking lipid disorders to barrier damage and suggesting TG-HDL interaction. Elevated 

337 TG often accompanies decreased HDL function, weakening the antioxidant and anti-

338 inflammatory ability of intestinal microenvironment, promoting inflammatory cytokine 

339 infiltration and bacterial translocation. This imbalance in the metabolism-immunity-tissue 

340 repair interplay collectively drives the chronic inflammatory state in the gut, explaining 

341 why high TG associates with both short-term inflammatory activity (CDAI) and long-term 

342 penetrating/fibrotic complications (structural damage) in CD. Therefore, targeting lipid 

343 metabolism to reduce lipotoxicity may represent a potential novel strategy for improving 

344 CD prognosis. 

345 TG/HDL-C ratio is a recognized indicator for identifying insulin resistance and atherogenic 

346 dyslipidemia 29,30 . Our finding that separate TG and HDL-C models outperformed the ratio 

347 model suggests that in CD, TG and HDL-C may influence disease via relatively 

348 independent pathways. For instance, TG may lean more towards "lipotoxicity" and acute 

349 pro-inflammatory effects, while HDL-C may focus more on immunomodulation and 

350 barrier protection. The traditional composite ratio might obscure this complexity, whereas 

351 separate consideration captures more nuanced risk information, reflecting the complex 

352 specificity of CD.

353 We found BMI, as a common influencing factor for both outcomes, was closely linked to 

354 both CD inflammation and complication development. A study involving 601,009 

355 participants concluded that obesity is associated with increased CD risk 31 . However, there 

356 is a ' higher BMI, better prognosis ' paradox in CD 32 , which may be due to the inability of 

357 BMI to distinguish between fat and muscle mass ( ' obesity paradox ' ). Research indicates 

358 that "sarcopenia" and "visceral obesity" are associated with poor prognosis in CD, while 

359 simple nutritional assessments are not 33 . BMI, as a general anthropometric index, has 

360 limitations and cannot assess visceral fat content; the traditional concept of "obesity" may 

361 mask sarcopenia risk 34 . Recent clinical research suggests that in the specific context of 

362 CD, the simple label of "overweight/obesity" may not pose a major threat to core treatment 

363 outcomes 35 . Low BMI may be due to long-term activity leading to impaired nutrition, 

364 because growth retardation precedes the diagnosis of CD in children 36 . Chronic 

365 undernutrition impairs immune and repair functions. The hazard may lie in visceral/ectopic 

366 fat (e.g., creeping fat), which promotes inflammation and fibrosis via local 

367 immunometabolic activities 37 . In the context of obesity, adipocytes promote chronic 

368 inflammation by recruiting and stimulating CD8 + T cells and macrophages, a process 

369 partially mediated by β -2 microglobulin (B2M) 38. This mechanism's insights remind us 

370 that the clinical management of Crohn's disease patients should go beyond body mass index 

371 (BMI), including comprehensive nutritional assessment as well as evaluation of specific 

372 body composition indicators such as muscle mass and visceral adipose tissue. 

373 Our research utilized the significant metabolic components identified through statistical 

374 analysis to develop a clinical predictive model for the occurrence of complications. 

375 Clinicians can use this tool to synthesize and graphically represent the possibility of 

376 complications based on metabolic profile data, providing stratified monitoring and 

377 personalized intervention plans. We also constructed a streamlined model for assessing 

378 disease activity, facilitating rapid evaluation of short-term inflammatory status through 

379 simple calculations. Therapeutic approaches aimed at increasing circulating levels or 

380 enhancing the functional capacity of high-density lipoprotein cholesterol should be 

381 prioritized in patients exhibiting low HDL-C. For individuals identified with elevated 

382 triglyceride-associated risk, clinical management must emphasize the control of lipotoxic 

383 effects to alleviate related inflammatory responses. When low body mass index is

389 4.1 Limitations and Future Directions 

390 Several study limitations need to be considered. Given the cross-sectional nature of this 

391 study, the relationships determined reflect those at a single point in time and no causal 

392 inferences can be drawn from them. Furthermore, the potential influence of residual 

393 confounding variables cannot be completely ruled out. Although the predictive model 

394 demonstrated robustness in internal validation, its universal applicability needs to be 

395 confirmed through external validation in independent cohorts or prospective longitudinal 

396 studies. To enhance the accuracy of predictions, future research can also examine the 

397 integration of more specific physiological indicators, such as directly evaluating HDL 

398 function or conducting detailed assessments of body composition. Prospective, multicenter 

399 cohorts would be valuable to validate and refine the presented models. Metabolomic 

400 profiling could also help identify disease-specific pathogenic lipid species and pathways in 

401 CD. For patients identified with high-risk metabolic phenotypes, interventional clinical 

402 trials could be designed to explore the adjunctive therapeutic potential of targeted 

403 metabolic modulation.
