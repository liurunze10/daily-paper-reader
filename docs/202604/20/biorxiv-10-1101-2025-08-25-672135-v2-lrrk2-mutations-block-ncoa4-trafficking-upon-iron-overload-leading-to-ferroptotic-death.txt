Abstract: 25 

Altered iron homeostasis has long been implicated in Parkinson's Disease (PD), although the 26 mechanisms have not been clear. Given the critical role of PD-related activating mutations in 27 LRRK2 (leucine-rich repeat protein kinase 2) within membrane trafficking pathways we 28 examined the impact of a homozygous mutant LRRK2G2019S on iron homeostasis within the 29 RAW macrophage cell line with high iron capacity. Proteomics analysis revealed a dysregulation 30 of iron-related proteins in steady state with highly elevated levels of ferritin light chain and a 31 reduction of ferritin heavy chain. LRRK2G2019S mutant cells showed efficient ferritinophagy 32 upon iron chelation, but upon iron overload there was a near complete block in the degradation 33 of the ferritinophagy adaptor NCOA4. These conditions lead to an accumulation of 34 phosphorylated Rab8 at the plasma membrane, which is selectively inhibited by LRRK type II 35 kinase inhibitors. Iron overload then leads to increased oxidative stress and ferroptotic cell death. 36 These data implicate LRRK2 as a key regulator of iron homeostasis and point to the need for an 37 increased focus on the mechanisms of iron dysregulation in PD. 38 39

In the remaining ~90% of sporadic 49 PD, it is unclear what the underlying causes may be, although environmental factors like heavy 50 metals or pesticides (Aravindan et al., 2024), and various types of infection (Cannon and 51 Gruenheid, 2022) are under intense investigation. 52 An early candidate cause of selective vulnerability of dopamine neurons in PD was their 53 high dependence on iron metabolism required for synthesis of dopamine and production of 54 neuromelanin, which captures and stores excess iron (Jenner, 1989; Sulzer et al., 2000). PD 55 patients show dramatic losses of pigmented neuromelanin, which is a hallmark feature in 56 autopsied brain. The loss of neuromelanin results in higher levels of free iron, which is being 57 explored as a biomarker in early-stage PD patients by high-resolution MRI quantitative 58 susceptibility mapping (Guan et al., 2024). The mechanisms by which iron may be mishandled in 59 the SNpc are understudied, and causality to PD pathology is not clearly established. However, 60 there is increasing speculation that in PD increased iron toxicity may cause ferroptotic cell death, 61 making iron the Achilles’ heel of dopaminergic neurons (Jenner, 1989; Masaldan et al., 2019; 62 Riederer et al., 2023). To test these ideas clinically, a series of trials were initiated employing 63 iron chelators in PD patients, with mixed results (Devos et al., 2025). New formulations and 64 approaches are still under investigation, highlighting the importance of a better understanding of 65 the impact of iron on PD pathology. 66 A common risk factor for inherited PD are variants in the LRRK2 gene which encodes a 67 large kinase with a complex array of domains including armadillo repeat, ankyrin, WD40, ROC-68 COR (Ras of complex proteins [ROC] and C-terminal of ROC [COR]) GTPase domains, 69 leucine-rich repeat, and a kinase domain (Alessi and Pfeffer, 2024). The penetrance of PD in 70 carriers of LRRK2 pathogenic variants is typically ~50% at age 70, rising to 75% at 80 years, 71 implicating additional genetic and / or environmental factors in disease progression (Mata et al., 72 2023). LRRK2 variants have been the subject of intense research efforts which have 73 demonstrated an involvement in cellular functions that range from subcellular protein trafficking 74 (Singh and Muqit, 2020), lysosomal repair/function (Bentley-DeSousa et al., 2025), cytoskeletal 75 dynamics (Leschziner and Reck-Peterson, 2021), ciliogenesis and centrosome cohesion (Jaimon 76 et al., 2025; Li et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2025; Madero-Perez et al., 2018), mitochondrial function 77 (Buck and Sanders, 2025), synaptic transmission (Kuhlmann & Milnerwood 2020), and immune 78 responses (Peter and Strober, 2023). Despite much progress, the molecular basis of LRRK2 79 pathogenicity remains unclear. 80 All pathogenic LRRK2 variants, within and outside of LRRK2’s kinase domain, increase 81 its serine-threonine kinase activity, including the most common pathogenic substitution 82 p.G2019S (LRRK2 G2019S ) (Mata et al., 2023). In addition to autophosphorylation, LRRK2 83 substrates include numerous Rab GTPases that regulate vesicular transport; these are 84 hyperphosphorylated in cells from LRRK2 PD patients and models (Alessi and Pfeffer, 2024; 85 Steger et al., 2016). The downstream impact of each phosphorylated Rab on membrane transport 86 and signaling pathways appears highly dependent on the cell type and context, and the field has 87 not reached consensus on which of the many implicated LRRK2 variant phenotypes are the 88 driver of disease. Thus, there is a breadth of evidence associating LRRK2 kinase gain-of-function with PD 91 pathogenesis. 92 In the context of iron handling, previous work reported context-specific changes in iron 93 homeostasis in cells carrying the LRRK2 G2019S mutation in HEK293T cells and iPSC-derived 94 microglia (Mamais et al., 2025; Mamais et al., 2021). It was initially shown that the 95 phosphorylation of Rab8, a key LRRK2 substrate, reduced its interaction with its GTP exchange 96 factor Rabin8, which in turn led to the lysosomal degradation of the transferrin receptor. They 97 observed the accumulation of ferritin and free iron in microglia of LRRK2 G2019S mice following 98 injections with LPS, although the mechanisms underlying these changes in vivo were not 99 established (Mamais et al., 2021). While iron phenotypes have not been observed consistently 100 within LRRK2 G2019S mutant cells, single-cell RNA sequencing experiments did report a cell-101 specific loss in ferritin heavy chain mRNA within LRRK2 G2019S mice, specifically in astrocytes, 102 microglia, and oligodendrocytes (Khan et al., 2024). 103 Given evidence supporting a contributing role of dysregulated iron homeostasis in PD, 104 we explored the impact of LRRK2 mutations on iron transport and handling in a cultured 105 macrophage model. The data provide evidence that RAW macrophages carrying the 106 LRRK2 G2019S mutation have altered iron homeostasis in steady state conditions, which lead to an 107 inability to handle iron overload resulting in oxidative stress and ferroptotic cell death. We 108 observed a block in the normal iron-induced expansion of lysosomes and in the microautophagy 109 driven degradation of the ferritin autophagy receptor NCOA4. Interestingly, iron overload 110 conditions in LRRK2 G2019S cells leads to high levels of of the LRRK2 substrate p-Rab8 111 accumulating on the plasma membrane. This phosphorylation is resistant to the type I LRRK2 112 kinase inhibitor MLi-2, but remains sensitive to both the pan-specific type II kinase inhibitor 113 rebastinib and the newly developed LRRK selective type II kinase inhibitors RN277 and RN341 114 (Raig et al., 2025). Iron overload conditions are not common in healthy physiology but are a 115 significant factor in PD brains. Should this aspect of LRRK2 mutant function be relevant to the 116 death of dopaminergic neurons, our results would suggest the use of MLi-2 may not be effective 117 in this context, where LRRK2 selective type II kinase inhibitors would be active in iron related 118 pathologies linked to LRRK2. 119

LRRK2 G2019S RAW macrophages have altered iron homeostasis 122 

Macrophages ingest aged red blood cells and have a unique capacity to handle high levels 123 of iron (Winn et al., 2020). Therefore, we considered macrophages, given the high levels of 124 LRRK2 expression (Gardet et al., 2010), as a good model system to interrogate the impact of 125 pathogenic homozygous LRRK2 mutations on iron handling. To this end we generated a 126 CRISPR-knock-in RAW264.7 macrophage cell line carrying the LRRK2 G2019S mutation 127 (Supplemental Fig 1A ). To confirm the increased in LRRK2 kinase activity in this line we 128 probed cell extracts with two phospho-specific antibodies ( Figure 1A ). LRRK2 129 autophosphorylation was increased, as seen with an antibody recognizing LRRK2 pS1292 . A 130 second antibody recognizing phosphorylated Rab8 T72 (with some lower specificity for p-Rab10 131 and p-Rab35) (Lis et al., 2018), further confirmed an increased kinase activity in the CRISPR-132 edited LRRK2 G2019S macrophages, with no change in total Rab8 levels across the genotypes 133 (Fig1A ). Both phosphorylation events were reversed upon treatment with the type I LRRK2 134 kinase inhibitor MLi-2 ( Figure 1A, quantification in 1B ). These data confirm the successful 135 generation of the gain-of-function variant. We then performed unbiased whole cell proteomics 136

Fe 2+ is the form required by the cell to forge iron sulfur clusters and heme groups, while 142 excess free ferrous iron can catalyze the generation of free radical species through the Fenton 143 reaction (Galy et al., 2024). This makes Fe 2+ essential, yet potentially toxic, for the cell. Excess 144 Fe 2+ is rapidly captured by cytosolic ferritin where iron is harmlessly stored in the mineralized 145 ferric Fe 3+ state (Chasteen and Harrison, 1999; Melman and Bou-Abdallah, 2020). FTH and FTL 146 together form cage-like complexes that can hold up to ~4500 atoms of Fe 3+ . FTH has enzymatic 147 ferroxidase activity required to oxidize Fe 2+ into Fe 3+ for safe storage (Melman and Bou-148 Abdallah, 2020). We quantified the iron content and composition and found that LRRK2 G2019S 149 macrophages have reduced total iron content, marked by a selective loss of mineralized iron 150 Fe 3+ , and 2-fold increase in Fe 2+ content, compared to WT control ( Figure 1 F). This result was 151 validated using the fluorescent FerroOrange probe, which reported a similar 2-fold increase of 152 labile Fe 2+ in LRRK2 G2019S macrophages (Niwa et al., 2014) ( Supp Figure 1C,D ). 153 The iron quantification revealed a cellular reduction in total iron, yet we observed excess 154 FTL and reduced TfR which canonically suggests a translational response to iron overload 155 through the iron-response elements (IRE) within their mRNA (Galy et al., 2024; Rouault, 2006). 156 We therefore tested a series of additional iron-related proteins regulated by either the iron-sulfur 157 cluster binding iron responsive protein 1 (IRP1), or the Fe2+ regulated iron response protein 2 158 (IRP2). LRRK2 G2019S expression did not elicit any changes in the levels of HERC2/FBXL5/IRP2 159 axis ( Supp Figure 1E ), indicating IRP2 is not activated. In addition, we found the levels of other 160 proteins harboring IREs such as DMT1 or ferroportin were unaffected in LRRK2 G2019S 161 macrophages ( Supp Figure 1E ), implying the IRP1/2 axis is not involved in the upregulation of 162 FTL or downregulation of TfR. This indicates that the changes in FTL and FTH levels are not 163 through canonical iron response element mechanisms. 164 We further tested the enzymatic activity of the iron-sulfur containing IRP1 (also called 165 Aconitase 1 or ACO1) which is often used as a proxy to monitor any deficits in iron-sulfur 166 cluster assemblies (Beinert and Kennedy, 1993). As expected, given the evidence that the 167 translational regulation by IRP1 was unaltered, LRRK2 G2019S macrophages did not show any 168 change in ACO1 activity (Supp Figure 1F ). This further indicates that the delivery of ferrous 169 Fe 2+ iron to mitochondria for the biogenesis of iron-sulfur complexes remained intact. Therefore, 170 LRRK2 G2019S leads to a significant reduction in total cellular iron ( Figure 1F ), primarily due to 171 the loss of stored ferric Fe 3+ , and an increase in ferrous Fe 2+ , without an impairment of functional 172 delivery of Fe 2+ to mitochondria or the modulation of iron related protein synthesis pathways 173 (Supp Figs. 1E,F ). 174 FTH is critical for the lysosomal degradation of ferritin complexes through an autophagic 175 process termed ferritinophagy (Dowdle et al., 2014; Kuno et al., 2022; Mancias et al., 2015; 176 Mancias et al., 2014; Ohshima et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2024). Given the absence of FTH 177 expression, we considered this could cause a block in complex turnover, resulting in the 178 observed increases in FTL levels. To monitor the turnover of FTL we generated WT and 179 LRRK2 G2019S macrophages stably expressing N-terminal cherry tagged FTL to visualize its 180 cellular distribution (Ohshima et al., 2022). LRRK2 G2019S show defects in NCOA4 turnover upon iron overload 205 

The inverse of iron chelation is the response to iron overload, which requires NCOA4 to 206 segregate away from ferritin cages, resulting in a diffuse cytosolic distribution of ferritin that 207 stably mineralizes and stores excess iron as Fe 3+ (Galy et al., 2024). In high iron conditions 208 NCOA4 binds iron sulfur clusters, which promotes binding of the ubiquitin E3 ligase HERC2 to 209 drive NCOA4 degradation through the proteasome (Zhao et al., 2024). In a second degradation 210 pathway, ferric Fe 3+ bound NCOA4 can drive assembly into phase-condensed foci that become 211 internalized into lysosomes through a microautophagy pathway (Kuno et al., 2022). At later time 212 points of iron overload NCOA4 can also carry low levels of ferritin into the lysosomes through 213 microautophagy to maintain pools of labile iron (Kuno et al., 2022). The precise regulation of 214 these distinct mechanisms remains under intense investigation. In our experiments with ferrous 215 ammonium sulfate (FAS) to induce iron overload, we observe the expected diffuse cytosolic 216 staining of FTL in WT cells ( Supp Figure 2A,B ). In LRRK2 G2019S macrophages, FTL is already 217 diffuse in untreated cells, which remains the case upon iron overload, with the addition of a few 218 bright foci resembling phase-condensed foci ( Supp Figure 2A,B ). 219 A critical distinction emerged between the genotypes when imaging cherry-NCOA4. In 220 WT untreated cells cherry-NCOA4 is seen primarily within lysosomes ( Figure 2A ), but in 221 LRRK2 G2019S macrophages cherry-NCOA4 had limited lysosomal co-localization and appeared 222 in bright cytosolic foci ( Figure 2A, number and size of foci quantified in 2B,C ). In WT cells 223 treated with FAS NCOA4 appeared in bright cytosolic foci and within lysosomes, which became 224 enlarged ( seen upon FAS treatment in Fig 2A and Supp Figure 2C, quantified in Supp 225 

2D,E ). In contrast, LRRK2 G2019S macrophages showed NCOA4 accumulating in larger bright 226 cytosolic (non-lysosomal) foci, suggesting very little internalization into lysosomes in high iron. 227

To test the hydrolytic activity within 345 lysosomes in these conditions we employed a proteolytic sensor BSA-DQ Red that is internalized 346 into endolysosomes and becomes fluorescent upon cleavage by lysosomal hydrolyases (Marwaha 347 and Sharma, 2017). In steady state there was little difference in lysosomal hydrolytic activity in 348 LRRK2 G2019S macrophages, relative to WT ( Figure 4C ). Upon iron overload we observe a ~50% 349 decrease in activity in control cells, as observed previously (Fernandez et al., 2016). This 350 decrease was exacerbated in LRRK2 G2019S macrophages showing an ~90% reduction in 351 hydrolytic capacity reported by the probe ( Figure 4C ). This was not due to a lack of 352 acidification, as demonstrated by lysotracker probes that showed increased signal upon iron 353 overload in both genotypes ( Figure 4D ). 354 Together with the observation that the lysosomes within LRRK2 G2019S cells do not 355 expand like they do in wild type cells upon iron overload, and that there is a block in NCOA4 356 microautophagy ( Figure 2 ), the data indicate that the lysosomes within LRRK2 G2019S iron loaded 357 cells are dysfunctional. Very recent work has suggested that lysosomes in LRRK2 G2019S cells can 358 accumulate ferrous iron, which could promote lipid oxidation (Mamais et al., 2025). We 359 therefore employed the C11-Bodipy probe where the wavelength shifts to green upon the 360 oxidation of this lipid probe and observed an increased peroxidation of the probe within both 361 internal membranes and at the plasma membrane ( Figure 4E, quantified in F ). Upon costaining 362 with lysotracker deep red we further report the oxidized lipids localizing to lysosomal 363 membranes ( Figure 4G ). Discussion 370 

The literature describing pathogenic LRRK2 mutations in PD overwhelmingly supports a 371 model where a host of different mutations across the protein all result in a gain-of-function 372 kinase activity that has focused clinical trials on LRRK2 kinase inhibitors. Previous studies have 373 identified iron mishandling in LRRK2 mutant cells, and MRI approaches identify iron 374 accumulation as a biomarker in patient carriers of the mutation (Fernandez et al., 2022; Jia et al., 375 2023; Khan et al., 2024; Mamais et al., 2025; Mamais et al., 2021; Martinez et al., 2023; Nazish 376 et al., 2023; Oun et al., 2022). This work supports previous studies and further highlights 377 dysregulated iron metabolism as a potential pathway of interest in LRRK2-related PD. 378 Our data indicate a primary dysfunction in lysosomal dynamics and function in iron 379 loaded LRRK2 G2019S cells where they do not undergo lysosomal enlargement and begin to lose 380 hydrolytic activity. These organelles cannot promote the degradation of NCOA4, which then 381 accumulates in the cytosol within detergent-insoluble foci. The accumulation of peroxidated 382 C11-Bodipy within lysosomes is consistent with a recent preprint reporting that LRRK2 G2019S 383 cells accumulate Fe2+ within lysosomes that can results in lipid oxidation (Mamais et al., 2025). 384 Our data do not resolve the earliest mechansisms to explain why the kinase-active LRRK2 385 prevents the dynamic changes within lysosomes that are triggered by iron overload. However, 386 our experiments revelealed that the increased LRRK2 G2019S -dependent phosphorylation of Rab8 387 seen in iron overload was reversed only with a type II inhibitor, and was resistant to the type I 388 inhibitor MLi-2 even though the inhibitor engaged LRRK2 G2019S , as indicated by the loss of the 389 pS1292 autophosphorylation mark. These results suggest that iron treatment alters the 390 conformational landscape of LRRK2 G2019S differently, which could reflect LRRK2 as an iron 391 binding protein, or that a new protein interaction occurs in high iron that alters the conformation 392 of the kinase domain. One study identified the NCOA4 ubiquitin ligase HERC2 as a LRRK2 393 binding protein (Imai et al., 2015), although no follow-up studies have confirmed this. One 394 possibility is that LRRK2 could interfere with HERC2s capacity to degrade NCOA4. 395 In addition to this unusual feature of LRRK2 in iron loaded cells, the appearance of p-396 Rab8 at the plasma membrane was unexpected given the localization of LRRK2 has been 397 documented primarily at Golgi and lysosomes, particularly upon lysosomal damage. It is 398 possible that this reflects LRRK2 recruitment to the plasma membrane damage following the 399 increase in oxidized lipids, perhaps in an effort to repair the membrane given that established 400 function of LRRK2 (Bentley-DeSousa et al., 2025). This would suggest that the cell surface 401 pRab8 reflects a downstream consequence of altered lysosomal response to iron overload. Future 402 experiments will focus on the potential function of LRRK2 at the cell surface and whether this 403 localization could explain the differential sensitivity to the type I and type II kinase inhibitors. 404 Lastly, our experiments are limited to a cultured macrophage RAW cell line. It is 405 important to note that the impact of LRRK2 G2019S mutations on steady state iron homeostasis 406 appears to be highly cell and context specific. It was our proteomics analysis that initially 407 identified the loss of FTH and increased FTL within LRRK2 G2019S RAW macrophages. However 408 scRNAseq experiments from LRRK2 G2019S mice showed that most cell types had no alteration in 409 iron related genes, with the noted exception of microglia, some astrocyte cell types and 410

Functional experiments 411 examining iron homeostasis in LRRK2 heterozygous mutant iPSC derived lines have also seen 412 changes in ferrous iron accumulation in lysosomes(Mamais et al., 2025), and in mice showing an 413 induction of iron dysregulation in microglia upon LPS treatment (Mamais et al., 2021). In this 414 context our work adds some critical new insight into the consequence of the kinase active 415 LRRK2 on the processes of ferritinophagy, and degradation of NCOA4, highlighting an 416 unexpected localization of p-Rab8 to the plasma membrane. Future work will resolve the 417 underlying mechanisms and context specific actions of LRRK2 in iron metabolism. For now we 418 expand the experimental foundation for further exploration of LRRK2s impact in conditions of 419 iron overload.
