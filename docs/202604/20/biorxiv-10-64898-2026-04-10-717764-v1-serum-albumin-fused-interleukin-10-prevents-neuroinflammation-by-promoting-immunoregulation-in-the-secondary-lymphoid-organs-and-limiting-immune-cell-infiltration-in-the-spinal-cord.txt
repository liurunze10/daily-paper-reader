Abstract 43 

Interleukin-10 (IL-10) is a potent immunoregulatory cytokine that suppresses pro-44 

inflammatory cytokine production, reduces antigen presentation by myeloid cells, promotes M2 45 

macrophage polarization, and inhibits T cell activation. Despite these well-established 46 

immunoregulatory functions, efforts to harness recombinant IL-10 therapeutically have been 47 

limited by its short plasma half-life and poor retention in the secondary lymphoid organs (SLOs), 48 

key sites of autoreactive T cell priming in autoimmune disease. Previously, we engineered a 49 

fusion of serum albumin and IL-10 (SA-IL-10) with extended half-life and enhanced exposure in 50 

the SLOs following intravenous administration. Here, we integrate human transcriptomic 51 

analyses and a murine model of neuroinflammation, experimental autoimmune 52 

encephalomyelitis (EAE), to investigate how sustained IL-10 exposure in the SLOs modulates 53 

immune responses under inflammatory conditions. Human single-cell RNA sequencing 54 

analyses revealed reduced IL-10 expression alongside increased IL-10 receptor expression 55 

across multiple immune cell populations in treatment-naïve patients with multiple sclerosis (MS), 56 

motivating the investigation of IL-10–based immunomodulatory strategies. Prophylactic SA-IL-57 

10 administration prevented the development and progression of EAE with superior efficacy to 58 

wild type IL-10 and comparable protection to fingolimod, an FDA-approved MS therapy. 59

Introduction 73 

Interleukin-10 (IL-10) is an immunoregulatory cytokine secreted by myeloid and 74 

lymphoid cells, including macrophages, monocytes, dendritic cells, neutrophils, mast cells, 75 

eosinophils, natural killer cells, CD4 + and CD8 + T cell subsets, and B cells. 1,2 By engaging the 76 

IL-10Rα:IL-10Rβ signaling complex, IL-10 suppresses pro-inflammatory cytokine production, 77 

reduces antigen presentation by myeloid cells, promotes M2 macrophage polarization, and 78 

inhibits T cell activation. 3 IL-10 suppresses T H1 and T H17-associated responses by 79 

downregulating antigen presentation by activated dendritic cells (DCs) and macrophages, 1,4 

80 

reducing DC migration to the lymph nodes (LNs), 5,6 and inhibiting CD4 + T cell proliferation and 81 

effector cytokine secretion. 7,8 Through these mechanisms, IL-10 plays a key role in maintaining 82 

immune homeostasis. 9

83 

Despite its potent immunoregulatory functions, efforts to harness recombinant IL-10 as a 84 

therapy have been limited by its unfavorable pharmacokinetic properties, including its short 85 

plasma half-life (the mean terminal phase half-life of recombinant human IL-10 is 2.7 to 4.5 hr) 86 

and poor tissue biodistribution following systemic administration. 3,10,11 Wild type (WT) IL-10 has 87 

a molecular weight below the threshold for renal filtration and lacks recycling mechanisms, 88 

resulting in rapid renal clearance and proteolytic degradation in the bloodstream. 12 

89 

Consequently, IL-10 exhibits limited systemic persistence and poor accumulation in the 90 

secondary lymphoid organs (SLOs), critical sites of antigen presentation, immune cell activation, 91 

and autoreactive T cell priming in autoimmune diseases. 12 

92 

To address these limitations, we previously engineered a fusion protein consisting of IL-93 

10 recombinantly linked to serum albumin (SA), 11 an abundant, endogenous plasma protein with 94

Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic, autoimmune demyelinating and neurodegenerative 102 

disease where immune dysregulation contributes to damage of the central nervous system 103 

(CNS). 14 MS pathology is characterized by the activation and migration of autoreactive 104 

lymphocytes and innate immune cells into the CNS, resulting in demyelination, followed by glial 105 

cell activation and proliferation, and ultimately neurodegeneration. 14,15 Although current MS 106 

therapies reduce relapse frequency and manage acute symptoms, many require long-term 107 

administration, broadly suppress the immune system, and display limited efficacy in halting 108 

progressive forms of the disease. 14 

109 

MS is now understood to arise not only from the expansion of pathogenic immune cells, 110 

but also from impaired immunoregulatory networks, resulting in defective suppressor cell 111 

function and dysregulated immune responses. 16,17 Peripheral blood mononuclear cells from MS 112 

patients exhibit reduced IL10 mRNA levels and elevated pro-inflammatory cytokine production 113 

compared to healthy controls. 18,19 Regulatory T cells (Tregs) from MS patients display impaired 114 

effector function and suppressive capacity, 19,20 while inflammatory monocytes and dendritic cells 115 

are expanded and exhibit increased activation marker expression and pro-inflammatory cytokine 116 

production. 21–23 Consistent with IL-10-mediated immunoregulatory mechanisms in MS, several 117 

FDA-approved MS therapies, including glatiramer acetate, 24–26 interferon-b,27–30 and 118 

fingolimod, 31,32 have been shown to increase IL-10 production or IL-10 receptor signaling across 119

Despite the central role of IL-10-IL10R signaling in immunoregulation, administration of 122 

recombinant IL-10 has shown variable efficacy in experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis 123

to active EAE development. 38 Recombinant IL-10 administration has yielded divergent 127

in Lewis rats with MOG 35-55 /CFA-induced EAE when treatment was administered either at the 129

Subcutaneous SA-IL-10 administration prevents the development and progression of MOG 35-55 -203 

induced EAE 204 

SA-IL-10 was generated by recombinantly fusing murine SA to murine IL-10 using a 205 

(GGGS) 2 flexible linker, as described previously (Table S3) .11,42 SA-IL-10 was expressed in 206 

HEK 293-F cells and purified via affinity chromatography followed by size-exclusion 207 

chromatography, as described previously. 11,42 We sought to evaluate the effect of prophylactic, 208 

subcutaneous SA-IL-10 administration on EAE onset and progression. To this end, we induced 209 

active EAE in C57BL/6 mice by immunization with myelin oligodendrocyte glycoprotein (MOG) 35-210  

> 55

antigen emulsified in Complete Freund’s Adjuvant (CFA), followed by pertussis toxin to 211 

increase blood-brain barrier permeability. 43 

212 

To identify a dose of SA-IL-10 capable of preventing disease onset, we administered 213 

PBS; 10 μg WT IL-10; or 2 μg, 10 μg, or 20 μg SA-IL-10 (all equimolar with respect to IL-10) 214 

subcutaneously every other day beginning on day 8 after disease induction (Figure 2a) . We 215 

selected subcutaneous administration due to its clinical convenience and potential to enable 216 

patient self-administration. 44 Mice that received PBS, 2 μg SA-IL-10, or 10 μg WT IL-10 217 

developed severe hindlimb paralysis by day 16, as indicated by the elevated EAE clinical scores 218 

and an increased percentage of mice with disease score ≥ 1 (Figure 2b-c, Supp. Figure 2a-e) .219 

In contrast, treatment with 10 μg or 20 μg SA-IL-10 (both equimolar with respect to IL-10) 220 

prevented disease development in nearly all mice (Figure 2b-c, Supp. Figure 2a-e) .221

Discussion 362 

In this study, we evaluated the effects of sustained IL-10 exposure in the SLOs using 363 

EAE as a murine model of neuroinflammation. We show that subcutaneous administration of 364 

SA-IL-10, a long-circulating fusion of SA and the immunoregulatory cytokine IL-10, prevented 365 

the development and progression of MOG 35-55 -induced EAE. In EAE, SA-IL-10 demonstrated 366 

superior prophylactic efficacy to WT IL-10 and comparable protection to FTY720, an FDA-367 

approved MS therapy. In the SLOs, SA-IL-10 administration suppressed pathogenic T H17 cells, 368 

reduced myeloid cell activation, and dampened pro-inflammatory cytokine production, while 369 

expanding immunoregulatory myeloid populations and increasing the frequency of checkpoint-370 

expressing T H2 cells. Despite the absence of direct CNS targeting, SA-IL-10 administration also 371 

reduced leukocyte infiltration and activation in the spinal cord. Together, these findings 372 

demonstrate that enhancing IL-10 exposure in the SLOs via albumin fusion promotes 373 

immunoregulation and prevents the onset of neuroinflammatory disease in this murine model. 374 

Our scRNA-seq analyses revealed reduced IL10 transcript abundance and increased 375 

IL10RA and IL10RB expression in immune cells from treatment-naïve MS patients, motivating 376

Limitations of this study 511 

In this study, we focused on prophylactic administration of SA-IL-10 to prevent the onset 512 

of MOG 35-55 -induced EAE. SA-IL-10 was evaluated primarily for its ability to prevent disease 513 

development in this model. Because most MS patients initiate treatment after symptom onset, 514 

future studies will need to evaluate the effects of therapeutic SA-IL-10 administration during the 515 

chronic phase of disease. Additionally, as MS occurs in both chronic and relapsing-remitting 516 

forms, it will be important to evaluate SA-IL-10 in relapsing-remitting models, such as the 517 

PLP 131-151 model, to determine its effects on relapse frequency and disease severity. 518

highlighted in Table S2. 74 Sample size for murine studies was determined based on previous 675
