# Integrated bioinformatics and single-cell analysis identifies vascular aging-related hub genes and immune drivers in atherosclerosis. 

Xiaofeng Chen 1, Kunlin Zhou 1, Weichun Wang 1, and Jingying Wu 1*

1 Abstract 

2 Atherosclerosis (AS) is a chronic inflammatory disease closely linked to vascular senescence, yet the 

3 specific molecular mechanisms connecting aging processes to AS pathogenesis remain incompletely 

4 understood. This study integrated transcriptomic data from GEO datasets (GSE100927 and GSE43292) to 

5 identify vascular aging-related differentially expressed genes (VARDEGs). Following batch effect 

6 correction, 28 VARDEGs were screened and subjected to functional enrichment, protein-protein interaction 

7 (PPI) network analysis, and immune infiltration assessment. Seven hub genes ( MMP9, APOE, TNF, ICAM1, 

8 PPARG, CYBA, and NCF2 ) were identified and experimentally validated via qRT-PCR, confirming their 

9 significant upregulation in AS samples. Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) analysis demonstrated high 

10 diagnostic accuracy for six of these genes (AUC > 0.7), with TNF exhibiting superior performance. Immune 

11 infiltration analysis revealed profound alterations in 28 immune cell types, particularly monocytes and T 

12 cells, which correlated strongly with hub gene expression. Furthermore, single-cell RNA sequencing 

13 analysis (GSE184073) localized the expression of core genes predominantly to monocytes and T cells,

21 Introduction 

22 Atherosclerosis, a chronic cardiovascular disease, is the leading cause of death in humans and is the 

23 underlying cause of peripheral vascular disease, coronary heart disease, and stroke [1]. Its pathogenesis is 

24 complex and involves multiple cell types, including endothelial cells (ECs), vascular smooth muscle cells 

25 (VSMCs), adventitia fibroblasts, macrophages, and other immune cells [2, 3]. The key factors for the 

26 development of atherosclerosis include endothelial dysfunction, leukocyte adhesion, macrophage foam 

27 formation, and changes in the VSMC phenotype [4, 5]. Atherosclerosis is driven by both traditional and 

28 non-traditional risk factors [6]. Hypertension, diabetes, smoking, and hypercholesterolemia, which lead to 

29 the dysfunction of Ecs and VSMCs, have been identified as the traditional risk factors for atherosclerosis 

30 [7, 8]. Meanwhile, the nontraditional risk factors, including C-reactive protein, oxidative stress, and 

31 inflammation, accelerate arteriosclerosis [9]. Despite aggressive control of the risk factors for 

32 atherosclerosis, many people still die from its acute complications. The incidence and mortality rates of 

33 atherosclerosis increase significantly with age. Several studies have demonstrated the important role of 

34 vascular aging in the development of atherosclerosis [10, 11].

36 between vascular aging and atherosclerosis, the underlying mechanisms should be thoroughly examined. 

37 Vascular aging is a specific type of aging that involves the senescence of various cells in the arteries. 

38 Atherosclerosis is a chronic inflammatory disease in which cellular senescence occurs in atherosclerotic 

39 plaques [12]. Senescent cells secrete inflammatory cytokines and chemokines through the senescence-

40 associated secretory phenotype (SASP), which induces local and systemic inflammatory responses, immune 

41 system activation, tissue damage, fibrosis, apoptosis, and dysfunction [13]. The SASP of senescent cells has 

42 several proatherogenic effects that may involve vascular remodeling, plaque formation, and rupture [2, 12]. 

43 These events lead to vascular aging and accelerate the development of atherosclerosis via a series of 

44 structural and functional changes. Therefore, focusing on the role of vascular aging in the pathogenesis of 

45 atherosclerosis is crucial for delaying atherosclerosis. However, the potential role of vascular aging in the 

46 diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment of atherosclerosis remains unclear. 

47 In this study, we integrated an atherosclerosis dataset from the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) and 

48 vascular aging-related genes (VARGs) obtained from GeneCards to identify reliable vascular aging-related 

49 differentially expressed genes (VARDEGs) in atherosclerosis. Through rigorous bioinformatics analysis, 

50 we identified 28 VARDEGs and constructed a protein–protein interaction (PPI) network to screen for key 

51 hub genes. We further validated the expression of seven hub genes ( MMP9, APOE, TNF, ICAM1, PPARG, 

52 CYBA, and NCF2 ) via qRT-PCR and evaluated their diagnostic potential using receiver operating 

53 characteristic (ROC) curve analysis. Moreover, we employed immune infiltration analysis and single-cell 

54 RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) to dissect the relationship between these hub genes and the immune 

55 microenvironment, revealing a strong association with monocyte and T cell dynamics. Finally, molecular 

56 docking simulations were performed to explore the therapeutic potential of curcumin in targeting these 

57 aging-related hubs. Our findings provide novel insights into the molecular crosstalk between vascular aging 

58 and atherosclerosis, identifying potential biomarkers for early diagnosis and new targets for therapeutic 

59 intervention.

61 Animals

63 protocols of animal experiments were performed following the National Institutes of Health Laboratory 

64 Animal Care and Use Guidelines and approved by the Animal Ethics Committee at Fujian Medical 

65 University. ApoE −/− mice were randomized into the following two groups: Control, AS. ApoE −/− mice were 

66 fed a high-fat diet containing 0.15% cholesterol and 21% fat for 12 weeks to establish the animal model of 

67 atherosclerosis. ApoE −/− mice fed a standard chow diet served as the control.

102 DEGs related to atherosclerosis related vascular aging 

103 Based on the sample grouping of the combined GEO datasets, the samples were divided into the 

104 atherosclerosis and control groups. Differential analysis of genes between the atherosclerosis and control 

105 groups was performed using the R package limma, setting | logFC | > 0.25 and adj. P < 0.05 for the DEG 

106 threshold. DEGs with logFC > 0.25 and adj. P < 0.05 were considered upregulated, whereas those with 

107 logFC < −0.25 and adj. P < 0.05 were downregulated. The P-value correction method was Benjamini– 

108 Hochberg (BH). The results of the difference analysis were plotted as volcano plots using the R package 

109 ggplot2. To obtain VARDEGs, all DEGs with |logFC| > 0.25 and adj. P < 0.05 in the integrated GEO dataset 

110 were drawn Venn diagram with VARGs. Furthermore, heat maps were drawn using the R package pheatmap 

111 and the R package Rcircos map chromosomal location [22]. 

112 Gene ontology (GO) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes 

113 (KEGG) pathway enrichment analysis 

114 GO analysis [23] is commonly used for large-scale functional enrichment studies, including biological 

115 processes (BP), cell components (CC), and molecular functions (MF). KEGG [24] is a widely used database 

116 storing information on genomes, biological pathways, diseases, and drugs. We used the R package 

117 clusterProfiler [25] to perform GO and KEGG pathway enrichment analyses of VARDEGs. The item 

118 screening criteria were adj. P < 0.05 and false discovery rate (FDR) (q value) < 0.25 were considered 

119 significant. The P-value correction method was BH.

120 Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) 

121 GSEA [26] was used to evaluate the distribution trend of genes in a predefined gene set in a gene table 

122 ranked by correlation with the phenotype and thus determine their contribution to the phenotype. In this 

123 study, genes from the combined GEO datasets were first ranked by logFC values, and the R package 

124 clusterProfiler was used to perform GSEA on all genes in the integrated GEO datasets (combined datasets). 

125 Regarding the parameters used in the GSEA, the number of seeds and computations was 2020 and 1000, 

126 respectively. The minimum and maximum number of genes contained in each gene set was 10 and 500, 

127 respectively. The Molecular Signatures Database [27] (https://www.gsea-msigdb.org/gsea/msigdb) was 

128 used to access c2 gene sets. Cp. All. V2022.1. Hs. Symbols. GMT [All Canonical Pathways] (3050) was 

129 used for GSEA. Significance in the screening criteria for GSEA was set at adj. P < 0.05 and FDR (q value) 

130 < 0.25. The P-value correction method was BH. 

131 PPI network and hub gene screening 

132 The PPI network is composed of proteins interacting with each other and participating in biological 

133 signaling, gene expression regulation, all aspects of life processes, such as energy and substance 

134 metabolism, and cell cycle regulation. A systematic analysis of the interaction of proteins in biological 

135 systems is crucial for understanding the working principle of proteins in biological systems, the reaction 

136 mechanism of biological signals, energy and substance metabolism under special physiological conditions, 

137 such as diseases, and the functional relationship between proteins. The STRING database [28] 

138 (https://string-db.org/) was searched to determine interactions between known and predicted proteins. In 

139 this study, the STRING database was used based on VARDEGs, with a minimum interaction coefficient of 

140 >0.700 (minimum required interaction score: high confidence, 0.700) used as the standard to construct the 

141 PPI network related to VARDEGs. The closely connected local regions in the PPI network may represent

142 molecular complexes with specific biological functions. Genes that interacted with other genes in the PPI 

143 network were selected for subsequent analyses. Five algorithms in the CytoHubba software [29] were 

144 applied as follows: maximal clique centrality (MCC), degree, maximum neighborhood component (MNC), 

145 edge percolated component (EPC), and closeness [30]. In the PPI network, the VARDEG scores were 

146 calculated, and then the TOP10 VARDEGs were selected according to the scores. Finally, the genes 

147 obtained by the five different algorithms were interfaced, and a Venn diagram was drawn for analysis. The 

148 intersecting genes in the algorithms were vascular age-related hub genes. 

149 Construction of regulatory network 

150 Transcription factors (TFs) control gene expression by interacting with hub genes at the post-

151 transcriptional stage. The ChIPBase database [31] (http://rna.sysu.edu.cn/chipbase/) was searched to 

152 retrieve TFs, and only TFs with the sum of “number of samples found (upstream)” and “Number of samples 

153 found (downstream)” > 6 were retained. The regulatory effects of TFs on hub genes were analyzed, and the 

154 mRNA–TF regulatory network was visualized using the Cytoscape software. 

155 Additionally, miRNAs play important regulatory roles in biological development and evolution. They 

156 regulate various target genes, and the same target gene can be regulated by multiple miRNAs. To analyze 

157 the hub genes and the relationship between miRNA, through the StarBase v2.0 database [32], only miRNAs 

158 with “pancancerNum > 6” were retained, and the mRNA–miRNA regulatory network was visualized using 

159 the Cytoscape software.

168 Immune infiltration analysis 

169 Single-sample gene set enrichment analysis (ssGSEA) [33] quantifies the relative abundance of each 

170 immune cell infiltrate. First, each infiltrating immune cell type was labeled, including activated CD8+ T 

171 cells, activated dendritic cells, gamma delta T cells, and natural killer cells. Human immune cells have 

172 various subtypes, such as regulatory T cells. Subsequently, enrichment scores calculated using the ssGSEA 

173 were used to represent the relative abundance of each immune cell infiltrate in each sample, resulting in an 

174 immune cell infiltration matrix for the combined GEO datasets. Subsequently, the R package ggplot2 was 

175 used to draw group comparison maps to show the expression differences in immune cells between the 

176 control and atherosclerosis groups of the combined GEO datasets. Immune cells with significant differences 

177 between the two groups were screened for subsequent analyses. The correlation between immune cells was 

178 calculated using the Spearman algorithm, and the R package pheatmap was used to draw a correlation 

179 heatmap to show the correlation analysis results of the immune cells themselves. The correlation between 

180 hub genes and immune cells was calculated based on the Spearman algorithm, and the correlation bubble 

181 plot was drawn using the R package ggplot2.

195 Molecular docking 

196 The binding affinity and interaction mode between the candidate drug and the target were analyzed 

197 using AutodockVina 1.2.2. The molecular structures were obtained from the PubChem compound database 

198 (https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/). The three-dimensional coordinates of hub genes ( MMP9, APOE, TNF, 

199 ICAM1, PPARG, CYBA, and NCF2 ) were downloaded from the PDB 

200 (http://www.rcsb.org/pdb/home/home.do). For the docking analysis, all protein and molecular files were 

201 converted to the PDBQT format, all water molecules were excluded, and polar hydrogen atoms were added. 

202 The grid box was centered on covering the structure domain of each protein and accommodating free 

203 molecular movement. The molecular docking study was conducted through Autodock Vina 1.2.2 

204 (http://autodock.scripps.edu/).

408 Discussion 

409 Atherosclerosis is the leading cause of cardiovascular death worldwide [35]. Atherosclerotic plaque 

410 rupture leading to thrombosis may lead to life-threatening clinical complications including myocardial 

411 infarction, stroke, and sudden death [36]. However, the mechanisms underlying atherosclerosis remain 

412 unclear. Vascular aging is a key factor in the development and progression of atherosclerosis. Vascular 

413 remodeling related to vascular aging can lead to patients with high sensitivity to cardiovascular risk factors 

414 (such as hyperlipidemia, hypertension, diabetes, and smoking) who are more susceptible to atherosclerosis 

415 [37]. 

416 Upon exposure to cardiovascular risk factors, senescent cells accumulate in the arteries and release 

417 excessive inflammatory factors through the SASP, which may activate the senescence pathway and further 

418 senesce neighboring cells [38]. Inflammation is an important biological marker, and its activation can lead 

419 to high platelet reactivity, leading to blood platelet coagulation potential, increased blood adhesion, and 

420 thrombosis [39]. These events lead to vascular aging and accelerate the development of arteriosclerosis via 

421 a series of structural and functional changes. Vascular aging and arteriosclerosis interact in a vicious circle, 

422 which jointly promotes the occurrence and development of vascular-related diseases, such as coronary heart 

423 disease, hypertension, cerebrovascular disease, and chronic kidney disease[38, 40]. The impairment of 

424 endothelial function or barrier integrity caused by vascular aging can accelerate the deposition of LDL in 

425 the intimal layer, potentially triggering platelet aggregation and thrombosis [2]. The vascular inflammatory

It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted April 17, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.04.14.718580 doi: bioRxiv preprint

426 response affects the recruitment and adhesion of inflammatory cells and promotes the formation of 

427 atherosclerotic plaques [39, 41]. Thus, many forms of vascular aging are involved in the development and 

428 progression of arteriosclerosis. However, the specific mechanisms remain unclear, and further studies are 

429 needed to broaden our knowledge of vascular aging in the pathogenesis of arteriosclerosis. 

430 To the best of our knowledge, we identified 28 potential vascular age-related genes in arteriosclerosis 

431 for the first time using bioinformatics analysis. We further identified seven hub genes associated with 

432 arteriosclerosis, including MMP9 , APOE , TNF , ICAM1 , PPARG , CYBA , and NCF2 , using PPI network and 

433 key module analyses. The roles of these genes in arteriosclerosis have been studied extensively. For 

434 example, MMP9 is involved in plaque formation by degrading the extracellular matrix to weaken arterial 

435 walls, leading to cardiovascular remodeling [42]. APOE participates in the transformation and metabolism 

436 of lipoproteins, and APOE knockout mice are the most common models of atherosclerosis. TNF and ICAM1 

437 are genes that regulate inflammatory cytokines and participate in the chronic inflammatory process of 

438 arteries, playing an important role in vascular aging and arteriosclerosis, which further indicates that 

439 inflammation may be a same mechanism of vascular aging and arteriosclerosis[43, 44]. PPARG is a gene 

440 related to lipid metabolism[45]. PPARG increases insulin sensitivity, promotes lipogenesis, and plays an 

441 anti-inflammatory and anti-atherosclerotic role[46]. Both CYBA and NCF2 genes are involved in the 

442 metabolism of NAPDH oxidase. NADPH oxidase is the main source of reactive oxygen species (ROS), and 

443 oxidative stress is a risk factor for atherosclerosis[47, 48]. However, the roles of these vascular senescence-

444 related genes in the regulation of atherosclerosis have not been fully explored [1]. 

445 GO analysis showed that 28 VARDEGs were enriched in muscle cell proliferation (including smooth 

446 muscles) and its regulation as well as EC muscle cell proliferation and its regulation (BP). They were also 

447 enriched in lipids, atherosclerosis, fluid shear stress, the AGE–RAGE signaling pathway in diabetic 

448 complications, leukocyte transendothelial migration, and the osteoclast differentiation pathway (KEGG).

449 Atherosclerosis involves the pathological activation of various cell types, including immune cells (e.g., 

450 macrophages and T cells), SMCs, and ECs [49]. Increasing evidence has suggested that the transition of 

451 SMCs to other cell types, known as phenotypic switching, plays an important role in the development and 

452 complications of atherosclerosis [50]. All genes in the combined GEO datasets were significantly enriched 

453 in the IL12 [51], Pi3kci [52], Nfkb [53] and JAK–STAT signaling pathways [54], which are related to cell 

454 senescence and vascular aging. Inflammation is a key driver of atherosclerosis, and the upstream source of 

455 inflammatory factors might be cellular senescence [39]. Cell senescence promotes the occurrence and 

456 development of atherosclerosis by secreting proinflammatory cytokines (including IL-12, etc.) through the 

457 SASP [38]. The JAK–STAT pathway is an important signaling pathway that regulates the initiation and 

458 progression of atherosclerosis, and JAK–STAT activation has been observed in atherosclerotic lesions [55]. 

459 Further studies have reported that inhibition of the JAK–STAT signaling pathway can attenuate cell 

460 senescence and the SASP [56]. Therefore, treatments targeting the JAK–STAT signaling pathway may 

461 alleviate atherosclerosis by delaying cell senescence. 

462 Next, we constructed the mRNA–TF and mRNA–miRNA regulatory networks of hub genes using the 

463 ChIPBase and TarBase databases, respectively. The ROC curve showed that the expression levels of the six 

464 hub genes ( MMP9 , APOE , TNF , PPARG , CYBA and NCF2 ) in atherosclerotic samples were accurate in 

465 different groups (0.7 < AUC < 0.9). Furthermore, through the analysis of the immune infiltration sclerosis 

466 of arterial congestion appearance, the hub genes and immune cells may have a strong positive correlation, 

467 prompting the hub genes to release proinflammatory cytokines, further influencing the process of vascular 

468 aging and hardening of the arteries. More immune cells may mean more pro-inflammatory cytokines. This 

469 observation was further refined by single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) analysis of coronary artery 

470 disease samples (GSE184073), which revealed that the hub genes are predominantly expressed in 

471 monocytes and T cells. Specifically, T cells constituted the largest cell subpopulation, followed by 

472 monocytes, suggesting their dominant role in the pathogenic landscape of AS. A phenomenon in which

473 higher levels of inflammatory cytokines in tissues lead to a low-grade, sterile and chronic inflammatory 

474 state, defined as immunosenescence or inflamm-aging[57]. In addition to aging, other factors that contribute 

475 to inflammation include genetic predisposition, obesity, oxidative stress, changes in intestinal barrier 

476 permeability, chronic infections, and immune cell defects[58]. Conversely, interventions that reduce 

477 inflammatory markers, such as dietary restriction and exercise, can increase longevity[59, 60]. Therefore, 

478 promoting the resolution of inflammation is a potential treatment for alleviating cell aging and organ aging. 

479 However, it is still unclear which specific immune cell type plays a decisive role in vascular aging and 

480 atherosclerosis. 

481 Identifying effective therapeutic strategies targeting these hub genes is of paramount importance. Our 

482 molecular docking analysis explored the interaction between curcumin, a natural polyphenol with known 

483 anti-inflammatory and anti-atherosclerotic properties, and the six high-performing hub genes. The results 

484 demonstrated strong binding affinities (binding energy < -5 kcal/mol) between curcumin and all target genes, 

485 with the most pronounced interaction observed with PPARG . PPARG is a nuclear receptor that regulates 

486 lipid metabolism and inflammation; its activation is generally considered protective in AS. The strong 

487 binding of curcumin to PPARG suggests that curcumin may exert its therapeutic effects partly by 

488 modulating PPARG activity, thereby improving lipid profiles and suppressing inflammation. Furthermore, 

489 the predicted interactions with TNF and NCF2 imply that curcumin could also directly inhibit pro-

490 inflammatory signaling and oxidative stress. These computational findings provide a molecular basis for 

491 future experimental studies to validate curcumin as a targeted therapy for vascular aging-related 

492 atherosclerosis. 

493 Despite these promising findings, several limitations must be acknowledged. First, while we validated 

494 the hub genes using qRT-PCR, the sample size for experimental validation was relatively small. Larger 

495 clinical cohorts are needed to confirm the diagnostic utility of these genes. Second, the bioinformatics

496 analysis relied on publicly available bulk RNA-seq and microarray data, which may mask cell-type-specific 

497 nuances that only scRNA-seq can fully resolve; although we incorporated scRNA-seq data for validation, 

498 further dedicated single-cell studies on vascular tissues would be beneficial. Third, the molecular docking 

499 results are theoretical predictions; in vitro and in vivo experiments are required to verify the actual binding 

500 and functional effects of curcumin on these targets. Finally, the precise mechanistic link between vascular 

501 senescence markers and the specific immune subsets (e.g., specific T cell subtypes like Th1 or Th17) 

502 warrants deeper investigation using spatial transcriptomics to map these interactions within the plaque 

503 architecture. 

504 In conclusion, this study systematically identified a set of vascular aging-related hub genes that are 

505 critically involved in the pathogenesis of atherosclerosis. We highlighted the pivotal roles of monocytes and 

506 T cells, particularly through TNF -mediated inflammation, as key drivers of the disease. The high diagnostic 

507 accuracy of these genes offers potential for clinical application, while the predicted interaction with 

508 curcumin opens new avenues for therapeutic intervention. By bridging the gap between vascular aging, 

509 immune dysregulation, and potential natural therapeutics, our work provides a comprehensive framework 

510 for understanding and treating atherosclerosis. Future research should focus on translating these 

511 bioinformatics discoveries into clinical biomarkers and targeted therapies to mitigate the burden of this 

512 pervasive cardiovascular disease.
