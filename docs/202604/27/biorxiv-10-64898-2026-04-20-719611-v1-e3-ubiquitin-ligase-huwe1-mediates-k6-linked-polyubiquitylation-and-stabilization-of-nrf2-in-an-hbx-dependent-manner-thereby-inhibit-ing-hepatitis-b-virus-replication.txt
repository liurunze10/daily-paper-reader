ABSTRACT 37 

We previously reported that the oxidative stress sensor Kelch-like ECH-associated 38 

protein 1 (Keap1) recognizes hepatitis B virus (HBV) X protein (HBx) to activate the NF-39 

E2-related factor 2 (Nrf2) signaling pathway, thereby inhibiting HBV replication, and that 40 

HBx promotes K6-linked polyubiquitylation of Nrf2. However, the molecular mechanism 41 

remains unclear. Here, we investigated the role of HECT, UBA, and WWE domain-42 

containing E3 ubiquitin ligase 1 (HUWE1) in HBx-mediated K6-linked 43 

polyubiquitylation of Nrf2 and its impact on HBV replication. Cell-based ubiquitylation 44 

assays demonstrated that HUWE1 knockdown reduced HBx-mediated K6-linked 45 

polyubiquitylation of Nrf2, while overexpression of wild-type HUWE1, but not the 46 

catalytically inactive HUWE1(C4341A) mutant, enhanced it, indicating that HUWE1 E3 47 

ligase activity is required. Coimmunoprecipitation and proximity ligation assays 48 

demonstrated that HUWE1 interacts with HBx in the cytoplasm and binds Nrf2 only in 49 

the presence of HBx, suggesting that HBx bridges HUWE1 and Nrf2 into a ternary 50 

complex. Cycloheximide chase assays demonstrated that HUWE1 knockdown 51 

destabilized Nrf2 in HBx-expressing cells, supporting a role for HUWE1 in Nrf2 52 

stabilization via K6-linked polyubiquitylation. Furthermore, HUWE1 knockdown or 53 

treatment with the HUWE1 inhibitor BI8626 significantly increased HBV RNA and 54 

pgRNA levels in HBV-infected cells. Collectively, these results demonstrate that HUWE1 55 

promotes K6-linked polyubiquitylation and stabilization of Nrf2 in an HBx-dependent 56 

manner to inhibit HBV replication. (209 words) 57

INTRODUCTION 74 

Chronic infection with hepatitis B virus (HBV) remains a major global health 75 

challenge. The World Health Organization estimates that 254 million people have chronic 76 

HBV infection worldwide, and complications such as HBV-related cirrhosis and 77 

hepatocellular carcinoma were responsible for approximately 1.1 million deaths in 2022 78 

(1, 2). Current treatment include nucleos(t)ide analogues and interferon-α, but they rarely 79 

achieve a functional cure, underscoring the urgent need for novel therapeutic strategies 80 

(3). 81 

HBV is a hepatotropic virus composed of a 3.2-kb partially double-stranded relaxed 82 

circular DNA (rcDNA) genome (4). In infected hepatocytes, rcDNA is converted to 83 

covalently closed circular DNA (cccDNA), which serves as a template for the production 84 

of viral proteins, including HBV surface antigen (HBsAg), HBV core antigen (HBcAg), 85 

HBV e antigen (HBeAg), HBV polymerase (Pol), and HBV X protein (HBx) (4, 5). 86 

Among these viral proteins, HBx is a multifunctional regulatory protein that is essential 87 

for initiating cccDNA transcription and sustaining HBV replication, and has also been 88 

associated with hepatocarcinogenesis (5, 6). 89 

The Kelch-like ECH-associated protein 1 (Keap1)/NF-E2-related factor 2 (Nrf2) 90 

signaling pathway is a major defense mechanism against oxidative stress (7). Under basal 91 

conditions, Keap1 binds Nrf2 in the cytoplasm and promotes Nrf2 polyubiquitylation and 92 

degradation via K48-linked ubiquitin chains (8). However, under oxidative stress 93 

conditions, Keap1 becomes inactivated, leading to Nrf2 stabilization. Nrf2 subsequently 94 

translocates to the nucleus and activates transcription of antioxidant response genes (8– 95 

10). 96 

We previously reported that during HBV infection, Keap1 recognizes the HBx 97 

protein, resulting in Nrf2 stabilization and activation. Activated Nrf2 then translocates to 98 

the nucleus and inhibits HBV replication by suppressing HBV promoter activity or 99 

increasing the transcription of the antioxidant enzyme peroxiredoxin 1, which promotes 100 

degradation of HBV RNA (11, 12). Interestingly, we also found that HBx mediates 101 

polyubiquitylation of Nrf2 by switching the ubiquitin linkage from K48- to K6-linked 102 

polyubiquitylation. Moreover, knockdown of HECT, UBA, and WWE domain-containing 103 

E3 ubiquitin ligase 1 (HUWE1) reduced HBx-mediated K6-linked polyubiquitylation of 104

Nrf2 (11). While canonical K48-linked ubiquitylation mediated by the Keap1-Cullin3 105 

complex is known to regulate Nrf2 degradation (8, 10), the role of HUWE1 in modulating 106 

Nrf2 function remains largely unexplored. 107 

HUWE1 is a 482-kDa E3 ubiquitin ligase belonging to the HECT family ubiquitin 108 

ligase, also known as Mule (13), ARF-BP1 (14), HECTH9 (15), URE-B1 (16), LASU1, 109 

or E3Histone (17). It was first identified as an enzyme that promotes the degradation of 110 

several key regulators of stress response and survival pathways, including Mcl-1, p53, 111 

and c-Myc (13–15). In addition to the C-terminal HECT domain, HUWE1 contains 112 

multiple functional domains, including a ubiquitin-associated domain, a Bcl-2 homology 113 

region 3 domain, and a UBM1 domain. HUWE1 catalyzes K48-, K63-, and K6-linked 114 

ubiquitin conjugation (18). Importantly, HUWE1 has been reported to be a major E3 115 

ligase responsible for generating non-canonical K6-linked ubiquitin chains (19). Unlike 116 

the more widely recognized K48- or K63-linked chains, which are associated with protein 117 

degradation or signaling processes, K6-linked polyubiquitylation has recently attracted 118 

attention for its role in non-degradative functions such as protein stabilization (20, 21). 119 

HUWE1 possesses catalytic cysteine at C4341 in the HECT domain (22). 120 

Here, we show that HUWE1 forms a HUWE1/HBx/Nrf2 ternary complex in an HBx-121 

dependent manner, thereby promotes K6-linked polyubiquitylation and stabilization of 122 

Nrf2 and ultimately suppressing HBV replication. 123

DISCUSSION 276 

In this study, we demonstrated that the HECT-type E3 ubiquitin ligase HUWE1 mediates 277 

K6-linked polyubiquitylation of Nrf2 in an HBx-dependent manner, leading to Nrf2 278 

stabilization and nuclear translocation, and suppression of HBV replication. Our findings 279 

identify HUWE1 as a candidate host restriction factor against HBV and reveal a non-280 

canonical K6-ubiquitin switch as an antiviral mechanism (Fig. 8). 281 

K6-linked polyubiquitylation has been shown to serve non-degradative functions, in 282 

contrast to the canonical K48-linked chains that target proteins for proteasomal 283 

degradation (20, 21, 23, 24). K6-linked ubiquitin chains have been implicated in 284 

microtubule dynamics (25), mitophagy via Parkin (26), and innate immunity signaling 285 

(27–30). Our results add to this growing body of evidence by showing that HUWE1-286 

mediated K6-linked polyubiquitylation stabilizes Nrf2 during HBV infection, 287 

representing a previously unrecognized antiviral function of K6-linked ubiquitin chains. 288 

HUWE1 is a 482-kDa HECT domain E3 ubiquitin ligase known to catalyze K48-, 289

K63-, and K6-linked ubiquitin (18, 31). The HECT domain of HUWE1 forms a thioester 290 

intermediate with ubiquitin before transfer to a substrate lysine, and the C4341A 291 

substitution abolishes this catalytic activity (20). Our results clearly demonstrated that the 292 

HUWE1(C4341A) mutant failed to promote K6-linked polyubiquitylation of Nrf2, 293 

confirming that catalytic activity is indispensable. Consistent with this, the small-294 

molecule HUWE1 inhibitor BI8626 targets the ubiquitin-binding site of the HECT 295 

domain (32), recapitulated the effects of HUWE1 knockdown on both Nrf2 ubiquitylation 296 

and HBV replication, validating the pharmacological approach. 297 

A key finding of this study is that HUWE1 bound Nrf2 only in the presence of HBx, 298 

suggesting that HBx acts as an adapter that recruits HUWE1 to Nrf2 and remodels the 299 

ubiquitylation machinery. Under basal conditions, Nrf2 is degraded via Keap1-Cullin3-300 

mediated K48-linked polyubiquitylation (8, 10). We propose that during HBV infection, 301 

HBx competes with or re-routes this ubiquitin code by recruiting HUWE1, thereby 302 

switching K48 to K6 linkage, stabilizing Nrf2, and suppressing HBV promoter activity. 303 

This ubiquitylation switch represents a novel mechanism by which a viral protein co-opts 304 

a cellular E3 ligase to activate an antiviral pathway. 305 

HUWE1 has previously been reported to exhibit antiviral activity against other 306 

viruses. HUWE1 promotes ubiquitin-dependent degradation of MERS-CoV accessory 307 

protein ORF3 (33), negatively influences human immunodeficiency virus type 1308 

infectivity through interaction with viral Gag-Pol precursor protein (34), and acts as an 309 

antiviral E3 ligase against SARS-CoV-2 ORF9b (35). Our study expands the antiviral 310 

repertoire of HUWE1 to HBV and reveals a novel mechanism by which HUWE1 311 

stabilizes a host antiviral transcription factor Nrf2 through non-canonical ubiquitylation 312 

K6-linked polyubiquitylation. 313 

Several questions remain. First, the specific lysine residue(s) on Nrf2 that receive 314 

K6-linked ubiquitin chains are unknown. Further investigation is needed to identify the 315 

precise K6-linked polyubiquitylation site(s) on Nrf2 targeted by HUWE1. Second, the 316 

mechanism by which HBx facilitates the HUWE1−Nrf2 interaction−whether through 317 

direct protein-protein contacts or conformational rearrangements−warrants structural 318 

investigation. 319

Fig. 8. Proposed mechanism of HUWE1-mediated K6-linked polyubiquitylation of Nrf2 880 

promoting its stabilization and suppression of HBV replication. 881 

Under normal conditions (upper panel), HUWE1 does not associate with Nrf2. Keap1 bridges 882 

Nrf2 to the Cullin3 E3 ligase complex, leading to K48-linked polyubiquitylation and 883 

proteasomal degradation of Nrf2. Upon HBV infection (lower panel), HBx recruits HUWE1 to 884 

form a ternary HUWE1/HBx/Nrf2 complex in the cytoplasm. HUWE1 then adds K6-linked 885 

polyubiquitylation chains to Nrf2, switching the ubiquitin code from K48 to K6 linkage. K6-886 

linked polyubiquitylation stabilizes Nrf2, promotes its nuclear translocation, and leads 887 

transcriptional activation of antioxidant response element (ARE)-driven genes. In the nucleus, 888 

Nrf2 suppresses HBV core, PreS1, and PreS2/S promoter activities, thereby suppressing HBV 889 

replication. Meanwhile, Nrf2 also suppresses HBV replication through its target gene Prdx1, 890 

which facilitates HBV RNA degradation, as we reported previously (12). 891
