24 Abstract 

25 Intestinal epithelial cells are central players in mucosal barrier integrity and host-microbe interactions. Genetic 

26 studies have revealed that epithelial dysfunction is a key contributor to the pathogenesis of inflammatory bowel 

27 disease. Non-SMC condensin II complex subunit D3 (NCAPD3) is essential for chromatin organization and 

28 stability. NCAPD3 also promotes antimicrobial defense and autophagy responses in vitro . NCAPD3 expression 

29 is decreased in intestinal epithelial cells from patients with ulcerative colitis; however, it is not known whether 

30 loss of NCAPD3 expression drives intestinal barrier dysfunction or is a result of disease-associated inflammation. 

31 To investigate this relationship in vivo , a tissue-specific approach was required, as global constitutive knockout 

32 of NCAPD3 is embryonic lethal. Therefore, a transgenic mouse line with doxycycline-inducible expression of a 

33 short hairpin RNA targeting NCAPD3 restricted to villin-expressing cells was generated (NCAPD3KD mice) to 

34 enable the study of NCAPD3 function in the intestinal epithelium. Treatment of NCAPD3KD mice with 9-tert-

35 butyl doxycycline resulted in ~75% reduction of NCAPD3 protein in EpCAM ⁺ intestinal cells. Short-term epithelial 

36 NCAPD3 knockdown did not induce spontaneous colitis but was associated with increased serum amyloid A and 

37 a trend towards increased intestinal permeability. Upon dextran sodium sulfate or Salmonella enterica serovar 

38 Typhimurium ΔAroA challenge, NCAPD3KD mice exhibited exacerbated weight loss, higher disease activity, 

39 increased histopathological damage, abnormal colonic cytokines and chemokines, and significantly increased 

40 intestinal permeability. These results indicate that NCAPD3 expression in the intestinal epithelium is required for 

41 optimal barrier maintenance and antimicrobial defense under chemical or microbial stress. These findings 

42 support prior in vitro observations and solidify NCAPD3 as a regulator of intestinal epithelial barrier function and 

43 mucosal host defense.

58 59 Introduction 

60 The intestinal epithelium is a vital single-cell barrier between the host and the external environment, consisting 

61 of a diverse range of specialized intestinal epithelial cells (IECs) (1). This dynamic interface allows the absorption 

62 of nutrients and fluids, provides innate immune defenses, and helps maintain a homeostatic balance with the gut 

63 microbiota (2). The epithelium provides a physical barrier, with intercellular tight junctions responsible for 

64 maintaining a seal between adjacent cells (3). Additionally, IECs convey signals to mucosal immune cells and 

65 help coordinate differential immune responses to commensal and pathogenic bacteria. Defects in epithelial 

66 barrier integrity are a hallmark of inflammatory bowel diseases (IBD), such as ulcerative colitis (UC) and Crohn’s 

67 disease (CD), and render the host susceptible to enteric infections (2). 

68 In the context of IBD, disruption and dysregulation of IEC function are key drivers of chronic intestinal 

69 inflammation (4). Defects in epithelial barrier integrity, including altered tight junction architecture, impaired 

70 mucus production, and reduced antimicrobial peptide secretion, lead to increased intestinal permeability and 

71 inappropriate exposure of the mucosal immune system to luminal microbes and antigens. Increased exposure 

72 of the host tissues to microbial products, from a so-called “leaky gut”, leads to chronic inflammation. Beyond 

73 barrier failure, IECs in IBD display aberrant innate immune signaling, stress responses, and altered interactions 

74 with the microbiota, all of which contribute to sustained inflammatory responses characteristic of IBD. 

75 Importantly, IBD has a clear genetic component, with genome-wide association studies identifying over 250 risk 

76 loci, many of which are directly linked to epithelial cell function (5). Collectively, these findings highlight IECs as

79 Non-SMC condensin II complex subunit D3 (NCAPD3) is a core component of the condensin II complex, 

80 which promotes chromatin condensation during mitotic initiation (6) and maintains genomic stability (7). Beyond 

81 its canonical nuclear functions, NCAPD3 has been implicated in retrotransposon silencing (8), mitochondrial 

82 redox (9), and antimicrobial defenses (10, 11). Consequently, the dysregulation of NCAPD3 has been associated 

83 with multiple human pathologies. Loss-of-function mutations drive microcephaly (12), while overexpression has 

84 been associated with cancer progression (13-19). Critically, reduced NCAPD3 expression has been observed 

85 in intestinal epithelial cells isolated from UC patients (11). However, despite evidence from cell-based and ex 

86 vivo analyses of intestinal epithelial cells, the role of NCAPD3 in maintaining intestinal epithelial homeostasis in 

87 vivo remains uncharacterized. Specifically, it is unclear how loss or dysregulation of NCAPD3 affects epithelial 

88 integrity or susceptibility to intestinal inflammation within an organismal context. 

89 To directly define the role of NCAPD3 in the intestinal epithelium in vivo , we created an inducible, 

90 intestinal epithelial cell-specific NCAPD3 knockdown mouse model. Using this model, we examined whether 

91 epithelial NCAPD3 is necessary to maintain intestinal homeostasis or to protect the intestinal barrier during 

92 inflammatory or infectious challenges. Specifically, we assessed the effects of NCAPD3 depletion on intestinal 

93 permeability, susceptibility to chemically induced epithelial injury, and host defense against enteric bacterial 

94 infection. Our studies provide evidence that NCAPD3 expression in the intestinal epithelium is essential for 

95 optimal barrier integrity and antimicrobial defense under stress from chemical or microbial challenges. These 

96 findings underscore the important role of NCAPD3 in maintaining intestinal health.

136 Short term NCAPD3 knockdown in the intestinal epithelium is not sufficient to induce spontaneous 

137 colitis. To assess the direct impact of reduced NCAPD3 expression in intestinal epithelial cells in vivo ,

138 NCAPD3KD mice were treated daily with 9-TB-Dox or saline for 10 days. Although 9-TB-Dox-treated mice tended 

139 to lose more weight than control animals, no significant differences in weight loss, gross colonic appearance, or 

140 colon length were observed ( Fig. 2A & 2B ). Colonic tissue histopathology evaluation using a standardized colitis 

141 scoring system did not identify signs of intestinal inflammation in any animal ( Fig. 2C ). Intestinal permeability 

142 was assessed using a flagellin bioassay on endpoint plasma samples and showed a trend of increased 

143 permeability in NCAPD3KD mice treated with 9-TB-Dox ( Fig. 2D ). In addition, 9-TB-Dox treated NCAPD3KD 

144 mice had 2.1 fold increased levels of the systemic acute phase marker of inflammation, SAA relative to control 

145 mice ( Fig. 2E ). Overall, these data indicate that short term NCAPD3 knockdown in the intestinal epithelium is 

146 not sufficient to induce spontaneous colitis but appears to mildly elevate basal inflammation and gut 

147 permeability.

188 189 Discussion 

190 In this study, we generated and validated a novel inducible, intestinal epithelial cell-specific NCAPD3 knockdown 

191 mouse model and used it to investigate the in vivo role of NCAPD3 in intestinal barrier homeostasis. Using a 

192 villin-rtTA driven shRNA system targeted to the Col1a1 safe harbor locus, we achieved robust epithelial-specific 

193 reduction of NCAPD3 following induction with the non-antimicrobial tetracycline analogue 9-TB-Dox. This in vivo 

194 mammalian model enables mechanistic investigation of the observation that UC patients have reduced NCAPD3 

195 expression specifically in intestinal epithelial cells (11). Our data support the interpretation that this epithelial 

196 NCAPD3 reduction is not a secondary consequence of inflammation but may contribute to epithelial dysfunction 

197 and disease pathogenesis. 

198 Despite efficient (~75%) epithelial knockdown over 10 days, NCAPD3KD mice did not develop 

199 spontaneous colitis. However, the trend toward increased intestinal permeability, elevated circulating flagellin 

200 levels, and significantly increased SAA suggest that NCAPD3 loss subtly compromises epithelial barrier integrity, 

201 even in the absence of overt histological inflammation. Increased intestinal permeability is increasingly 

202 recognized as an early event in IBD, detectable even in quiescent disease and in individuals prior to clinical 

203 onset (23-25). The observation that NCAPD3KD mice exhibit low-level permeability and systemic inflammation 

204 without visible tissue injury supports the concept that NCAPD3 loss can contribute to epithelial barrier weakening 

205 and predispose one to inflammatory conditions. While this study examined only short-term knockdown, chronic 

206 NCAPD3 deficiency may exacerbate this basal inflammatory tone and potentially lead to spontaneous disease. 

207 Extended knockdown studies will be required to determine whether long-term NCAPD3 reduction alone is 

208 sufficient to break the intestinal barrier and drive intestinal pathology. 

209 The increase in SAA in NCAPD3KD mice, even at baseline, is notable. SAA is not merely a marker of 

210 inflammation but an active participant in inflammatory processes, capable of inducing cytokine production, 

211 recruiting immune cells, and contributing to tissue repair responses (26). In this context, elevated SAA in the

216 When challenged with DSS or an enteric Salmonella infection, NCAPD3KD mice exhibited significantly 

217 heightened disease activity, epithelial damage, inflammatory cytokine production, and intestinal permeability 

218 compared to controls. The amplification of permeability and inflammation in these settings aligns with the widely 

219 proposed “two-hit hypothesis” for IBD pathogenesis, where a genetically predisposed individual may have 

220 epithelial vulnerability and mild barrier dysfunction that, when coupled with environmental triggers, can lead to 

221 an uncontrolled inflammatory state (27). In this context, reduced NCAPD3 expression may represent a 

222 susceptibility factor that does not independently cause disease but sensitizes the host to secondary insults. 

223 During DSS-induced injury, NCAPD3 deficiency was associated with significantly increased epithelial 

224 damage and elevation in the proinflammatory mediators MIP1α/CCL3, MIP2/CXCL2, IL-9, IL-15, and IL-17. IL-

225 17 and IL-15 are well-established contributors to mucosal inflammation in both DSS colitis and human IBD, 

226 promoting leukocyte recruitment and sustaining immune activation (28). Notably, MIP1α/CCL3 and MIP2/CXCL2 

227 are key chemokines in IBD that promote the recruitment of macrophages and neutrophils, respectively, and their 

228 upregulation correlates with disease severity (29). Whilst it remains unclear whether these changes reflect direct 

229 transcriptional dysregulation resulting from NCAPD3 loss or are secondary to increased epithelial damage and 

230 permeability, this data suggests that NCAPD3 plays a role in restraining inflammatory amplification following 

231 barrier disruption. Further studies are required to determine whether this occurs through direct chromatin-

232 mediated regulation of inflammatory gene programs or indirectly because of worsened epithelial injury. 

233 Consistent with our prior in vitro and Drosophila studies, epithelial NCAPD3 knockdown compromised 

234 host defense during Salmonella infection. Although tissue bacterial burdens were comparable, NCAPD3KD mice 

235 exhibited increased diarrheal severity and worsened histopathology. This was accompanied by reduced 

236 expression of the antimicrobial peptides Reg3β and DEFB14. Reg3β is strongly induced during a Salmonella 

237 enteric infection and contributes to maintaining a distance between luminal bacteria and the epithelial surface 

238 (30), while β-defensins directly limit bacterial growth and shape microbiota composition (31). Deficits in

240 even in the presence of pro-inflammatory signaling that typically upregulates their expression (32). As prior 

241 enteric infections are known to increase IBD risk in genetically predisposed individuals (33), heightened tissue 

242 damage during infection in the setting of reduced NCAPD3 may represent another mechanism linking epithelial 

243 chromatin dysregulation to chronic inflammatory susceptibility. 

244 Recent advances have highlighted broader roles for chromatin regulators in controlling transcriptional 

245 programs governing epithelial differentiation and innate immunity (34). For example, the chromatin architectural 

246 protein CTCF, together with cohesin, organizes higher-order chromatin looping at inflammatory cytokine loci and 

247 directly regulates transcriptional responses (35, 36). Similarly, SATB1 acts as a genome organizer in immune 

248 cells, controlling the expression of cytokines and other immune genes by regulating long-range chromatin 

249 interactions (37, 38). NCAPD3 also contributes to genomic stability by repressing retrotransposons and limiting 

250 apoptosis (8, 39). Loss of this function can increase DNA damage and cell stress, potentially weakening epithelial 

251 turnover and barrier integrity. These examples demonstrate how disruption of chromatin-organizing systems can 

252 reprogram inflammatory, stress-response, and differentiation pathways, indicating mechanisms by which 

253 dampened levels of NCAPD3 could reshape epithelial function through skewing expression of cytokines, 

254 antimicrobial peptides, or junctional proteins. 

255 In summary, our study establishes NCAPD3 as an epithelial regulator of the mammalian intestine that 

256 enhances epithelial barrier resilience and antimicrobial defense during stress. Although dispensable for short-

257 term basal homeostasis, NCAPD3 function becomes critical during epithelial injury and enteric infection. 

258 Reduced NCAPD3 expression may therefore lower the threshold for inflammatory disease by weakening barrier 

259 integrity, amplifying inflammatory cascades, and impairing antimicrobial defenses. These findings position 

260 NCAPD3 as a potential modulator of IBD susceptibility and highlight chromatin organization as an important, 

261 previously underappreciated layer of intestinal epithelial regulation.
