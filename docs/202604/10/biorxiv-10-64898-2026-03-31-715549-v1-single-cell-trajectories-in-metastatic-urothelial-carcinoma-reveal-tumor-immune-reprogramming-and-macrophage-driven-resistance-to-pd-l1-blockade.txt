Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI) improve d outcomes in metastatic urothelial carcinoma (mUC), but 40 

primary and acquired resistance remain poorly understood. We performed single -nuclei RNA 41 

sequencing on sequential metastatic biopsies from ICI -treated mUC patients. Tumor cells showed 42 

transcriptomic heterogeneity within individual lesions , basal cells being associated with increas ed43 

immune infiltration and response. Myeloid and lymphoid compartments exhibited features of immune 44 

dysfunction in non -responders . Longitudinal analyses revea led convergent adaptive resistance 45 

mechanisms , dominated by polarization toward pro -tumoral macrophage states, but also including 46 

downregulation of the antigen presentation machinery in tumor cells , increased checkpoint expression 47 

with loss of cytotoxicity in T cells. Individual trajectories point to distinct evolutionary routes under ICI 48 

pressure. Across pivotal ICI trials, bulk expression of the M2 -like macrophage marker HES1 predicted 49 

ICI resistance . Our study provides the first single -cell longitudinal atlas of ICI -treated mUC , revealing 50 

macrophage reprogramming as a dominant driver of resistance, establishing a framework for 51 

individualized immunotherapy strategies. 52

# Introduction 54 

Metastatic urothelial carcinoma (mUC) remains a lethal disease despi te rec ent therapeutic advances 1.55 

Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI s) targeting PD -L(1) have improved survival across treatment 56 

settings, yet only a minority of patients derive durable benefit 2–6. Most individuals experience primary 57 

resistance, an d among init ial responders, acquired resistance is almost universal. While PD -L1 58 

immunohistochemistry and common genomic alterations offer limited predictive value in metastatic 59 

setting, the biological determinants that shape sensitivity or resistance to I CIs in mUC remain largely 60 

undefined. 61 

Several studies in localized muscle -invasive bladder cancer (MIBC) have suggested the importance of 62 

pre -existing immune contexture, including T and B cell infiltration, tertiary lymphoid structures and 63 

inflammatory tran scriptional programs 7–13 . However, these analyses mostly relied on bulk 64 

transcriptomics or immunohistochemistry from primary tumors, which may not reflect the biology of 65 

metastatic lesions. Bulk -based analyses also obscure the cellular heterogeneity of both tumor and 66 

immune compartments, preventing refined characterization of antitumor versus protumoral states. 67 

Single -cell technologies have begun to r eveal the complexity of urothel ial carcinoma, including luminal -68 

basal p lasticity and diverse myeloid cell states, but these studies remains cross -sectional and do not 69 

capture the dynamic rewiring induced by ICIs. 70 

Understa nd ing how the tumor and its microenvironment evolve over time during immunotherap y is 71 

critical to unravel resistance mechanisms. In solid tumors, emerging evidence indicates that ICI 72 

resistance may arise through multiple non -mutually -exclusive processes, including antigen 73 

presentation defects (HLA or B2 -microglobulin loss), impaired IF N signaling, T cell exhaustion and 74 

expansion of immunosuppressive myeloid populations. However, whether these mechanisms operate 75 

in metastatic urothelial carcinoma, how they evolve under selective pressure, and how they combine 76 

within individual patients r emain unknown. No study to date has mapped, at the single -cell level the 77 

longitudinal trajectories of tumor, lymphoid and myeloid compartment s in mUC during PD -L(1) 78 

blockad e. 79 

To ad dress this gap, we conducted an in -depth, single -cell analysis of metastat ic tumor biopsies in the 80 

MATCH -R biological trial (NCT02517892) which prospectively collected sequential metastatic samples 81 

at baseline, during therapy and at progression in patients treated with anti -PD -(L)1 monotherapy .82 

Using single -nuclei RNA sequencing s supported by whole -exome sequencing and bulk transcriptomics, 83 

we generated a dataset of more than 220,000 high -quality cells mapped across 55 biopsies. This design 84 

provides a unique opportunity to dissect both pre -treatment determinants of response and a daptive 85 

resistance mechanisms emerging under immune pressure. 86

# Results 102 

Single -nuclei profiling of metastatic urothelial carcinoma unveils extensive t umor and 103 

microenvironmental heterogeneity 104 

A total of 32 patients with metastatic urothelial carcinoma were included in the MATCH -R trial and 105 

treated with single -agent PD -1 or PD -L1 inhibitor in the metastatic setting. Median age was 71 (IQR 61 -106 

77), and mos t (66%) had bladder cancer as a primary tumor. Patients were treated either in the first 107 

line metastatic setting (28%), second -line (50%), or third -line and more (22%). Most common 108 

metastatic sites at immune checkpoint inhibitor initiation were lymph nodes (65%), lung (38%), bone 109 

(31%), liver (28%), peritoneal or retroperitoneal soft tissue (28%) ( Supplementary Table S1 ). Overall, 110 

seven (22%) patients achieved objective response (OR), which translated to prolonged benefit to ICI: 111 

with a median follow -up of 26.4 months (95% confidence interval [CI] 16.0 -35.4), median progression -112 

free survival was 24.7 months (95%CI 17.8 -31.0) in patients who achieved OR versus 1.4 months (95%CI 113 

1.0 -1.6) in those who did not, and median overall survival not reached versus 4.2 months (95%CI 2.5 -114 

6.2) ( Figure 1a ). 115 

To allow for longitudinal assessment of the tumor and its microenvironment on therapy, we performed 116 

single -nuclei RNA sequencing in matched sequential biopsies at baseline, 3 months after ICI initiation 117 

(for patients who achieved disease control), and at disease progression. Biopsies at baseline were 118 

performed in 31 patients (97%), including lung (23%), lymph node (19%), soft tissue (19%), liver (13%) 119 

or bone (9%) metastases. Seventeen patients (53%) had a biopsy at disea se progression, while five 120 

(16%) had an interval biopsy on -therapy. Overall, 55 samples were collected. After quality controls 121 

(Supplementary Figure S1 ), we obtained single -nuclei transcriptomes from 226,627 cells, with a 122 

median number of 4,573 cells per s ample, and a median gene coverage of 2,549 ( Figure 1b -c). 123 

Main immune cell populations were annotated based on the expression of marker genes (see 124 

Methods), and tumor cells based on gene expression and virtual copy number profiles ( Figure 1d, 125 

Supplementar y Figure S2 -3). Tumor cells were the most abundant cell type (mean 59.2% across 126 

samples, range 0.2% -98.7%), followed by fibroblasts (mean 11.8%, range 0.1% -68.7%), T cells (mean 127 

8.0%, range 0% -65.6%), macrophages (mean 7.9%, range 0.2% -34.9%), endothelial cells (mean 6.3%, 128 

range 0.1% -22.8%) and B cells (mean 2.9%, range 0% -37.7%). Though, a high heterogeneity in immune 129 

cell abundance and tumor microenvironment composition was seen across samples and timepoints 130 

(Figure 1e -f). In particular, biopsies sampled in lymph nodes displayed higher B cell abundance 131 

compared to other locations. We also observed an increased abundance of T cells in on -therapy 132 

samples ( Figure 1f ). 133

Intratumoral coexistence of basal and luminal programs shapes immune infiltration and ICI 135 

sensitivity 136 

To explore tumor cell differentiation states, we assessed molecular signatures established from bulk 137 

RNA sequencing data 8,14 in our sing le -nuclei dataset. We used a pseudobulk approach to classify 138 

samples into the consensus MIBC subgroups based on the aggregated single -nuclei transcriptome of 139 

their tumor cells. We obtained a high concordance between classifications derived from single -nucl ei 140 

and matched bulk RNA -seq data ( Supplementary Figure S4 ). Yet, individual samples displayed a high 141 

heterogeneity regarding tumor cell states, with the coexistence of cells with basal and luminal 142 

differentiation ( Figure 2a,b, Supplementary Figure S5 ). Pri ncipal component analysis revealed two 143 

main components significantly associated with the basal, luminal and neuroendocrine differentiation 144 

signatures 14 (Figure 2a ). Principal component 1 (PC1) was associated with neuroendocrine 145 

diff erentiation (cor = 0.87, P < 2.2E –16), whereas PC2 was positively correlated with the luminal 146 

signature and anticorrelated with the basal signature (cor = -0.37, P < 2.2E –16), indicating a continuum 147 

between luminal and basal phenotypes. 148 

We then explored wh ether tumor cell states were associated with specific genomic alterations. We 149 

generated whole exome sequencing (WES) data in 32 samples with matched single -cell RNA -seq data. 150 

Sixteen potential driver genes were mutated in ≥3 cases including TP53 (N=26), KMT2D (N=18), TSC1 ,151 

ERBB3 (N=6 each), KDM6A (N=5), ARID1A , ERBB2 , FGFR3 , HRAS , RB1 (N=3 each) ( Supplementary 152 

Figure S6a ). We assessed the ability to detect driver alterations in snRNA -seq data, and reached a 74% 153 

detection rate for single -nucleotide variant s and 83% for oncogene amplifications ( Supplementary 154 

Figure S6a,b ). Among putative driver mutations, KDM6A was the only one associated with increased 155 

PC2 mean ( P = 0.004) and lower basal cell content (pval = 0.01, Supplementary Figure S6c ). At the 156 

intra -tu moral level, we found two genes, ELF3 (pval = 1E -05) and KDM6A (pval = 0.01), which mutations 157 

were associated with higher cell -wise PC2 values ( Supplementary Figure S6d ). 158 

The relationship between the tumor microenvironment composition and tumor cell state s is displayed 159 

in Figure 2c . Tumors classified as basal/squamous using the pseudobulk classification had increased 160 

immune infiltration ( Figure 2c -d cor=0.46, pval=4E -04), but did not display a significantly different 161 

microenvironment composition regarding the main immune cell populations. Despite evidence of 162 

higher immune infiltration, the pseudobulk consensus MIBC subgroups did not predict therapy 163 

outcome; however, the proportion of basal tumor cells at baseline appeared a significant predictor of 164 

response to ICI (pval = 0.01, Figure 2e ). 165

Tumor cells undergo convergent adaptive resistance via loss of antigen presentation and IFN –167 

JAK/STAT signaling 168 

To understand adaptive mechanisms of resistance to ICI related to tumor cells, we assessed gene 169 

expression ch anges between baseline and disease progression. Although no gene was significantly 170 

deregulated between pre - and post -treatment in the whole cohort after multiple testing correction, 171 

several genes previously associated with immunotherapy response ( Supplemen tary Table S2 ) were 172 

significantly down -regulated in post -treatment in individual patients with longitudinal data. We found 173 

that genes involved in antigen presentation, including HLA -A, HLA -B, HLA -C, or B2M were significantly 174 

downregulated in tumor cells at progression in 10 /16 patients ( Figure 2f ). Alterations in the JAK/STAT 175 

signaling pathway also appeared prominent, with a significant repression of IFNGR1 , JAK1 or STAT1 176 

observed in 11 /16 patients with matched samples. In patients who also had tissue sampl ing during ICI 177 

in the context of controlled disease, we observed transient upregulation of HLA -A/B/C , B2M , IFNGR1 ,178 

and STAT1 (Figure 2f ), suggestive of an immune -responsive contexture. 179

CD4/CD8 T -cell balance and exhaustion states define response and resistance trajectories 181 

T lymphocytes represented the predominant immune component of the tumor microenvironment in 182 

our cohort, with a total of 16,453 cells across the 55 samples ( mean = 300 per sample). Following 183 

Harmony integration, we identified seven m ajor infiltrating T cell subpopulations: CD4 regulatory cells, 184 

CD4 follicular helper cells (naïve, effector, exhausted), and CD8 cytotoxic cells (naïve, effector, 185 

exhausted) ( Figure 3a ). 186 

The abundance of overall CD8 infiltration was associated with high C D8 effector and CD8 exhausted 187 

cell infiltration; both enriched in non -responders ( Figure 3b ). A higher proportion of CD8 T cells among 188 

total T cells was associated with poor treatment response (pval = 0.0032, Figure 3c ), and a lower 189 

CD4/CD8 ratio was assoc iated with shorter progression -free survival (pval = 0.024) (Figure 3d ). At the 190 

single -gene level, elevated expression of PDCD1 and GZMA was linked to shorter progression -free 191 

survival ( Figure 3e ), suggesting that the CD8 infiltrate in poor responders is e nriched for terminally 192 

differentiated, exhausted T cells. In contrast, higher overall CD4 T cell infiltration was associated with 193 

improved clinical outcomes. 194 

Longitudinal analysis revealed heterogeneous changes in the T cell transcriptome during therapy. F ive 195 

patients (31%) with sequential T cell single -nuclei data displayed upregulation of at least one immune 196 

checkpoint at progression, including PDCD1 , CTLA4 , HAVCR2 , LAG3 and TIGIT (Figure 3f ). 197

A total of 12,208 myeloid cells were identified across the 55 samples ( mean = 222 per sample). 201 

Following Harmony integration, we used the MoMac -VERSE data set comprising a compendium of 202 

myeloid cells from multiple single -cel l datasets to annotate five macrophage and two monocyte 203 

subsets within our clusters of myeloid cells ( Figure 4a )15 .204 

Primary resistance to ICI was associated with higher abundance of pro -tumoral M2 macrophages ( P =205 

0.016, GLMM), and in particular HES1 macrophages ( P = 0.0010, GLMM, Figure 4b ). A high abundance 206 

of HES1 macrophages also predicted shorter PFS ( P = 0.016, log -rank test, Figure 4c ). Accordingly, 207 

immunosuppressive genes involved in the M2 polarization like CD163 or MS4A4A were upregulated in 208 

the macrophages of patients who did not respond to ICI ( Figure 4d ). 209 

In addition, most patients (81%) displayed evidence of a shift towards M2 -like macrophage polarization 210 

at progression, consisting in increased HES1 macrophage abundance ( Figure 5a ), upregulation of M2 -211 

genes such as CD163 and VSIG4 , and downregulatio n of M1 genes such as CLEC5A and HLA -DRA (Figure 212 

5b). This remodeling is illustrated by the UMAPs in Figure 5 c showing the trajectory of myeloid cells 213 

between pre - and post -treatment samples in two patients. Altogether, these data indicated that pro -214 

tumora l macrophages are both implicated in the initial ICI response and in the development of 215 

resistance upon treatment. 216 

Bulk HES1 expression stratifies benefit from PD -L1 blockade across independent clinical trials 217 

Considering that HES1 macrophages were associa ted with primary and secondary resistance to ICI in 218 

our cohort, we evaluated bulk expression of HES1 across publicly available datasets from clinical trials 219 

in patients with urothelial cancers, and assessed its impact on outcomes to ICI. Across four trials of 220 

atezolizumab in patients with localised or metastatic urothelial cancers, HES1 expression was 221 

significantly increased across stages (metastatic vs. localised) and line of therapy (sec ond -line vs. first -222 

line metastatic setting) (Supplementary Figure S7 ). Pooled overall survival analysis of phase 3 trials 223 

evaluating atezolizumab vs. chemotherapy in metastatic urothelial carcinoma showed that only 224 

patients with low HES1 expression derived benefit to atezolizumab versus chemotherapy with an 225 

overall survival rate of 48.5% vs. 38.4% at 12 months, and 34.4% vs. 20.8 % at 24 months, respectively 226 

(HR 0.72 , 95% conf idence interval [CI] 0.54 -0.96 ). Conversely, patients with high expression of HES1 227 

did not derive benefit to at ez olizumab vs. chemotherapy (HR 0.97 , 95% CI 0.84 - 1.12 ) (Figure 5d ).228

# Discussion 243 

Using longitudinal single -nuclei sequencing of metastatic biopsies, this study delineates dynamic 244 

tumor -immune trajectories underlying response and resistance to PD -(L)1 blockade in metastatic 245 

urothelial carcinoma. By resolving cell -state -specific programs across tumor, lymphoid, and myeloid 246 

compartments, we identify macrophage -driven tumo r-immune reprogramming as a dominant and 247 

convergent mechanism of both primary and acquired resistance to immune checkpoint inhibition. 248 

A major strength of this work lies in its focus on metastatic lesions sample d longitudinally, a setting in 249 

which resistance to immune checkpoint i nhibitors ultimately emerges and therapeutic decisions are 250 

made. While previous studies have largely relied on bulk transcriptomics or primary tumor samples, 251 

our approach captures the evolving cellular ecosystems of metastatic disease at single -cell resolu tion, 252 

revealing resistance mechanisms that are both dynamic and patient specific. 253 

First, we observed that tumor cell plasticity modulates , but does not dominate, resistance trajectories. 254 

At the tumor cell level, we found extensive intratumoral heterogeneit y, with basal and luminal 255 

differentiation states coexisting within individual metastatic lesions. This plasticity challenge s the 256 

predictive value of molecular subtyping derived from bulk analyses and suggests that tumor cell state 257 

composition, rather than subtype assignment, more accurately reflects immunotherapy sensitivity. 258 

Indeed, the impact of tumor transcriptome on outcomes for patients treated with ICI has been 259 

controversial in studies using bulk transcriptome datasets. In localized trials evaluating PD -(L)1 260 

inhibition as neoadjuvant therapy, the PURE01 trial showed that patients with basal phenotypes had 261 

highest response rates, a finding that was not confirmed in the ABACUS trial 9. In the metastatic setting, 262 

the atlas published by Loriot et al. showed that bulk transcriptome was not indicative of immune 263 

infiltration 16 . Our study showed that a higher proportion of basal -like tumor ce lls was associated with 264 

increased immune infiltration and impro ved response to PD -(L)1 8,13 . Longitudinal analyses further 265 

reveal adaptive tumor -intrinsic mechanisms at progression, includ ing downregulation of antigen 266 

presentation machinery and interferon signaling pathways. These changes likely contribute to immune 267 

escape in a subset of patients, echoing resistance mechanisms described across multiple cancer types. 268 

However, the heterogenei ty and incomplete penetrance of these alterations suggest tha t tumor -269 

intrinsic reprogramming alone is not enough to explain resistance in most patients. 270 

Lymphocyte infiltration has been usually positively associated with outcomes, with B and T cell lineages 271 

repeatedly associated with improved response to ICI 7,9,10 . Though, it has been shown that many 272 

patients with hig h lymphocyte infiltration may not derive benefit from ICI. In the study from Robertson 273 

et al. based on localized tumors treated with ICI, patients who were non -responders despite high 274 

lymphocyte infiltration harbored high checkpoint expression 9. In the lymphoid compartment, we 275

Our findings have important implications for biomarker development. Bulk transcriptomic signatures 317 

largely reflect overall immune abundance and fail to discriminate protu moral from antitumoral 318 

immune states, limiting their predictive utility. In contrast, single -cell resolved macrophages states, 319 

such as HES1 expressing populations capture functionally relevant resistance programs and may 320 

inform patient stratification for i mmunotherapy. Therapeutically, our results provide a strong rationale 321 

for targeting macrophage -driven immunosuppression in combination with PD -(L)1 inhibitors. 322 

Strategies aimed at reprogramming tumor -associated macrophages or disrupting protumoral myeloid 323 

signaling pathways could restore immune sensitivity in both primary and acquired resistance settings. 324 

This study has limitations inherent to its design. The cohort size is modest, reflecting the technical and 325 

logistical challenges of longitudinal metastati c biopsies and sampling of small core biopsies may 326 

underrepresent spatial immune structures such as tertiary lymphoid structures. Limited tumor 327 

material availability could also explain the low number of dendritic cells in our samples, although those 328 

have b een previously reported to be present in immune -responsive tumor subtypes 10 . Assessment of 329 

the greater stromal architecture may also be hampered by the small sampling size. This could also 330 

explain why cancer associated fibroblasts did not emerge as a potential predictor of outcomes in our 331 

study, while those have been previously associated with stromal, immune -resistant tumors 10,19 .332 

Nevertheless, the depth of longitudinal sampling and the consistency of macrophage -driven resistance 333 

across patients and external datasets mitigate these limitations. Future studies integrating spatial 334 

transcri ptomics, functional perturbation models and prospective clinical validation will be required to 335 

fully elucidate the regulatory network governing macrophage reprogramming and to translate these 336 

insights inti therapeutic appro aches. 337 

In conclusion, longitudin al single -cell profiling of metastatic urothelial carcinoma reveals that 338 

resistance to PD -(L)1 bl ockade converges on macrophage driven tumor -immune reprogramming. 339 

While tumor cell plasticity and T cell exhaustion contribute to resistance in selected contexts, 340 

protumoral macrophage polarization emerges as a dominant and recurrent mechanism underlying 341 

both primary and acquired immune checkpoint inhibitor failure. These data support the development 342 

of macrophage -targeted combination strategies in metastatic urothelial carcinoma. 343

# Material and methods 344 

Single -nuclei RNA sequencing 345 

Cryopreserved cells were thawed and nucleus permeabiliz ation was performed following 346 

recommendations from 10X Genomics. Single ‐nucleus 5’ gene expression libraries were generated 347 

using 10X Genomics single cell gene expression 5’ v1.1 kit, following the protocol provided by the 348 

manufacturer. A total of 10,000 n uclei per sample were loaded in each lane of the Chromium Single 349 

Cell Controller, unless fewer nuclei were recovered after dissociation, in which case they were all 350 

loaded. Libraries were sequenced on a NovaSeq 6000 to a target depth of 150 million reads ( Paired -351 

end, 28bp Read 1, 90bp Read 2), and topped -up accordingly in order to reach sequencing saturation 352 

for each library. 353

Single -nuclei RNA -seq processing 355 

We used a custom workflow to process raw FASTQ files. The UMI counts matrices were generated 356 

using STAR 20 align ment and count (STARsolo) with the human genome reference (GrCh38/hg38) and 357 

10x barcode whitelists. We used CellBender 21 to remove counts from ambient RNA molecules and 358 

random barcode swapping with recommended settings. We used DoubletCollection 22 to detect 359 

doublet cells with doubletCells , cxds, bcds , hybrid, scDblFinder, Scrublet, DoubletDetection and 360 

DoubletFinder algorithms. Each cell detected as doublet by ≥3 methods was removed. We retained 361 

only high -quality cells (>500 genes detected and <10% o f mitochondrial RNA reads). The samples were 362 

aggregated into one matrix and the cohort matrix was normalized using monocle3 23 normalization 363 

method by size factor. We then performed dimensionality reduction using principal component 364 

analysis (PCA) (producing 20 principal compon ents) and UMAP ( Uniform Manifold Approximation and 365 

Projection) on the first 20 principal components. Last, we applied PhenoGraph clustering (with Leiden 366 

algorithm) with a resolution of 0.3 on the 20 PCA components. 367

Annotation of broad cell types 369 

We annota ted cells by projecting the expression of established marker genes on the PhenoGraph 370 

clusters obtained during the previous step. Each cluster was assigned to the cell type with highest 371 

marker expression, and doublets were removed. This annotation step enab led to identify potential 372 

bladder tumor cells as well as cells from the tumor microenvironment (TME: fibroblasts, T cells, 373 

macrophages, endothelial cells, B cells, neutrophils) and from the host tissue of the metastasis biopsy 374 

(lung, liver, adrenal). We ra n infercnvpy 24  to compute a CNV score per cell, taking TME populations as 375 

reference cells. We then checked that  all epithelial cells displayed higher CNV scores compared to the 376 

reference cells and labeled these as “tumor cells”. To refine the annotation of TME populations, we 377

Detailed annotation of T cells 382 

To annotate in -depth the func tional subsets of T cells, we used the Seurat package 25  to process the 383 

corresponding datasets as follows: log -normalization (NormalizeData), identification of the most 384 

variable genes (FindVariableFeatures with vst), scaling (ScaleData), dimensionality reduction with PCA 385 

(RunPCA with 30 components), identificati on of transcriptomic clusters (FindNeighbors and 386 

FindClusters with a resolution of 0.5 for T cells and 1 for myeloid cells) and UMAP visualization 387 

(runUMAP). We used the harmony 26  package  (RunHarmony with default parameters) to integrate cells 388 

across multiple biopsy sites.  We then used the  UCell  27  package to compute cell -level scores associated 389 

to CD4 T cell markers ( CD4 , IL7R , FOXP3 , IKZF2 , CXCR3 , CCR6 , IL2RA , LEF1 ) and CD8 T cell markers 390 

(CD8A , CD8B , GZMB , PRF1 , KLRD1 , NKG7 , IFNG , CCL4 , EOMES ). T cell Seurat clusters  were annotated 391 

as CD4/CD8 based on their average UCell scores, and  cells clusters with high expression of NK cell 392 

markers ( NCAM1 , KLRF1 , KLRC1 , KLRD1  and  FCGR3A ) were labeled “NK cells”. We then isolated CD4 393 

and CD8 T cells and computed UCell scores per c ell using five CD4 -related signatures (naïve, effector, 394 

exhausted, cytotoxic, regulatory) and three CD8 -related signatures (naïve, effector, exhausted) 395 

extracted from a published pan -cancer T cell atlas 28 . We kept genes with sufficient  expression in the 396 

dataset (at least 1 count in 1% cells) and removed genes overlapping multiple signatures. UCell scores 397 

were scaled between 0 and 1 for each signature. Cells with all scores lower than 0.1 were labeled 398 

“Unknown”, while remaining cells were  labeled according to the state with the highest UCell score. 399

cells, we used Seurat FindAllMarkers function to identify markers of each myeloid subset defined in 404 

the MoMacVerse data set. For each subset, we used as markers the 50 genes with the highest fold -405

Bladder cancer cell states 413 

To explore the heterogeneity of bladder cancer cells, we conducted a PCA a nalysis with the Seurat 414 

package (20 components) on 103 genes previously associated with urothelial, basal and 415 

neuroendocrine differentiation signatures in bladder cancer 29 . Projected tumor cells on the two first 416 

components revealed a clear separation of neuroendocrine cells but a gradient between the most 417 

basal and luminal differentiation. We then computed the log -normalized mean expression of each set 418 

of marker genes in each cell. The proportion of basal cells in each sample was estimated as the 419 

proportion of cells with a log -normalized expression of the basal signature ≥ 0.1. We applied 420 

consensusMIBC 29 on pseudo -bulk RNA profiles generated by pooling tumor cell profiles to predict the 421 

molecular class of each sample and we compared the results to consensu sMIBC annotations based on 422 

matched bulk RNA -seq data. 423

Association with clinical response 425 

To identify predictors of ICI response, we compared cell type proportions and expression of individual 426 

genes between baseline samples of responders and non -responders. We used quasi -binomial 427 

generalized linear mixed models (GLMM function from the lme4 30 R package with logit link, using the 428 

biopsy site as random effect) to compare the abundance of immune cell subse ts (or cells positive for a 429 

given functional signature) between responders and non -responders. To evaluate the expression of 430 

individual genes within cell types of interest (e.g. PDCD1 in T cells), we computed pseudobulk 431 

expression levels and we used limma 31 package to identify differentially expressed genes between 432 

responders and non -responders. We used log -rank tests ( survival 32 R package) to compare the 433 

progression -free survival (PFS) and overall survival (OS) between patients with low/high scores for each 434 

feature, split by the median. 435

Mutation analyses 448 

To investigate mutation detection at the single -cell level, we ran the scReadCounts 33 tool on the 449 

scRNA -seq BAM alignment files of 32 bladder tumors with paired bulk whole exo me sequencing (WES) 450 

data. To identify somatic mutations from WES data, reads were aligned to the human genome build 451 

hg38/GRCh38.p7 using the Burrows -Wheeler Aligner (BWA) tool 34 (v0.7.17 -r1188). Duplicated reads 452 

were removed usi ng Sambamba 35 . Soma tic single nucleotide variants (SNVs) and small 453 

insertions/deletions (indels) were detected using MuTect2 36 tool (2.0, --454 

max_alt_alleles_in_normal_count=2; --max_alt_allele_in_normal_fraction=0.04) and annotated using 455 

Ensembl’s Variant Effect Predictor 37 (VEP, rele ase 101). We next screened identified somatic mutations 456 

in snRNA -seq data. For each sample, scReadCounts computed the number of reference (REF) or 457 

alternate/mutated (ALT) reads for each somatic coding SNV detected in matched WES data. Cells were 458 

considered mutated for a position if they displayed >= 1 mutated (ALT) read for the position. Mutated 459 

(ALT) and reference (REF) reads were aggregated by cell population (Tumor or TME), and mutations 460 

were labeled as follows: “Detected” if they displayed >= 3 mutated (ALT) reads in the tumor 461 

population, “Not detected” if they displayed < 3 mutated (ALT) reads and >= 10 total reads in the tumor 462 

population, “Not covered” if they displayed < 10 total reads in the tumor population. 463

Gene Expression Analysis and Survival C orrelation 465 

Bulk RNA -sequencing and clinical data were obtained from the European Genome -phenome Archive 466 

(EGA) for four clinical trials evaluating atezolizumab in bladder cancer (EGAS50000000497). Gene 467 

expression data were provided as log -transformed transc ripts per million (logTPM) values. HES1 468 

expression was analyzed across all studies using the Kruskal -Wallis test to assess inter -study 469 

distribution differences. For survival analysis, the optimal cutpoint for HES1 expression was 470 

determined using the maxima lly selected rank statistics method (surv_cutpoint function from the 471 

survminer 38 R package) in the atezolizumab treatment arm of metastatic patients. Patients were 472 

classified as HES1 -high or HES1 -low based on this cutpoint. Overall survival (OS) was estimated using 473 

the Kaplan -Meier method, and differences between groups were assessed using the log -rank test. To 474 

assess outcomes according to HES1 in the metastatic setting, data from the two randomised phase 3 475 

studies IMVigor130 and IMVigor211 were combined, excl uding patients treated with the 476 

atezolizumab -chemotherapy combination arm to focus on monotherapy comparisons (atezolizumab 477 

vs. chemotherapy alone). Cox proportional hazards regression was performed within each HES1 478 

expression subgroup to evaluate the trea tment effect (atezolizumab vs. control) and to test for 479 

treatment -biomarker interaction. Median follow -up was calculated using the reverse Kaplan -Meier 480 

method. All statistical analyses were performed using R version 4.2.0 with the survival, survminer, 481 

dply r, and ggplot2 packages. A two -sided p -value < 0.05 was considered statistically significant .482
