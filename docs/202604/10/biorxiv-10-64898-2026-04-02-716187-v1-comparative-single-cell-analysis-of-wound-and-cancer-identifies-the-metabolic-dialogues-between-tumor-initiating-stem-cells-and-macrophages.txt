# Abstract 1

Macrophages are pivotal mediators of wound healing, yet the cellular programs they 2

employ can be hijacked by cancers to drive tumorigenesis. Although similar macrophage 3

programs support both physiological tissue regeneration and pathological cell growth, the 4

molecular and functional difference between wound-associated macrophages (WAMs) 5

and tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) remain poorly defined. Here, we perform 6

comparative single-cell RNA sequencing to delineate the dynamic cell states of 7

macrophages during skin wound healing and the progression of cutaneous squamous 8

cell carcinoma. Our analyses reveal that aberrantly regulated lipid metabolism is a distinct 9

feature of TAMs. Critically, our genetic manipulations allow us to identify SOX2 High tumor-10 

initiating stem cells as key orchestrators that modulate the lipid metabolism of TAMs and 11 

shape their cell states. These findings suggest that disrupting the metabolic crosstalk 12 

between tumor-initiating stem cells and TAMs represents a promising strategy to 13 

normalize myeloid cell function and enhance cancer immunotherapy efficacy. 14

# Introduction 1

Macrophages are highly plastic myeloid cells that can rapidly shift their phenotypes 2

and cell states in response to diverse environmental cues (Locati et al., 2020; Wynn et 3

al., 2013). Stimulation by pathogen- or damage-associated molecular patterns drives 4

macrophages toward pro-inflammatory states, whereas exposure to immune-modulatory 5

ligands promotes alternative activation and regulatory functions (Biswas and Mantovani, 6

2010; Mosser and Edwards, 2008; Murray et al., 2014). Their versatility and plasticity 7

enable macrophages to play central roles in facilitating tissue repair (Vannella and Wynn, 8

2017; Wynn and Vannella, 2016). Immediately following injury, tissue-resident and 9

monocyte-derived macrophages adopt pro-inflammatory functional states to control 10 

infection and clear cellular debris (Wynn and Vannella, 2016). As repair progresses to the 11 

healing phase, macrophages promptly transition to pro-healing states to suppress 12 

inflammation and induce angiogenesis (Shook et al., 2016; Shook et al., 2018). Given 13 

that stem cells are the principal mediators of tissue regeneration, a critical function of 14 

wound-associated macrophages (WAMs) during wound repair is to support stem cell self-15 

renewal and differentiation, creating a niche that enables stem cells to efficiently restore 16 

tissue integrity (Shook et al., 2016; Shook et al., 2018). 17 

18 

Although many macrophage functions are essential for normal tissue repair, these 19 

programs are frequently co-opted by tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) to promote 20 

tumorigenesis. As one of the most abundant immune cell populations in the tumor 21 

microenvironment (TME), TAMs drive cancer cell proliferation, enhance angiogenesis, 22 

and most importantly, establish an immunosuppressive milieu (Biswas and Mantovani, 23 

2010). Indeed, similar myeloid cell responses underpin the conceptualization of cancer 24 

as “a wound that never heals” (Hu et al., 2023). Supporting this notion, recent 25 

spatiotemporal transcriptomic mapping of macrophage states during wound healing has 26 

highlighted processes such as antigen presentation and interferon responses are similarly 27 

activated in WAMs and TAMs (Hu et al., 2023). Notably, both WAMs and TAMs are 28 

thought to adopt the alternative activation (or M2-like) state, facilitating direct interactions 29 

with stem cells. It is increasingly evident that many solid tumors are initiated and 30 

maintained by a subset of tumor-initiating stem cells (tSCs) characterized by robust 31

(Ricci-Vitiani et al., 2007; Singh et al., 2004). These tSCs activate unique molecular 2

programs to drive tumorigenesis and therapy resistance. In particular, they can survive 3

robust immunotherapy treatments which otherwise effectively eradicates differentiated 4

cancer cells (Miao et al., 2019; Oshimori et al., 2015). Similar to normal tissue 5

regenerations, close interactions with the microenvironment are essential for tSCs to gain 6

extrinsic protections during tumorigenesis (Guo et al., 2017; Lu et al., 2014; Taniguchi et 7

al., 2020). Imaging analyses in skin squamous cell carcinomas (SCCs) have revealed 8

that a subset of FcεRI+ macrophages cluster near tSCs (Casanova-Acebes et al., 2021), 9

and these TAMs can produce signaling molecules—such as Wnt and TGFβ ligands—that 10 

activate corresponding pathways in tSCs (Oshimori et al., 2015; Taniguchi et al., 2020). 11 

Deciphering the reciprocal interactions between stem cells and macrophages is crucial 12 

for preventing cancer progression. 13 

14 

While previous research has largely focused on pathways that are similarly activated 15 

in macrophages during wound healing and tumorigenesis, the precise degree of similarity 16 

in cell states between WAMs and TAMs—along with any molecular features uniquely 17 

activated in either group—has not been comprehensively assessed. A fundamental 18 

difference between wound healing and cancer is that, whereas regeneration can conclude 19 

with resolution of inflammation and restoration of tissue architecture, these processes 20 

persist in cancers (Ge and Fuchs, 2018; Ge et al., 2017). Consequently, macrophages in 21 

tumors are chronically reeducated by constant cytokine signaling and reprogrammed by 22 

aberrant metabolic cues within the TME, endowing them with additional properties 23 

(Casanova-Acebes et al., 2021; Franklin et al., 2014; Hegde et al., 2025). Identifying the 24 

unique features of WAMs and TAMs and elucidating the respective mechanisms shaping 25 

their distinct cell states, may hold the key for developing strategies that normalize 26 

macrophage function in the TME as a novel approach to cancer immunotherapy. 27

In parallel, we investigated tumor-promoting macrophages in a chemically induced, 10

20 

Using these refined models, we performed comparative single-cell analysis of 21 

dynamic macrophage states during skin wound healing and cutaneous malignant 22 

progression. Through transcriptome comparisons of WAMs and TAMs, we further 23 

characterized changes in macrophage heterogeneity and cell states in response to 24 

immunotherapy, with and without perturbation of critical pathways in tSCs. This analysis 25 

identified a pivotal role for a subset of SOX2 High tSCs in sculpting the unique phenotypes 26 

of a subset of TAMs, enabling these specialized TAMs to persist through immunotherapy 27 

and reciprocally protect tSCs. 28

To compare the molecular features of immune responses elicited during 2

physiological tissue regeneration (e.g., wound healing) and pathological cell growth (e.g., 3

cancer), we subjected immune cells from different stages of partial-thickness skin wounds, 4

as well as various phases of skin squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) progression to single-5

cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) (Fig. 1a). Specifically, we used fluorescence-activated 6

cell sorting (FACS) to isolate CD45 + immune cells from Day 2 (inflammatory stage) and 7

Day 4 (healing stage) wounds in mice, and from mice bearing skin papilloma (benign) or 8

carcinoma (malignant) (two replicates per group; two pooled mice or tumors per replicate) 9

(Fig. 1a). These timepoints were chosen based on previous studies characterizing the 10 

kinetics of partial thickness wound healing (Ge et al., 2017; Gonzales et al., 2021; Luan 11 

et al., 2024). Staging of SCCs was determined by their distinct appearance, necrotic 12 

states, tumor size, and histology. To profile transcriptomic similarities and differences 13 

among immune populations under these conditions, we employed a split-pool 14 

combinatorial barcoding-based scRNA-seq approach (Rosenberg et al., 2018). This 15 

method allows for the fixation, labeling, pooling, and simultaneous sequencing of cells 16 

from various conditions and time points, thereby minimizing batch effects (Rosenberg et 17 

al., 2018). 18 

19 

Following sequencing and quality control, we selected 18,456 immune cells for 20 

further analysis (3,781 cells from D2 WD, 1,492 cells from D4 WD, 6,601 cells from 21 

papilloma, and 6,582 from SCCs). Based on transcriptional profiles, we first examined 22 

major immune cell types and global changes in the immune landscape during wound 23 

healing and tumorigenesis. In total, we identified 17 distinct clusters when combining 24 

immune cells from both wounds and cancers (Fig. 1b). Among these clusters, we 25 

detected 10 clusters in the myeloid cell compartment and 7 clusters of lymphocytes (Fig. 26 

1b). A global comparison of immune landscapes in wounds and cancers revealed key 27 

differences and similarities in the composition of these compartments. As expected, 28 

lymphocyte infiltration is the major difference between cancer and wound, and both T 29 

cells and B cells are mostly enriched in tumor samples (Fig. 1, c and d, Fig. S1a). A 30 

particularly notable T cell population that influxes into the tumor is the Foxp3 + regulatory 31

Given the critical roles of macrophages in both wound healing and cancer 11 

progression, in this study, we specifically focused on comprehensive comparison of the 12 

molecular signatures of these myeloid cells when they were driving wound repair and 13 

tumorigenesis. From our scRNA-seq data, we identified 7 major cell clusters of 14 

macrophages (Fig. 2a). Each cluster is enriched with markers (Fig. 2b) which can define 15 

the unique molecular features and distinct cell states. For example, the cells in cluster 0 16 

(C0) are M1-like, being mostly enriched with proinflammatory genes, such as genes 17 

involved in antigen presentation and providing co-stimulatory signals (Fig. 2c), whereas 18 

the cells in cluster 1 (C1) were mostly enriched with signature genes that are known to 19 

define M2-like macrophages, such as Mrc1 (genes encoding CD206), Arg1 and Cd68 20 

(Fig. 2c). Compared to these two clusters, most macrophages found in the wound or 21 

cancer are in a “hybrid” cell state, being simultaneously enriched with both M1 and M2-22 

like signatures (Fig. 2c). This dataset allowed us to identify the unique cell states that are 23 

associated with either wound healing or cancer progression. 24

Based on previous phenotype characterizations, it was established that distinct 3

macrophage functional states drive different stages of wound healing. But the previous 4

research has assumed an over-simplified paradigm. For example, the pro-inflammatory 5

macrophages are thought to be the first responders being recruited immediately upon 6

wounding to clear the dead cells or microbes (Mosser and Edwards, 2008; Wynn and 7

Vannella, 2016). When the wound healing progress to the regenerative phase after the 8

stem cells have migrated into the wound bed, these proinflammatory macrophages are 9

converted into anti-inflammatory macrophages to promote re-epithelization and tissue 10 

remodeling (Mosser and Edwards, 2008; Wynn and Vannella, 2016). Consistent with this 11 

paradigm, our analysis confirmed that the cluster 0 (C0) WAMs which are mainly enriched 12 

with pro-inflammatory gene signatures were mostly found in Day 2 post-wounding, and 13 

by Day 4, majority of these pro-inflammatory macrophages were eliminated from the 14 

wounded tissue (Fig. 3, a and b). Even with a few C0 cells remaining in the wounded skin 15 

by Day 4, these cells turned off their pro-inflammatory programs (Fig. 3c). Unlike the 16 

pattern of proinflammatory macrophages which was consistent with previously reported, 17 

to our surprise, the emergence of pro-healing macrophages was fairly early. Although the 18 

hair follicle stem cells won’t migrate into the damaged epidermis until Day3 (Ge et al., 19 

2017; Gonzales et al., 2021; Luan et al., 2024), as early as Day 2 post-wounding, more 20 

than 60% of WAMs were already in the cell states which are enriched with genes involved 21 

in immune suppression, lipid metabolism, and tissue regeneration (Fig. 3, c to e). Another 22 

interesting finding from this analysis was that, in contrast to the pro-inflammatory 23 

macrophages that had limited heterogeneity (mainly in C0 cluster), we found three distinct 24 

clusters of macrophages harboring the potential of promoting tissue regeneration and 25 

suppressing inflammation (Fig. 3, a and b). Importantly, each of these three clusters 26 

activated a different set of genes that could contribute to different aspects of tissue 27 

regeneration. For instance, among all the macrophages, the C1 cells were defined as 28 

their highest level of Arg1 (Fig. 2c) and Ptges (Fig. 3e), while the cells in C2 expressed 29 

highest level of Tgfβ1 (Fig. 2c) and Il10 (Fig. 3c). Both C1 and C2 macrophages also 30 

blunted the production of pro-inflammatory chemokines when the wound healing 31

Comprehensive comparison of macrophage signatures in wound and cancer 10 

Based on the insights gained from analyzing WAMs and TAMs separately, we next 11 

sought to compare their molecular features. To this end, we first computationally overlaid 12 

the clusters of WAMs and TAMs. Interestingly, although macrophages play similar roles 13 

in promoting cell growth and share many key functions during both wound repair and 14 

tumorigenesis, we found that the C0 cluster was the only cell state that was shared 15 

between skin wounds and skin cancers (Fig. 5, a and b). Consistent with previous 16 

reports(Hu et al., 2023), the antigen presentation and co-stimulation signature were 17 

indeed similarly activated in C0 cluster in both wound and cancer. (Fig. 3b and Fig. 4b). 18 

However, most other cell stats of WAMs and TAMs were highly distinct (Fig. 5a and 5b). 19 

As shown in Figure 5A and 5B, the C1, C2 and C3 were found mainly in wounded skin, 20 

while C4, C5, and C6 represented the cell states of macrophage in cancer. RNA velocity 21 

indicated the developmental trajectory of these macrophage clusters, showing that cell 22 

states identified in WAMs appear to be more intermediate on the trajectory, since these 23 

clusters can differentiate towards the C4 and C5 states found in TAMs (Fig. 5c). 24

SOX2 High tSCs shaped the metabolic states of TAMs. 20 

Given the shared roles of macrophages in promoting rapid cell proliferation, 21 

establishing immune suppression, and facilitating tissue remodeling in both wound 22 

healing and cancer, it was striking to observe extensive distinctions in the macrophage 23 

cell states in wound and cancer. To dissect the underlying basis that shaped the aberrant 24 

cell states of TAMs, we focused on the factors and mechanisms that were uniquely 25 

activated in cancers. The specific expression of FcεR1 (Fig. 2c) in TAM subpopulations 26 

that are enriched with lipid metabolism related genes was particularly intriguing. Recent 27 

investigations have shed light into this question. FcεR1 + TAMs are predominantly located 28 

at the tumor-stroma interface (Taniguchi et al., 2020), where they interact with TGFβ-29 

responding tSCs. These tSCs recruit TAMs via IL33 secretion(Taniguchi et al., 2020), and 30 

in turn, these FcεR1+ macrophages provide reciprocal regulation by producing ligands 31

control and Sox2 cOE wounds (Fig. 6f). In contrast to the WAMs in control wounds, the 11

such as Spp1 and Cd36 , are upregulated in C2 WAMs in the Sox2 cOE wounds (Fig. 6g). 15

we also observed upregulation of genes involved in immune suppression, such as Il10 17 

and Arg1 , and pathological inflammation, such as Ptgs2 and Il1a (Fig. 6h) in Sox2 cOE 18 

wounds. Taken together, these analysis allows us to confirm that, even without the 19 

oncogenic transformation and chronic inflammation exposure, when the epithelial stem 20 

cells start to express Sox2 , the gene signatures of their surrounding WAMs start to 21 

resemble the lipid laden macrophage-like TAMs. 22

Jing et al., 2023; Zhivaki and Kagan, 2021) and Nos2 (Sun et al., 2021; Weiss et al., 2010) 29 

(Fig. 7b), leading to significantly increase of T cells (Fig. 7c). These key results further 30 

confirmed our speculation that when the macrophages are recruited by tSCs (Taniguchi 31

# Discussion 1

Macrophages play critical roles in both physiological tissue regeneration and 2

malignant cell growth. While many studies highlighted important similarities between 3

macrophages infiltrating wounded and cancerous tissue, the key distinctions and 4

underlying regulatory mechanisms shaping the differences between these macrophages 5

are still unexplored. In this study, by performing comparative single cell analysis of 6

macrophages in skin cancers and skin wounds, we unveiled that the aberrant lipid 7

metabolism is a previously unrecognized molecular feature of TAMs that might explain 8

the unresolvable myeloid cell dysfunctions specifically found in cancers. Macrophages 9

enriched for genes involved in lipid metabolism—commonly referred to as lipid-laden 10 

macrophages—have recently been identified as the predominant TAM population 11 

responsible for pro-tumor and immune suppressive functions (Governa et al., 2024; 12 

Kloosterman et al., 2024). In particular, as verified from our single cell analysis, these 13 

subsets of macrophage are the major subpopulation expressing many important TAM-14 

associated markers, such as ARG1, SPP1, and TREM2 (Marelli et al., 2022). Thus, 15 

compared to many other macrophage subpopulations that have pro-regeneration 16 

functions in both injury repair and tumorigenesis, this subset of special TAMs might 17 

represent a special therapeutic target for normalizing the TME. Indeed, recent studies 18 

using TREM2 or SPP1 blocking antibodies has shown promising effects in treating 19 

several types of cancers in preclinical models (Kartal et al., 2025; Katzenelenbogen et al., 20 

2020; Molgora et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2023). Thus, our study provided important 21 

explanations for these therapeutic effects of new treatments. 22 

23 

A particularly surprising finding from this study is that the development of these 24 

special macrophages appears to rely on a special subset of SOX2 high tumor initiating 25 

epithelial stem cells. Importantly, we have found that when we ectopically activated Sox2 26 

expression in skin epithelium wound repair, most macrophage subpopulations are 27 

induced to differentiate towards macrophage cluster that are enriched in lipid metabolism 28 

and this cluster of cells also further upregulate their lipid metabolism, acquiring the gene 29 

signatures resembling the lipid laden TAMs. On the other hand, when we genetically 30 

ablated Sox2 in cancer, these macrophages reduced their lipid metabolism, leaving them 31

SOX2 high tSCs modulate macrophages. Furthermore, the identity of the specific lipids or 2

fatty acids produced by these tSCs to mediate this metabolic crosstalk represents a 3

critical area for future investigation. Although these important questions are out of the 4

scope and cannot be addressed in the current study, this study indeed unveiled an 5

important strategy to design novel immunotherapies. Recent studies have demonstrated 6

that these SOX2 high tSCs are the culprit of cancer relapse since they can survive from 7

robust anti-tumor immune response. This study demonstrated that these tSCs may 8

achieve this feature by modulating the differentiation of macrophages into lipid-laden 9

macrophages, so they can form a special niche and provide reciprocal protections for 10 

these tSCs. Future investigation should focus on dissecting the molecular mechanisms 11 

employed by these SOX2 high tSCs to modulate macrophages. These future studies will 12 

uncover novel strategies to disrupt the tSC-macrophage communications, thereby 13 

removing this critical niche protection and sensitizing tSCs to T cell-mediated attack. 14

Taken together, this comprehensive comparative analysis provides an important 16 

resource for understanding the developmental trajectory and molecular features of tumor-17 

associated macrophages. Moreover, it uncovers a key orchestrator that shapes the 18 

heterogeneity and functional states of TAMs. 19

Tumor formation and treatment 4

A previously established two-stage chemical carcinogenesis protocol was used to induce 5

skin SCC formation. The shaved dorsal skin of various mouse lines was topically treated 6

with 200 μL of 200 nM DMBA once a week for 4 weeks to initiate tumorigenesis. Four 7

weeks later, tumor promotion was induced by weekly (twice a week) applications of 200 8

μL of 35 μM TPA for 20 weeks. 9

For immunotherapy treatment, tumor bearing mice were treated with a combination of 10 

anti-PD-L1 blocking antibody (Clone 10F.9G2, 100 μg/mouse, BioXCell) and anti-CD40 11 

agonist antibody (Clone FGK4.5, 100 μg/mouse, BioXCell) intraperitoneal for a total of 2 12 

doses every other day. Tumors were collected 2 days after the last treatment for 13 

downstream experiments. 14 

15 

Partial thickness wound 16 

The partial thickness wounding was performed following previously established protocols 17 

(Ge et al., 2017). Briefly, mice were shaved in second telogen (postnatal day P60). The 18 

remaining hair was removed using depilatory cream, and a Dremel drill head was used 19 

to gently scrape the back skin of anesthetized mice. To standardize the wound depth, the 20 

number of touches performed with the Dremel drill was pre-determined by inspecting the 21 

wounded skin for the first signs of erythema and pinpoint bleeding. This method removes 22 

the epidermis and upper HF, including most infundibulum and isthmus cells, but leaves 23 

the HF bulge intact. 24 

25
