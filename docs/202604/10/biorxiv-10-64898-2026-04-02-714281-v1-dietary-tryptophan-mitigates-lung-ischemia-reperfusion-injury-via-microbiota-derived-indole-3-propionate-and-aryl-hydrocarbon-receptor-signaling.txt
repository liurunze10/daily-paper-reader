Background: Lung ischemia–reperfusion (IR) injury drives early morbidity after lung 1transplantation and cardiothoracic surgery, yet targeted preventive therapies are lacking. 2The gut–lung axis and microbiota-derived tryptophan metabolites, including indole-3-3propionate (IPA), may regulate pulmonary immunity and inflammation. We investigated 4whether a tryptophan-rich (Trp-Rich) diet attenuates sterile lung IR injury by increasing 5microbiota-derived indole metabolites and reprogramming alveolar macrophage (AM) 6inflammatory responses. 7

Methods: C57BL/6 mice received isocaloric tryptophan-standard (Trp-Std; 0.18%) or 8Trp-Rich (0.60%) diets for 14 days, then underwent unilateral left lung IR (60 min 9ischemia followed by 60 min reperfusion). Oxygen saturation, lung cytokines, and aryl 10 hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) signaling readouts ( Cyp1a1 /Cyp1b1 ) were evaluated. Gut 11 microbiota was profiled by 16S rRNA sequencing, and targeted metabolomics quantified 12 tryptophan metabolites in feces, portal vein (PV) plasma, and lung tissue. To further 13 assess inflammatory priming in vivo , mice were additionally challenged with 14 intratracheal lipopolysaccharide (LPS). Mechanistic studies compared IPA with related 15 indole metabolites in MH-S cells and primary human AMs, including ex vivo nutritional 16 IR, LPS stimulation, and AhR stimulation and blockade using synthetic agonists and 17 antagonists. 18 

Results: Trp-Rich feeding improved post-IR oxygenation, reduced lung IL-1 β, and 19 increased pulmonary Cyp1a1 /Cyp1b1 gene expression. Trp-Rich diet remodeled the gut 20 microbiota, including enrichment of Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus , and increased 21 IPA levels across feces, PV plasma, and lung tissue, with lower kynurenine/IPA ratios 22 across matrices. 5Lung ischemia-reperfusion (IR) injury constitutes a significant contributor to early 1morbidity and mortality following lung transplantation and cardiothoracic surgical 2procedures, characterized by an abrupt onset of sterile inflammation, disruption of 3endothelial and epithelial barriers, and neutrophil-predominant alveolitis [1-3]. Recent 4experimental and translational investigations have elucidated the pivotal roles of 5activated alveolar macrophages (AMs) in exacerbating vascular permeability and diffuse 6alveolar damage [4-8]. Mechanistically, lung IR injury is consistent with a two-signal 7model in which priming via Lipopolysaccharide (LPS)/TLR4/ NF-κB (Signal 1) and 8subsequent inflammasome activation triggered by IR-associated cues (Signal 2), 9including (NOD-like receptor family pyrin domain containing 3 (NLRP3) activation, 10 together drive sterile lung inflammasome [8-11]. Nevertheless, no specific therapeutic 11 interventions for either signal have been incorporated into routine clinical practice. 12 These limitations underscore the need for innovative strategies that modulate upstream 13 inflammatory pathways rather than solely addressing downstream injury. 14 Compelling evidence positions the gut–lung axis as a fundamental regulator of 15 pulmonary immune responses [12]. Alterations in both the composition and functionality 16 of the intestinal microbiota significantly influence susceptibility to pneumonia, acute 17 respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), and chronic pulmonary diseases through 18 microbially-derived metabolites, such as short-chain fatty acids, tryptophan derivatives, 19 and others, which orchestrate myeloid and epithelial responses at remote mucosal 20 locations (reviewed in [13-18]). Mechanistic investigations reveal that gut-derived 21 metabolites can modulate myeloid and tissue-resident immune cell responses at distant 22 sites [12,19,20]. Conversely, dysbiosis or the depletion of beneficial microbes and 23

These findings suggest that salutary dietary 1or microbiota-based interventions may provide an indirect protective effect against lung 2IR injury by reprogramming systemic and pulmonary immune responses [21]. 3Nonetheless, this concept remains inadequately explored within the context of acute 4sterile lung injury. 5Among microbial metabolites, indole derivatives generated from dietary tryptophan are 6emerging as key regulators of mucosal immunity (reviewed in [22]). Indole-3-propionate 7(IPA) is a prominent member of the microbiota-derived indole family, which also includes 8indole-3-acetate (IAA) and indole-3-lactate (ILA). IPA exerts antioxidant and anti-9inflammatory effects across experimental models, including suppression of NF-κB and 10 inflammasome signaling, preservation of barrier integrity, and modulation of 11 macrophage phenotypes via aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) signaling [23-25]. 12 Microbiota-derived indoles can act through AhR-dependent pathways to regulate 13 immune responses at barrier and distant tissues [19,20]. Microbiota-derived indoles 14 have also been linked to protection against systemic inflammation and mitigation of 15 mucosal and pulmonary injury in infection-related models [26,27]. However, it remains 16 unclear whether a dietary strategy that increases microbial indole metabolism can raise 17 endogenous IPA across the gut and circulation, particularly within the lung, and whether 18 these changes are coupled with reduced lung inflammation and altered AhR-related 19 responses during sterile lung IR injury. 20 Consequently, we posited that a diet rich in tryptophan would modify gut microbiota 21 composition and enhance the production of gut-derived tryptophan metabolites, 22 particularly IPA, reducing lung inflammation following IR injury.
