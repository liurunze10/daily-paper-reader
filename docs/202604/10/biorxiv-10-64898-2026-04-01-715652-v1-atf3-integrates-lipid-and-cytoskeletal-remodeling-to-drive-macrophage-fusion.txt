> ABSTRACT 26
> Macrophages are highly plastic innate immune cells that can form multinucleated giant cells 27
> (MGCs) under physiological and pathological conditions, such as osteoclasts and foreign body 28
> giant cells. The mechanisms governing macrophage multinucleation remain incompletely 29
> understood. Here, we identify activating transcription factor 3 (Atf3) as an essential regulator of 30
> MGC formation via cell-cell fusion in response to persistent Interleukin 4 (IL-4) and STAT6 31
> signaling, characteristic of the foreign body reaction.  Atf3 -deficient macrophages activate 32
> STAT6-dependent transcriptional programs in response to IL-4, including fusion-associated 33
> genes, yet fail to undergo multinucleation. This defect is associated with impaired actin 34
> cytoskeleton remodeling, abnormal nuclear morphology, reduced lamin A/C expression, and 35
> genome instability. Mechanistically, loss of  Atf3  derepresses the  Cholesterol-25-hydroxylase 36
> (Ch25h ), elevating 25-hydroxycholesterol (25-HC), suppressing the mevalonate pathway, and 37
> reducing cholesterol and isoprenoid biosynthesis essential for cytoskeletal dynamics. Deletion 38
> of  Ch25h  in  Atf3 -deficient macrophages restores cholesterol levels and actin turnover, but not 39
> lamin A/C or fusion. These findings establish Atf3 as a central transcriptional node integrating 40
> lipid  metabolism  with  cytoskeletal  and  nucleoskeletal  remodeling  to  control  IL-4-driven 41
> macrophage multinucleation. 42

> INTRODUCTION 47
> Macrophages are versatile sentinels of the innate immune system, serving as professional 48
> phagocytes, antigen-presenting cells and coordinators of tissue repair (Mass  et al , 2023; Park  et 49
> al , 2022; Wynn & Vannella, 2016). A remarkable feature of macrophages is their ability to form 50
> multinucleated giant cells (MGCs) under specific physiological and pathological conditions. 51
> Osteoclasts represent the prototypical physiological MGCs that differentiate in response to the 52
> receptor activator of NF-κB ligand (RANKL) to mediate bone remodeling and homeostasis 53
> (Levaot  et al , 2015; Yasuda  et al , 1998). In contrast, pathological MGCs arise during chronic 54
> inflammation associated with  granuloma formation in  infectious, autoinflammatory, and 55
> allergic contexts. Prominent examples include Langhans cells in tuberculosis, MGCs found in 56
> giant cell arteritis (GCA) and atherosclerosis, and foreign body giant cells (FBGCs) formed in 57
> response to implanted biomaterials or tissue-damaging amyloid fibrils (Ahmadzadeh  et al ,58
> 2022; Anderson  et al , 2008; Bodin  et al , 2010; Brooks  et al , 2019; Samokhin  et al , 2010). 59
> Importantly, the differentiation of distinct MGC subtypes is dictated by the local inflammatory 60
> milieu. For example, Langhans cells form in response to Toll-like receptor 2 (TLR2) and tumor 61
> necrosis factor (TNF) whereas FBGCs are driven by a Th2-polarized environment rich in IL-4 and 62
> IL-13, highlighting the existence of context-dependent transcriptional programs governing MGC 63
> differentiation (Helming & Gordon, 2009; Herrtwich  et al , 2016; McInnes & Rennick, 1988; 64
> McNally & Anderson, 1995; Puissegur  et al , 2007). 65
> Macrophage multinucleation occurs via two primary mechanisms: cell-cell fusion of individual 66
> macrophages,  and  acytokinetic  cell  division,  where  nuclear  division  proceeds  without 67
> cytokinesis (Helming & Gordon, 2009; Herrtwich  et al. , 2016; Takegahara  et al , 2016). Both 68
> processes  require  tight  coordination  of  transcriptional  reprograming,  cytoskeletal  and 69
> membrane remodeling. The process of cell fusion is particularly complex, involving migration, 70
> acquisition of a fusion-competent state, assembly of adhesive, force-generating podosomes, 71
> and finally the formation of the fusion pore and plasma membrane merging (Dufrancais  et al ,72
> 2021; Helming & Gordon, 2009). These steps critically depend on actin cytoskeleton dynamics, 73
> as disrupting key regulators like WASP, Rac-1, Cdc42, Filamin A or the Arp2/3 complex, severely 74
> impairs fusion efficiency (Arya  et al , 2021; DeFife  et al , 1997; Faust  et al , 2019; Gonzalo  et al ,75

> 2010;  Jay  et  al , 2007;  Leung  et  al , 2010).  Concurrently,  membrane  lipid  composition, 76
> particularly cholesterol content, provides another layer of regulation by modulating membrane 77
> fluidity  and  protein  distribution,  thereby  affecting  the  organization  of  the  cytoskeleton 78
> (Brukman  et al , 2019; Lee & Bensinger, 2022; Lösslein  et al , 2021). However, how macrophages 79
> integrate extracellular signals with these intrinsic programs of lipid metabolism, cytoskeletal 80
> rearrangement, and transcriptional control to drive multinucleation remains incompletely 81
> understood. 82
> The stress-responsive Activating transcription factor 3 (Atf3), a member of the ATF/CREB family, 83
> has emerged as a key regulator of inflammatory and metabolic programs (Hu  et al , 2024; 84
> Jadhav & Zhang, 2017; Rynes  et al , 2012). In macrophages, Atf3 attenuates pro-inflammatory 85
> cytokine expression driven by Toll-like receptor (TLR) signaling (De Nardo  et al , 2014; Gilchrist 86
> et al , 2006; Labzin  et al , 2015) while also suppressing the transcription of  Cholesterol-25-87
> hydroxylase  (Ch25h ) (Gold  et al , 2012). Ch25h is an ER-resident enzyme that produces 25-88
> hydroxycholesterol (25-HC) (Lund  et al , 1998), a potent oxysterol that restricts cholesterol and 89
> isoprenoid synthesis via the mevalonate pathway and alters plasma membrane cholesterol 90
> content (Griffiths & Wang, 2022). Furthermore, Atf3 promotes macrophage migration and 91
> metabolic phenotypes associated with alternative activation, which are processes permissive 92
> for MGC formation (Hu  et al. , 2024; Lösslein  et al. , 2021). The physiological importance of Atf3 93
> is highlighted by the increased susceptibility of Atf3-deficient mice to septic shock and 94
> atherosclerosis (De Nardo  et al. , 2014; Gilchrist  et al. , 2006; Gold  et al. , 2012). Intriguingly, 95
> emerging evidence also links Atf3 to regulation of cytoarchitecture, including components of 96
> the cytoskeleton and nucleoskeleton (Boespflug  et al , 2014; Donohoe  et al , 2018; Du  et al ,97
> 2022; Sekyrova  et al , 2010). Together, these functions position Atf3 as a potential integrator of 98
> the  inflammatory,  metabolic,  and  structural  remodeling  required  for  macrophage 99
> multinucleation. 100
> Here, we identify Atf3 as a central regulator of IL-4-driven macrophage fusion. We demonstrate 101
> that  Atf3  deficiency leads to the derepression of  Ch25h , resulting in 25-HC accumulation and 102
> suppression of the mevalonate pathway. These metabolic changes cause profound defects in 103
> membrane and actin dynamics, leading to aberrant morphology and impaired multinucleation. 104

> RESULTS 111
> Atf3 is required for macrophage multinucleation downstream of IL-4 112
> Macrophage multinucleation occurs physiologically in the bone, where osteoclasts form in 113
> response to RANKL, and pathologically during chronic inflammation such as  Mycobacteria 114
> infection or foreign body reactions driven by TLR2 and IL-4/IL-13 signaling, respectively. To 115
> determine whether Atf3 is involved in this process, we assessed its expression in mouse bone 116
> marrow-derived macrophages (BMDMs) exposed to different MGC-inducing stimuli, including 117
> RANKL, TLR2 agonists and IL-4. In this study, multinucleation is defined as the presence of more 118
> than  two  nuclei  within  a single  macrophage,  a criterion  used  throughout  to  define 119
> multinucleated giant cells (MGCs). Atf3 mRNA and protein levels were significantly induced 120
> after one day of IL-4 treatment but remained unchanged in response to RANKL or the synthetic 121
> TLR2 agonists, FSL-1 and Pam3CSK4 (Fig. 1A, B). In  control  BMDM cultures, giant cells appeared 122
> after one day of IL-4 treatment and increased in size and number over time (Fig. 1P-Q). 123
> Strikingly, macrophages isolated from whole-body  Atf3  knockout ( Atf3 KO ) mice generated by 124
> CRISPR/Cas9 (Fig. 1B, C) failed to multinucleate in response to IL-4 (Fig. 1D-O, Q). Increasing cell 125
> density, IL-4 concentration, or both, or culturing cells on a fusogenic Permanox substrate 126
> (Helming & Gordon, 2009), did not mitigate the multinucleation defect of  Atf3 KO  cells (Fig. 1r-u). 127
> The absence of MGCs was also evident in IL-4-stimulated macrophages isolated from the 128
> peritoneal cavity of  Atf3 -deficient mice (Fig. 1V-X), indicating that Atf3 is essential for IL-4-129
> driven macrophage multinucleation. 130
> Despite this defect,  Atf3 KO  BMDMs differentiated into multinucleated osteoclasts in response to 131
> RANKL  in vitro , albeit they were smaller compared to the  control  (Fig. 1Y-AA).  Atf3 -deficient 132
> BMDMs also formed MGCs when stimulated with FSL-1 or infected with a wild-type strain of 133

> Atf3 promotes macrophage cell-cell fusion in response to IL-4 140
> Given the multinucleation defect of  Atf3 KO  BMDMs in response to IL-4, we examined whether 141
> Atf3 is required for cell-cell fusion or acytokinetic division, the two principal mechanisms 142
> generating MGCs (Fig. 2A). Time-lapse imaging revealed that both processes occurred in  control 143
> BMDM cultures upon IL-4 stimulation (Fig. 2A, B and Supplementary Movie 1). To assess their 144
> relative  contributions,  we  co-cultured  differentially  labeled  BMDMs  expressing  either 145
> cytoplasmic tdTOMATO or nuclear H2B-EGFP (Fig. 2A). After three days of IL-4, most  control 146
> MGCs (95%) were positive for both fluorescent markers (Fig. 2C, E, F), indicating that cell-cell 147
> fusion is the dominant mechanisms underlying IL-4-induced multinucleation. GFP-positive 148
> nuclei accounted for nearly 50% of the total nuclei in tdTOMATO-positive MGCs , suggesting 149
> equal fusion competence of the two  control  populations (Fig. 2G). In contrast, fusion efficiency 150
> was dramatically reduced when  control  BMDMs were co-cultured with  Atf3 KO  cells (Fig. 2D-F). 151
> Atf3 KO  cells contributed less than 15% of nuclei to MGCs (Fig. 2G) and co-cultures of  Atf3 KO
> 152
> BMDMs alone failed to generate any MGCs (Fig. 2I). The proportion of binucleated cells was 153
> also decreased in  Atf3 KO  cultures, and those that did form never expressed both fluorescent 154
> markers (Fig. 2I, J) consistent with incomplete mitosis  rather than fusion. Together, these 155
> results establish Atf3 as a key regulator of macrophage fusion  and reinforce the concept that 156
> cell-cell fusion, rather than acytokinetic division, is the primary mechanism driving IL-4-157
> mediated MGC formation. 158

> Atf3 controls the mevalonate pathway required for protein prenylation and cholesterol 230
> homeostasis 231
> Actin regulators, such as WASP, Cofilin and FLNA are controlled by the Rho family GTPases 232
> (RhoA, Rac1 and Cdc42) (Jaffe & Hall, 2005). The membrane localization and activity of these 233
> GTPases require post-translational farnesylation or geranylgeranylation by prenyltransferases 234
> (Hodge & Ridley, 2016). The required isoprenoids are synthesized via the mevalonate pathway 235
> from farnesyl pyrophosphate (FPP), a common precursor for non-sterol isoprenoids and 236
> cholesterol (Fig. 5A and Supplementary Fig. 1C). Inhibition of prenyltransferases, or the rate-237
> limiting enzyme of the mevalonate pathway, HMG-CoA reductase (Hmgcr), disrupts Rho GTPase 238
> membrane association and activity resulting in altered actin turnover and remodeling (Akula  et 239
> al , 2019; Cordle  et al , 2005). 240
> Atf3 KO  macrophages showed downregulation of genes associated with small GTPase regulation 241
> and signaling (Fig. 3N and Supplementary Dataset 2), and specifically the mevalonate pathway, 242
> including  Hmgcs1  and  Hmgcr , the cholesterol biosynthesis regulator, Sterol Regulatory Element 243
> Binding Protein 2 ( Srebf2 ), and a Sterol O-acyltransferase 1 ( Soat1 ), which esterifies cholesterol 244
> (Figs. 3N, 5B, Supplementary Fig. 1C and Supplementary Dataset 2). In contrast,  Ch25h , a known 245
> direct Atf3 target gene encoding Cholesterol-25-hydroxylase (Gold  et al. , 2012), was strongly 246
> upregulated in  Atf3 KO  BMDMs compared to  control  cells (Figs. 3K, 5B, 5C, and Supplementary 247
> Dataset 1). Its oxysterol product, 25-hydroxycholesterol (25-HC), depletes accessible plasma 248
> membrane cholesterol, promotes LXR-mediated efflux, suppresses Srebf2  activation, and 249

> DISCUSSION 352
> Macrophage multinucleation is a striking cellular transformation observed both in physiological 353
> tissue remodeling and chronic inflammatory responses (Anderson  et al. , 2008; Brooks  et al. ,354
> 2019; Helming & Gordon, 2009; Milde  et al , 2015; Miron & Bosshardt, 2018; Pereira  et al ,355
> 2018). Here, we identify Atf3 as a pivotal transcriptional regulator of IL-4-driven macrophage 356
> multinucleation via cell-cell fusion, a process central to foreign body giant cell (FBGC) formation 357
> (Kao  et al , 1995; McNally & Anderson, 1995). Our findings support a model in which Atf3 358
> promotes fusion through two converging mechanisms: (1) Regulation of lipid metabolism via 359
> repression of  Ch25h , thereby sustaining cholesterol homeostasis and actin remodeling, and (2) 360
> Maintenance  of  nuclear  lamina  integrity,  ensuring  nuclear  resilience  and  effective 361
> mechanotransduction (Fig. 7W). 362
> 363
> Atf3 as a context-specific regulator of IL-4-driven cell-cell fusion 364

> The requirement for Atf3 in IL-4-induced multinucleation contrasts with its dispensability for 365
> RANKL-driven  osteoclastogenesis  or  mycobacteria-induced  TLR2-mediated  Langhans  cell 366
> formation. Its strong induction by IL-4, but not by RANKL or TLR2 agonists underscores its 367
> specificity for the FBGC program. Mechanistic differences in the formation of distinct MGC 368
> subtypes likely contribute to this selectivity. FBGCs and osteoclasts arise primarily via cell-cell 369
> fusion (Faust  et al. , 2019; McNally & Anderson, 1995; Stewart  et al , 2024; Yagi  et al , 2005), 370
> whereas Langhans cells form mainly via modified cell divisions and mitotic defects (Herrtwich  et 371
> al. , 2016). This may explain why Atf3-deficient macrophages can still generate MGCs in 372
> response to mycobacteria or bacterial lipoproteins. In a Th2-polarized environment favoring 373
> FBGC formation, Atf3 functions as a transcriptional node linking IL-4 signaling to fusion-374
> competent cellular programs. Interestingly, the reduced size of Atf3-deficient osteoclasts 375
> suggests a modulatory role in the RANKL-induced MGC program, potentially in cooperation 376
> with the closely related bZIP transcription factor Jdp2 (Maruyama  et al , 2012). 377
> The occasional integration of  Atf3 -deficient macrophages into MGCs when co-cultured with 378
> control cells indicates that their fusion defect is not absolute. Such residual activity aligns with 379
> the model of heterogeneous “founder” and “follower” subpopulations described in myoblast 380
> fusion (Kim  et al , 2015) and later recognized in osteoclasts (Hobolt-Pedersen  et al , 2014; Levaot 381
> et al. , 2015; Yagi  et al. , 2005), and FBGCs (Faust  et al. , 2019). Founder cells initiate fusion and 382
> undergo extensive cytoskeletal remodeling, implying that Atf3 may be essential for establishing 383
> the actin- and adhesion-based architecture required for this role. 384

> Lipid-cytoskeleton coupling in fusion competence 386
> Loss of Atf3 leads to pronounced cytoskeletal abnormalities despite IL-4-STAT-6-mediated 387
> transcriptional priming and induction of canonical fusion-associated genes.  Atf3 -deficient 388
> macrophages  exhibited  elongated  morphology,  reduced  membrane  ruffling,  defective 389
> lamellipodia and impaired podosome formation, phenotypes reminiscent of disruptions in Rho 390
> GTPase family members (RhoA/RhoB, Cdc42 and Rac1) or the Arp2/3 complex (Konigs  et al ,391
> 2014; Qi & Yu, 2025; Rotty  et al , 2017; Wells  et al , 2004), all of which are essential for efficient 392
> IL-4-induced fusion (Balabiyev  et al , 2020; Faust  et al. , 2019; Jay  et al. , 2007). Transcriptomic 393

> on Src family kinase-mediated phosphorylation downstream of Cdc42 (Looi  et al , 2014), 400
> changes in WASP localization and levels despite reduced F-actin network suggest impaired Rho 401

> and function (Faust  et al. , 2019; Guiet  et al , 2012; Song  et al , 2014), their misregulation likely 403

> We link these cytoskeletal defects to dysregulated lipid metabolism. Loss of  Atf3  leads to 405
> elevated  Ch25h  expression and accumulation of its oxysterol product 25-hydroxycholesterol 406
> (25-HC). 25-HC suppresses the mevalonate pathway, reducing cholesterol levels, altering 407
> membrane composition, and decreasing isoprenoid  intermediates. This, in turn, impairs 408
> prenylation of Rho GTPases, disrupting actin remodeling and weakening the coordination 409
> between the plasma membrane and the actin cytoskeleton that is essential for spatially and 410
> temporally controlled membrane dynamics during cell-cell fusion. The similarity between 411
> phenotypes induced by  Atf3  deficiency, 25-HC treatment, and pharmacological inhibition of 412
> prenylation, together with partial rescue of actin dynamics upon  Ch25h  deletion, highlights the 413
> importance of integrated lipid-cytoskeletal regulation in IL-4-driven fusion. While  Ch25h  is best 414
> known as an interferon-inducible regulator of cellular cholesterol content and inflammasome 415
> activation (Adams  et al , 2004; Dang  et al , 2017; Park & Scott, 2010; Reboldi  et al , 2014), 25-HC 416
> also inhibits virus-cell fusion by depleting an accessible pool of plasma membrane cholesterol 417
> (Heisler  et al , 2023; Liu  et al , 2013; Wang  et al , 2020; Zang  et al , 2020). Our findings extend this 418
> function of 25-HC to macrophage fusion. Notably,  Ch25h  expression is transiently induced in IL-419
> 4-stimulated macrophages (Czimmerer  et al , 2018, our data - Supplementary Dataset 1) 420
> suggesting a physiological role for 25-HC in fine-tuning immune-metabolic responses during 421
> early  alternative  activation.  However,  sustained  25-HC  elevation,  as  in  Atf3  deficient 422

> Ch25h-independent control of nuclear lamina integrity 429
> Restoring cholesterol levels and actin dynamics in  Atf3 KO Ch25h KO  macrophages did not rescue 430

> Implications and future directions 449
> Atf3 emerges as a central regulator of macrophage fusion competence in IL-4 dominated 450
> environments, coupling lipid-cytoskeletal regulation via Ch25h repression with lamin A/C-451
> dependent nuclear stability. This dual control ensures both membrane plasticity and nuclear 452
> resilience required for giant cell formation. Disruption of either pathway impairs fusion while 453
> combined loss, as observed in  Atf3  deficiency, blocks it entirely. Our findings have direct 454
> relevance  for  conditions  involving  macrophage  multinucleation,  including  foreign  body 455
> reactions and atherosclerosis, where Atf3 has been implicated (De Nardo  et al. , 2014; Gold  et 456
> al. , 2012; and this study), as well as osteoclastogenesis, or granulomatous inflammation, in 457
> which coordination of mechanotransduction and lipid metabolism may occur independently of 458
> Atf3. The inhibitory effect of sustained 25-HC parallels that of statins, widely used cholesterol 459
> lowering drugs, raising the possibility that pharmacological or endogenous suppression of the 460
> mevalonate pathway converges on shared mechanism limiting giant cell formation. These 461
> suggests that statin therapy may alter FBGC responses or osteoclastogenesis, with implications 462
> for biomaterial integration and bone homeostasis. Similarly, defects in lamin A/C, as seen in 463
> laminopathies or during natural aging (McClintock  et al , 2007; Scaffidi & Misteli, 2006) may 464
> compromise  nuclear  mechanics  in  other  fusion-dependent  contexts,  including  muscle 465
> regeneration to placental development. Modulating Atf3 or its downstream effectors may 466
> therefore represent a strategy to fine-tune multinucleated giant cell formation in therapeutic 467
> settings. 468
