Abstract 43 

Background and Aims: The rising incidence of Crohn’s disease (CD) in Westernized 44 countries has been linked to changes in diet and increased consumption of food additives, yet 45 the mechanisms by which these factors fuel intestinal inflammation remain unclear. Adherent-46 invasive Escherichia coli (AIEC), a pathobiont involved in CD pathogenesis, lacks a clear 47 genetic hallmark but exhibits intestinal colonization and virulence traits, raising questions 48 about the evolutionary forces promoting its emergence among select individuals. Here, we 49 investigated how chronic exposure to two common dietary emulsifiers, 50 carboxymethylcellulose (CMC) and polysorbate 80 (P80), along with host inflammation, 51 drives AIEC genomic evolution and pathogenic potential. 52 53 

Methods: Wild-type and Il10-deficient mice were monocolonized with AIEC and 54 chronically exposed to CMC, P80, or water. Bacterial isolates were collected and analyzed for 55 genomic diversification, mutations, and phenotype both in vitro and in vivo .56 57 

Results: Emulsifiers accelerated AIEC genomic diversification and selected for 58 mutations linked to increased motility, invasion, and pro-inflammatory activity. Moreover, 59 dietary emulsifier-evolved strains displayed a marked fitness advantage in vivo , outcompeting 60 their counterparts in murine hosts, with the greatest advantage observed when evolution 61 occurred under inflammatory conditions. Notably, evolutionary pathways and phenotypic 62 outcomes were shaped by both emulsifier and the host’s inflammatory status, highlighting 63 synergy between diet and host genetics in fostering pro-inflammatory pathobionts. 64 65 

Conclusion: These findings provide an evolutionary framework connecting modern 66 dietary habits to the emergence of pathogenic AIEC strains, and underscore the importance of 67 dietary interventions in individuals at risk for inflammatory bowel disease. 68 69 

Key Words: Dietary emulsifier, genomic evolution, AIEC, pathobiont, Crohn’s disease 70

Introduction 72 Throughout human history, dietary habits have evolved under the influence of 73 biological, environmental, technological, and sociocultural forces. Industrialization marked a 74 decisive shift, introducing food additives designed to extend shelf life, enhance texture, and 75 support large-scale production, particularly across Western countries 1. While these 76 innovations have diversified our food supply, the rapid emergence of novel dietary 77 components, especially food additives, raises important questions about human physiological 78 and microbial adaptation. The mismatch between our ancestral diets and modern eating 79 patterns may underlie the growing prevalence of noncommunicable diseases, such as Crohn’s 80 disease (CD), observed in Western societies 2–4 .81 Although CD lacks a single microbial signature, it is frequently associated with a 82 dysbiosis characterized by a decline in beneficial bacterial phyla and a proliferation of 83 mucosa-associated Enterobacteriaceae , most notably adherent-invasive Escherichia coli 84 (AIEC) 5–9 . AIEC strains are characterized by enhanced adherence to and invasion of intestinal 85 epithelial cells, features suspected to promote chronic mucosal inflammation 10–12 . However, 86 unlike classical pathogenic E. coli , AIEC strains do not share a unique genetic signature, and 87 are phylogenetically diverse, suggesting their independent emergence in response to diverse 88 selective pressures. Indeed, studies tracking AIEC evolution in animal models have reported 89 that host-associated factors, including chronic inflammation, can drive the selection of more 90 virulent lineages 13,14 .91 Among environmental factors, diet is a major determinant of gut microbiota 92 composition and host inflammatory status 15,16 . Epidemiological studies have associated high 93 consumption of ultra-processed foods and their commonly added dietary emulsifier, including 94 carboxymethylcellulose (CMC) and polysorbate 80 (P80), with increased risk of CD 4,17 .95 These emulsifiers have been shown in mouse models to disrupt the gut microbial ecosystem 96

Moreover, both human and animal 97 studies indicate that the detrimental impact of dietary emulsifiers depends on resident 98 microbiota 20,21 .99 Our previous work has identified AIEC as a key direct target of emulsifier-induced 100 dysbiosis and inflammation. Indeed, colonization with AIEC was observed to be sufficient to 101 make mice highly susceptible to the harmful effects of CMC and P80, and direct exposure to 102 emulsifier drives the expression of AIEC’s virulence genes 22 . However, the long-term 103 consequences of chronic emulsifier exposure on AIEC evolution remain unknown. We 104 hypothesize here that dietary emulsifier consumption, either alone or in the context of host 105 inflammation, could serve as a selective pressure driving AIEC adaptation to favor its ability 106 to stably colonize the gastrointestinal tract, together with increasing its pathogenic potential. 107 To address this, we tracked the evolution of AIEC in monocolonized wild-type (WT) and 108

Our findings importantly revealed that dietary 110 emulsifiers are sufficient to drive genomic and phenotypic evolution of AIEC bacteria, 111 ultimately leading to their increased pathogenic potential. Altogether, these data provide new 112 insight into how modern dietary practices might have contributed to the emergence of 113 pathobionts in Crohn’s disease. 114

Mice monocolonization experiment 116 As previously reported in Viennois et al 22 , C57BL/6 inbred mice (C57BL/6NTac) and 117 Germ-free C57BL/6 Il10 -/- inbred male mice (C57BL/6NTac-Il10 em8Tac ; Taconic model GF-118 16006) were maintained in isolated ventilated cages Isocages (Techniplast, West Chester, PA, 119 USA 23 ) to prevent exogenous bacterial contamination. All mice were bred and housed at 120 Georgia State University (Atlanta, GA, USA) under institutionally-approved protocols 121 (IACUC # A17047 and A18006). Whole genome sequencing of evolved AIEC clones 149 Total DNA were extracted from the isolated AIEC clones using the Wizard® HMW 150 DNA Extraction Kit (Promega), following the manufacturer’s instructions. Genomic 151 sequencing was performed on Illumina Nextseq 2000 (QC ADN Illumina DNA prep 200 152 cycles) sequencing runs at the Institut Cochin (Paris, France). Bioinformatic analysis were 153 performed as previously described 14 . Briefly, raw reads were filtered and trimmed to ensure 154 good quality using Cutadapt (version 3.2) 27 . Any pair containing Ns, homopolymers (10 155 nucleotides or more), or those longer than 151 bp were discarded. Sequences were trimmed 156 with a quality cutoff of 25 at both ends for both reads, Illumina adapters were removed, and 157 sequences shorter than 50 bp were discarded. Construction of phylogenetic tree 164 Single ‐nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) discovery and phylogenetic reconstruction of 165 Illumina paired-end reads from AIEC isolates were carried out by mapping to the LF82 166 reference genome (PRJNA33825). Briefly reads were aligned to the LF82 reference with 167 BWA-MEM, variants were called with FreeBayes v1.3.7, SNPs were filtered by base quality 168 (Q ≥ 20) and mapping quality (MAPQ ≥ 30), and variant annotation was performed with 169 SnpEff 5.1 30 . A core SNP alignment spanning the isolates was generated with snippy-core, 170 yielding both a full multiple-sequence alignment and a SNP-only alignment. Putative 171 recombinant regions were removed by applying Gubbins v2.4.1 31 to the full alignment. A 172 maximum ‐likelihood phylogeny was inferred from the recombination-filtered core alignment 173 using FastTree2.1.10 under the GTR+ Γ substitution model, resulting in the final phylogenetic 174 tree. Tree support values were calculated by FastTree’s internal Shimodaira–Hasegawa test. 175 A maximum likelihood phylogeny was reconstructed from the 176 recombination filtered core genome alignment with IQ TREE  2.2.6. Such analysis 177 employed the GTR  + Γ  nucleotide substitution model. Node reliability was assessed by 178 performing 1.000 ultrafast bootstrap (UFBoot2) replicates. Host inflammatory status and dietary emulsifiers independently and synergistically 281 

shape AIEC evolution 282 Diet profoundly influences the behavior and adaptation of intestinal bacteria, 283 including pathobionts such as AIEC that are linked to chronic inflammatory conditions 22,34,35 .284 To explore the evolutionary impact of chronic dietary emulsifier exposure in the presence or 285 absence of inflammation, we monocolonized wild-type (WT) and Il10 -/- mice with the AIEC 286 reference strain LF82. Since the route of administration does not qualitatively alter the 287 inflammatory outcome of emulsifier in these models, mice were exposed to CMC, P80, or 288 plain water in drinking water over a three month period ( Fig. 1A ) 36,37 . Four AIEC isolates 289 per mouse were randomly collected and analyzed by whole-genome sequencing. 290 Such analysis revealed marked differences in the mutational landscape driven by both 291 host genotype and treatment ( Fig. S1, S2A-B, Table 1 ). In WT mice, a total of 58 mutated 292 genes were identified: 15 in water-evolved isolates, and 15 and 28 in P80- and CMC-evolved 293 isolates respectively. In Il10 -/- mice, mutated genes count was substantially higher, with 90 in 294 water-evolved, 20 in CMC-evolved, and 61 in P80-evolved strains. Moreover, emulsifier 295 exposure influenced not only the frequency but also the spectrum of mutations. Both CMC 296 and P80 reduced the occurrence of deletions per strain relative to the water-evolved group in 297 WT strains, whereas CMC increased the proportion of substitutions in WT strains and P80 298 increased substitution in Il10 -/- isolates (Fig. S2C, D). Emulsifier-driven evolution enhances AIEC pathogenic traits in vitro 331 Having established emulsifier-driven genomic diversification, we next assessed the 332 functional consequences of accumulated mutations. For this purpose, all recovered isolates 333 were systematically tested for key virulence traits: motility, epithelial invasiveness, biofilm 334 formation, and production of major pro-inflammatory molecules ( Fig. 2 ). In WT-derived 335 strains, P80-evolved isolates were notably more invasive in epithelial cell assays while CMC-336 evolved isolates exhibited significantly increased motility compared to water-evolved controls 337 (Fig. 2A, B ). Moreover, both emulsifier-evolved groups also produced higher levels of 338 bioactive LPS, a major trigger of host inflammation 38 (Fig. 2C ). Conversely, in the Il10 -/-339 background, CMC-evolved strains displayed enhanced invasiveness and biofilm formation, 340 but decreased LPS activity relative to water-evolved isolates ( Fig. 2E, G, H ). For P80-341 evolved strains, no consistent virulence enhancement was observed in vitro , suggesting that 342 the strong inflammatory environment overrides emulsifier-induced adaptation in some 343 respects. Overall, except for the selective pressure exerted by dietary emulsifiers, water-344 evolved strains between both genotypes showed largely unaltered virulence phenotypes, with 345 the only notable exception being a modest decrease in biofilm formation in the Il10 ⁻

Discussion 402 Crohn’s disease (CD) exemplifies the complex interplay between genetics, 403 environment, and the intestinal microbiota in the development of chronic inflammatory 404 disorders. While patients display heterogeneous manifestations, a consistent finding is the 405 high prevalence of adherent-invasive Escherichia coli (AIEC) in individuals with CD 406 compared to healthy controls 5,6,40 . Intriguingly, AIEC strains do not belong to a single 407 evolutionary lineage but are scattered across E. coli phylogroups, suggesting their 408 independent and recurrent emergence through adaptation to disease-specific selective 409 pressures. 410 Here, we report that commonly used dietary emulsifiers, carboxymethylcellulose 411 (CMC) and polysorbate 80 (P80), can act as potent evolutionary drivers, accelerating the 412 adaptation of AIEC toward increased pathogenicity, which appears exacerbated in the context 413 of chronic host inflammation. Using mono-colonized murine models, we report that exposure 414 to these food additives, alone or combined with an inflammatory genetical background ( Il10 415 deficiency), promotes pronounced genomic diversification in AIEC, with distinct mutational 416 signatures depending on both the additive and the host context. Although the evolved strains 417 uniformly displayed a competitive advantage in vivo , phenotypes observed in vitro differed 418 markedly between WT and Il10 -/- isolated strains. In WT derived isolates, both CMC and 419 P80 exposure were associated with increased LPS level, classically linked to heightened 420 pro inflammatory potential. In contrast, in the Il10 -/- background, these emulsifiers reduced 421 LPS bioactivity while still promoting biofilm formation, especially CMC. Such observation 422 can be explained by the trade off between immune activation and immune evasion that 423 operates under severe inflammation. In a highly inflamed Il10 -/- gut, bacterial strains that 424 dampen LPS signaling may induce reduced detection by the host innate immune system, 425 thereby gaining a survival advantage despite a lower intrinsic pro inflammatory capacity. Hence, theses data 428 suggest that the selective pressures imposed by a chronic inflammatory environment shape the 429 direction of emulsifier induced evolution, favoring traits that balance immune activation with 430 immune evasion. 431 Our findings highlight that while inflammation alone drives AIEC evolution, it is the 432 chronic presence of emulsifiers that directs this diversification towards more pro-433 inflammatory and colonization-competent phenotypes. Hence, the synergy between modern 434 dietary components and host genetics could underpins the rapid emergence and persistence of 435 AIEC strains in the CD population. This aligns with epidemiological evidence associating 436 increased ultra-processed food consumption with rising rates of IBD 4,41 and provides a 437 plausible mechanism by which rapid cultural changes in diet may translate into new clinical 438 challenges. Although CMC and P80 have been reported to act directly through the intestinal 439 microbiota, other food additives have been reported in vitro to impact the host in a 440 microbiota-independent manner 42,43 . Such host effects could also influence the selection 441 pressures on pathobionts and thereby affect their evolutionary paths. 442 Mechanistically, we observed that specific combinations of mutations, rather than 443 single genetic changes, underlie the heightened flagellin response and virulence observed in 444 food additive-evolved AIEC. In wild-type hosts, mutations in key stress response and cell 445 surface biosynthesis genes such as rfe and rssB were observed, while Il10 -/- backgrounds 446 favored other adaptive trajectories. This context-dependent adaptation underscores the 447 dynamic and tailored nature of pathobiont evolution in response to environmental and 448 inflammatory triggers. Furthermore, several mutations were identified in independent clones, 449 suggesting convergent selection, particularly for clones isolated from WT mice. In addition, this study focused on the genetic and phenotypic adaptation of a single 462 specific AIEC strain. Given prior evidence that E. coli accumulates more mutations in 463 inflammatory environments, and that Crohn’s disease–associated strains can outcompete 464 those derived from healthy donors, further studies are warranted to determine whether food 465 emulsifiers similarly shape the evolutionary trajectories of other AIEC lineages 45 .466 Nevertheless, these results have broad implications, as they suggest that the modern 467 food environment, characterized by widespread additives use, may be driving not only the 468 selection but also the evolutionary acceleration of pro-inflammatory pathobionts such as 469 AIEC. Given that similar additives and processed food constituents are now global dietary 470 staples, it is plausible that comparable mechanisms are at work in the emergence of other 471 microbiota-derived pathobionts implicated in chronic inflammatory diseases beyond CD. 472 Further studies using emulsifier cocktails at dose mimicking real exposure in Crohn’s-disease 473 patients appear needed to determine the contribution of these additives to AIEC evolution and 474 adaptation. 475
