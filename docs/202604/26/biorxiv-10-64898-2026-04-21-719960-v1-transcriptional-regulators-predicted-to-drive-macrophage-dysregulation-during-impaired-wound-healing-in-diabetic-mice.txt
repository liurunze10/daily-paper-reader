Abstract. Dysregulation of Mo/M φ activity is known to contribute to impaired healing in diabetes; however, the mechanisms underlying this dysregulation are not well understood. In this study, we used a variety of bioinformatics approaches along with our time series scRNA-seq data on wound Mo/M φ from non-diabetic and diabetic mice to identify transcriptional regulators (TRs) that drive Mo/M φ state transitions during normal and impaired healing. First, we used the Lamian framework and our newly developed Pseudotime Graph Diffusion method to show that state transitions from early stage phenotypes to later stage reparative and antigen presenting phenotypes characteristic of normally healing wounds are impaired and that transitions to inflammatory, foam cell-like, and Lyve-1 + Mφ phenotypes are enhanced during impaired healing of diabetic mice. Using our BITFAM model, we identified a broad range of TRs predicted to be preferentially active in each cell state and using CellOracle, we performed in silico perturbation to identify groups of TRs predicted to drive cell state transitions along multiple trajectories (e.g. CEBPA, IRF8), whereas other TRs were predicted to drive cell state transition towards reparative phenotypes (e.g. NR1H3, NR3C1) or towards an antigen-presenting phenotype (e.g. IRF4, OGT). Selected findings were validated using existing experimental data, confirming the usefulness of this approach. In conclusion, we identified TRs that likely drive Mo/M φ state transitions towards desirable and undesirable phenotypes for wound healing. These findings provide insight into novel targets for altering Mo/M φ phenotypes to promote healing of diabetic wounds.

1 Introduction 3

Chronic wounds associated with diabetes are an escalating problem around the world [ 1]. People 4

suffering from diabetes incur a 25% lifetime risk of developing chronic wounds, and associated risk 5

of amputation, decreased quality of life, high morbidity and mortality [ 2 –4 ]. Effective wound healing 6

requires coordinated responses of different cell types during overlapping phases of inflammation, 7

proliferation and remodeling [ 5, 6] and chronic wounds are known to exhibit defects in each phase of 8

healing, resulting in a persistent inflammatory state [ 7 , 8]. Monocytes and macrophages (Mo/M φ)9

play essential roles in wound healing [ 9, 10], and dysregulation of Mo/M φ activity contributes to 10 

impaired healing in diabetes [ 11, 12]. However, the mechanisms underlying this dysregulation are 11 

not well understood. 12 

Over the course of wound healing, Mo/M φ adopt a wide spectrum of functional phenotypes 13 

that is best characterized by the resolution afforded by single cell RNA-seq (scRNA-seq) [ 13 – 16 ]. 14 

Transcription factors (TFs) and other transcriptional regulators (TRs), including TF modifiers and 15 

epigenetic regulators, play critical roles in regulating gene expression involved in specifying this 16 

spectrum of Mo/M φ phenotypes [ 17– 20]. We recently reported that, over the course of normal wound 17 

healing, wound Mo/M φ cluster into early-stage Mo/M φ, late-stage M φ and antigen presenting 18 

cells [ 16 ] and that each cluster showed differential chromatin accessibility and differential TR 19 

activity predicted by our BITFAM model [ 21, 22 ]. Network analysis revealed highly connected TR 20 

communities that cooperate to regulate wound Mo/M φ phenotypes. Importantly, we validated a 21 

pro-inflammatory role for NR4A1 during wound healing, showing that Nr4a1 knockout mice exhibit 22 

decreased inflammatory gene expression in early-stage wound Mo/M φ, along with delayed wound 23 

re-epithelialization and impaired granulation tissue formation. Although a number of reports have 24 

characterized dysregulation of wound Mo/M φ phenotypes during impaired diabetic wound healing, 25 

the role of altered TR activity in such dysregulation has not been elucidated. 26 

In the present study, we used the Lamian framework [ 23] and our newly developed Pseudotime 27 

Graph Diffusion method on our time-series scRNA-seq data from skin wound cells of non-diabetic 28 

and diabetic mice to show that wound Mo/M φ exhibit altered state transitions from early to later 29 

stage phenotypes in diabetic versus non-diabetic mice, including impaired transitions to reparative 30 

and antigen-presenting phenotypes and enhanced transitions to inflammatory, foam cell-like, and 31 

Lyve-1 + phenotypes. Using our BITFAM model [ 21 , 22], we identified a broad range of TRs that 32 

are predicted to be preferentially active in each cell state, and those that are dynamically active 33 

over specific ranges of pseudotime. Using CellOracle [ 24], we performed in silico TR perturbation 34 

to predict which TRs drive the various cell state transitions in non-diabetic and diabetic wounds. 35 

Finally, we validated selected findings using existing experimental data, confirming the usefulness of 36 

this approach to identify TRs that drive state transitions in diabetic wounds. 37 

2 Methods and Materials 38 

Animals. C57BL6/J (Non-diabetic, ND) and BKS.Cg-Dock7 m +/+ Lepr db /J (a model of type 2 39 

diabetes, DB) were purchased from the Jackson Laboratory (Bar Harbor, ME) and housed for at 40 

least two weeks before experiments. All animal procedures were approved by the Animal Care and 41 

Use Committee of the University of Illinois Chicago. 42 

Wound model. Adult male mice (age 10-12 weeks) were subjected to excisional skin wounding 43 

with an 8 mm biopsy punch as described [ 16 , 25 ]. Skin wounds were collected using a 12 mm 44 

biopsy punch, then dissociated by mechanical and enzymatic digestion into single cell suspension as 45 

previously described [16, 25]. 46 

scRNA-seq. Wound cells were pooled from 4 wounds of male ND or DB mice each on day 3, 6, 47 

and 10 post-injury. Sorted live CD11b +CD45 +Ly6G − cells were processed for scRNA-seq using 48 

the 10x Chromium Next GEM Single Cell 3’ Reagent Kits (10X Genomics, San Francisco, CA, 49 

USA). Libraries were sequenced on HiSeq Sequencing Systems (Illumina, San Diego, CA, USA) 50 

with paired-end reads aiming for 100,000 reads/cell [25]. 51

scRNA-seq data processing. Raw count matrices were processed using Seurat v5 [ 26] with default 57 

parameters unless otherwise noted. Expression data were log-normalized, and the top 2,000 highly 58 

variable genes (HVGs) were selected. UMAP embeddings were computed from the first 30 principal 59 

components. Cluster-specific marker genes were identified using a one-versus-rest framework, and 60 

gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) was performed against the MSigDB Hallmark collection using 61 

GSEApy [ 27 ] with preranked analysis (1,000 permutations), ranking genes by sign(log 2 fold change) 62 

× − log 10 (adjusted P-value). 63 

Trajectory inference and pseudotime analysis. Lamian [ 23] was used for trajectory inference 64 

and differential pseudotime analysis. Cells were clustered in PCA space via k-means ( k = 12, selected 65 

by elbow method), and a minimum spanning tree was constructed over cluster centroids, rooted 66 

at day 3 cells. Branch robustness was assessed by permutation (1,000 permutations). Pseudotime-67 

dependent gene expression was identified using Lamian 's time-dependent expression (TDE) test, 68 

applied branch-wise to the top 5,000 HVGs per branch. Significant genes (FDR < 0.05) were 69 

classified as early, middle, or late based on peak expression timing (top 1%, middle, or bottom 70 

1% of branch pseudotime, respectively) and subjected to pathway enrichment against the MSigDB 71 

Hallmark collection. 72 

Enhanced trajectory visualization. Low-dimensional embeddings such as UMAP may not 73 

faithfully preserve trajectory structures supported in higher-dimensional PCA space. To address 74 

this issue, we applied Pseudotime Graph Diffusion (PGD) [ 28], a method we developed that smooths 75 

cell-level embeddings along a pseudotime graph via Markovian diffusion. Seurat PCA embeddings 76 

were diffused along the Lamian-inferred pseudotime graph ( α = 0.6, with self-loops) and subsequently 77 

projected to UMAP for improved trajectory visualization. 78 

Transcriptional regulator activity inference. TR activities were inferred using BITFAM [ 22 ], a 79 

Bayesian factor analysis model that incorporates a gene regulatory network (GRN) as prior knowledge. 80 

The GRN used in this study was derived from ChIP-Atlas [ 29] TR–target gene associations ( ±581 

kb; experiments annotated as “dendritic”, “macrophage”, or “monocyte”; downloaded May 14, 82 

2025). Target genes of Zbtb46 were manually added given its established role in dendritic cell 83 

differentiation [ 30 ]. The GRN was pruned to exclude TRs detected in fewer than 100 cells (-3 TRs) 84 

or with fewer than 10 target genes (-6 TRs) among the top 2,000 HVGs. BITFAM was run four 85 

times for robustness; nominal P-values from one-versus-rest comparisons were combined across runs 86 

using a signed Stouffer Z -score method with Benjamini–Hochberg FDR correction. Per-cell TR 87 

activity was summarized as the median across runs. 88 

In silico transcriptional regulator perturbation. CellOracle [ 24] was used to simulate TR 89 

knockout (expression set to zero) and overexpression (expression set to the safe-range upper bound, 90 

calculated as the observed maximum plus the full expression range (max + (max − min)) of 91 

single-cell expression values for each gene). Perturbation scores, computed as the inner product of 92 

simulated perturbation vectors with pseudotime-derived developmental vectors, were summarized per 93 

branch by separately summing positive (promoting transitions) and negative (inhibiting transitions) 94 

scores. Markov random walk simulations (500 steps) were additionally performed to quantify 95 

perturbation-induced changes in cluster occupancy, reported as percent change relative to initial 96

3 Results 98 

Mo/M φ cell states are altered in diabetic wounds 99 

We recently reported that networks of TR guide wound Mo/M φ through state transitions from 100 

pro-inflammatory to pro-healing phenotypes as normal healing progresses in ND mice [ 16 ]. In the 101 

present study, we used the Lamian computational framework [ 23] to identify dysregulation of state 102 

transitions of wound Mo/M φ in DB mice (Fig. 1). We also developed and applied Pseudotime Graph 103 

Diffusion (PGD), a post hoc technique that enhances the visualization of pseudotemporal trajectory 104 

structure in low-dimensional representations through graph-based diffusion without altering the 105 

underlying data, facilitating downstream analyses. Next, we used our BITFAM model to predict 106 

TR activity that may contribute to dysregulation of state transitions [ 21, 22]. Finally, we used 107 

CellOracle to identify the impact of in silico perturbation of TR expression and validated the 108 

predicted effects of TR knockout using prior data from our laboratory and others [24]. 109

Next, we used Lamian [ 23 ] to infer a pseudotemporal trajectory followed by PGD to enhance its 141 

visualization. Two trajectories were identified with high confidence (Fig. 3a,b): the first branch 142 

started in cluster 12, then transitioned through clusters 6 and 7, and terminated in cluster 5 (branch 143 

1: 12-6-7-5). The second branch started in cluster 12, and transitioned through clusters 6 and 9 144 

before terminating in clusters 3 and 10 (branch 2: 12-6-9-3-10). A lower confidence branch connected 145 

cluster 12 to clusters 6, 9 and 3 and then to clusters 4 and 8 (branch 3: 12-6-9-3-4-8). 146 

Each branch showed dynamic expression of genes with subsets showing preferential expression 147 

during early, middle and late pseudotimes (Fig. 3c,d,e see Supplementary Data S3 for full list of 148 

dynamically expressed genes along each branch). Gene set enrichment analysis showed preferential 149 

enrichment of the interferon α and γ pathways at early pseudotimes for all trajectory branches (Fig 150 

S1). Branch 1 showed 1,659 dynamically expressed genes (FDR < 0.05), with a relatively rapid 151 

switch from early to late phenotypes over pseudotime, and preferential enrichment of the IL2/STAT5 152 

pathway at late pseudotimes, perhaps indicating the importance of STAT5 in state transitions or 153 

functions of APC phenotype cells in wounds [ 33, 34]. Branch 2 showed 1,063 dynamically expressed 154 

genes, with a more gradual switch from early to late phenotypes. Branch 2 showed preferential 155 

enrichment of epithelial-mesenchymal transition and angiogenesis at intermediate pseudotimes, 156 

reflecting the reparative phenotype of cells that populated these pseudotimes, but no pathways were 157 

significantly enriched at late pseudotimes, perhaps reflecting the mixed phenotype of the DB cells 158 

that populated late pseudotimes. Finally, branch 3, identified with less confidence, showed 1,704 159 

dynamically expressed genes with a large number of these expressed at late pseudotimes. Branch 3 160 

showed preferential enrichment of adipogenesis, fatty acid metabolism, and xenobiotic metabolism 161 

pathways at late pseudotimes, highlighting the altered metabolism of the DB cells that populated 162 

late pseudotimes of this branch. 163

TRs show differential inferred activity between Mo/M φ cell states 183 

Next, we used our BITFAM model to infer the activity of TRs that potentially drive the observed 184 

dysregulation in Mo/M φ cell states in diabetic wounds. Distinct groups of TRs showed differential 185 

inferred activity in different Mo/M φ clusters (Fig. 4a, Supplementary Data S4). Interestingly, levels 186 

of inferred TR activity generally did not correlate well with levels of mRNA expression (Fig. 4b, 187 

Supplementary Data S5). These findings corroborate those our previous study [ 21], that mRNA 188 

expression is necessary but not sufficient for high inferred TR activity predicted by BITFAM. 189 

Cluster 12, which expressed an early-stage interferon activation signature, showed preferential inferred 190 

activity of IRF9 and BRD3, which both promote the transcription of inflammatory cytokines, and 191 

promote the type I interferon response [ 35, 36] (Figure 4a-d). Cluster 3, which expressed a late-192 

stage reparative signature, showed preferential inferred activity of HES1 and ZMYND11. HES1 193 

is a transcriptional repressor that downregulates the expression of inflammatory genes [ 37] and 194 

ZMYND11 is a lesser known TF that may negatively regulate the NFkB pathway [ 38]; thus, both 195 

may contribute to the transition of pro-inflammatory to reparative M φ phenotypes. Cluster 5, which 196 

expressed an APC-like signature, showed preferential inferred activity of ZBTB46 and STAT5B. 197 

Interestingly, ZBTB46 is not thought to be expressed in Mo/M φ, but is important for maintaining 198 

a dendritic cell identity [ 39 ]; Zbtb46 was highly expressed only in cluster 5 cells. STAT5B may 199 

promote a tolerogenic state of dendritic cells, leading to enhanced T regulatory cell activity [34]. 200 

Cluster 10, which expressed a mixed signature, exhibited preferential inferred activity of NFE2L2 and 201 

PHF10 (Fig. 4a, S3). NFE2L2, or nuclear factor erythroid 2–related factor 2, is a master regulator 202 

for protection against oxidative stress, but also can downregulate pro-inflammatory cytokines [ 40 , 41 ]. 203 

PHF10 may be important in the development of myeloid progenitors in bone marrow [ 42 ] and is 204 

part of a chromatin remodeling complex that is important for activation of inflammatory genes [ 43 ]. 205 

Cluster 1, which expressed a lipid handling, foam cell-like phenotype, showed preferential inferred 206 

activity of NR1H3 and EZH2. NR1H3 encodes Liver X Receptor alpha, which is important for 207 

lipid metabolism and inflammatory gene regulation [ 44]. EZH2 is an epigenetic regulator that can 208 

repress either pro- or anti-inflammatory genes through histone methylation [ 45]. Clusters 4, 7, 8, 209 

which expressed mixed inflammatory phenotypes, showed preferential inferred activity of BRD2 210 

and NR3C1. BRD2, like its cousin BRD3, plays a key role in chromatin remodeling and promotes 211 

expression of inflammatory genes [ 46] and NR3C1 encodes the glucocorticoid receptor, which is 212 

a potent suppressor of inflammatory gene expression [ 47]. Thus, in these clusters, inferred TR 213 

activity predicts mixed and perhaps conflicting pro- and anti-inflammatory functions in DB wound 214 

Mo/M φ throughout the time course examined. Finally, we identified TR with preferential inferred 215 

activity during early, middle and late pseudotimes (Fig. 4e-g, Supplementary Data S6), highlighting 216 

additional potential regulators of Mo/M φ cell state transitions in wounds. 217

4 Discussion 303 

Although dysregulation of Mo/M φ activity is known to contribute to impaired healing in diabetes 304 

[11 , 12 ], the mechanisms underlying this dysregulation are not well understood. In the present 305 

study, we used the Lamian computational framework [ 23] and our newly developed Pseudotime 306 

Graph Diffusion method on our scRNA-seq data to comprehensively identify alterations in Mo/M φ307 

phenotypes over the course of healing, and show that state transitions from early stage inflammatory 308 

phenotypes to later stage reparative and APC phenotypes are impaired and that transitions to 309 

inflammatory, foam cell-like, and Lyve-1 + phenotypes are enhanced in wounds of diabetic mice. Using 310 

our BITFAM model [ 21, 22], we also identified a broad range of TRs predicted to be preferentially 311 

active in each cell state. We used CellOracle [ 24] to perform in silico TR perturbation to predict 312 

which TRs drive cell state transitions in non-diabetic and diabetic wounds; certain TRs were 313 

predicted to drive cell state transitions along multiple trajectories (e.g. CEBPA, IRF8), whereas 314 

other TRs were predicted to drive cell state transition towards reparative phenotypes (e.g. NR1H3, 315 

NR3C1) and or towards an antigen-presenting phenotype (e.g. IRF4, OGT). Finally, we validated 316 

selected findings using existing experimental data, confirming the usefulness of this approach for 317 

identifying TRs that drive state transitions in both non-diabetic and diabetic wounds. 318 

Other groups have recently published findings on the heterogeneity of wound Mo/M φ in both 319 

diabetic mice and humans. Using the streptozotocin model of type 1 diabetes and scRNA-seq [ 51 ], 320 

six Mo/M φ subsets and two dendritic cell subsets were observed in skin swounds, with varying 321 

contributions of ND and DB cells to each cluster over the time points observed (one to seven days 322 

post-five mm excisional wounding). Although a list of TFs that were expressed in each cluster 323 

was provided, TF activity was not assessed, and findings from the present study indicate that 324 

TF expression levels do not correlate well with predicted TF activity. Another study profiled 325 

human wound cells during normal skin wound healing (one to 30 days post four mm excisional 326 

wounding) [ 52], and found that, similar to what occurs in rodent models, Mo/M φ progress from 327 

pro-inflammatory to pro-resolution phenotypes as healing progresses; however the role of TFs in this 328 

switch was not a focus of the study. Another study profiled wound cells from human diabetic foot 329

A number of recent studies have demonstrated that epigenetic gene regulation via histone modifica-336 

tions, DNA modifications, and microRNA can influence macrophage state transitions during wound 337 

healing [ 12 , 53]. Because epigenetic regulators are included in the ChIP-Atlas database, these were 338 

present in both our BITFAM and CellOracle analyses, and our findings highlight the importance 339 

of these regulators in Mo/M φ state transitions during wound healing. For example, the histone 340 

methylase KMT2A, or MLL1, adds methyl groups to histone H3 at K4, thereby promoting chromatin 341 

opening and subsequent gene expression. We found that KMT2A was predicted to be highly active 342 

in the early-stage pro-inflammatory cluster 12 and to a lesser extent in intermediate-stage clusters 6 343 

and 7, and that in silico knockout of Kmt2a was predicted to reduce cells in cluster 12, as well as 344 

terminal clusters 3 and 5, and increase cells in cluster 6 and to a lesser extent cluster 1, the latter 345 

expressing the DB-associated lipid handling phenotype that was less inflammatory than cluster 346 

12. Consistent with these findings, Mo/M φ specific Mll1 knockout mice exhibited reduced wound 347 

macrophage inflammatory cytokine production and delayed wound healing [54]. 348 

Additional epigenetic regulators predicted to be important in specifying Mo/M φ states, which 349 

have been less well-studied during wound healing, include Bromodomain-containing protein (BRD) 350 

proteins, which are epigenetic readers considered to be master regulators of gene expression, O-351 

GlcNAc transferase (OGT), which adds O-GlcNAc to TFs and other proteins, generally suppressing 352 

pro-inflammatory responses, and Zinc Finger MYND-Type Containing 11 (ZMYND11), which acts 353 

as a transcriptional corepressor of inflammatory genes that are marked with the histone modification 354 

H3.3K36me3. These results highlight the wide variety of factors that contribute to specifying wound 355 

Mo/M φ, many of which have not been studied in the context of impaired wound healing with 356 

diabetes. 357 

A limitation of our study is that, although our results provide unbiased and comprehensive insight 358 

into the regulation of Mo/M φ cell states in normal and impaired wound healing, how the TRs 359 

identified as being important in specifying Mo/M φ cell states are themselves regulated awaits 360 

further study. Another limitation of our study includes inability to differentiate between resident 361 

and infiltrating cells that contribute to the wound Mo/M φ pool. The question of whether resident 362 

and infiltrating Mo/M φ exhibit different phenotypes in ND and DB wounds, and whether different 363 

regulatory mechanisms are involved in cells from different sources will be a topic of future study. 364 

Other limitations of this study relate to the computational analyses. First, our trajectory inference 365 

relied on a single method (Lamian), which constructs a minimum spanning tree across both ND 366 

and DB cells jointly. This tree-based topology assumes linear progression from an initial cluster 367 

to a limited number of terminal clusters, precluding more complex dynamics such as phenotypic 368 

reversion or cyclical state transitions. Moreover, although every cluster contains cells from both 369 

conditions, the joint construction may suggest transitions that do not occur within either single 370 

condition. The trajectory modeled here therefore represents possible, but not exhaustive, cell state 371 

transitions agnostic to condition. In addition, our CellOracle perturbation analyses lack formal 372 

statistical testing; perturbation scores should be interpreted as computational predictions that 373 

prioritize candidates for experimental validation rather than as statistically confirmed effects. 374
