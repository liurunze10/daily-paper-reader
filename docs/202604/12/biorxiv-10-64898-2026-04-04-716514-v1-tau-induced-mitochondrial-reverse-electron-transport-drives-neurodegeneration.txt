Abstract 1

2

Hyperphosphorylation and aggregation of the microtubule-associated protein tau are 3

recognized as pathological hallmarks of tauopathies; however, the biological activity of tau 4

that drives its pathophysiological effects remains poorly understood 1-6 . Mitochondrial 5

dysfunction is a common feature of tauopathies 7,8 . Despite this, the mechanistic link between 6

tau abnormalities and mitochondrial dysfunction, as well as its relationship to tau’s 7

physiological function, remains unclear. Here, we demonstrate that tau regulates 8

mitochondrial reverse electron transport (RET), which produces excess ROS, reduces the 9

NAD +/NADH ratio, and is activated by aging or stress. In flies, mice, and human induced 10 

pluripotent stem cells (hiPSC)-derived neurons, tau depletion eliminates stress-induced RET 11 

and confers significant stress resistance. Mechanistically, tau enters mitochondria and 12 

directly interacts with the mitochondrial complex I (C-I) subunit NDUFS3, enhancing RET 13 

activation in a phosphorylation-dependent manner that correlates with tau pathogenicity. 14 

Elevated RET further drives tau hyperphosphorylation, establishing a self-perpetuating 15 

pathological loop. Blocking tau entry into mitochondria or disrupting tau/NDUFS3 16 

interaction reduces tau-induced RET. Genetic or pharmacological inhibition of RET protects 17 

against tau-induced neurodegeneration across species. RET regulation represents a18 

previously unrecognized normal function of tau that becomes pathological in disease, 19 

providing a therapeutic target for conditions characterized by tau abnormalities and 20 

mitochondrial dysfunction. 21

Main 1

Many neurodegenerative diseases share overlapping neuropathological and clinical features. If this 2

overlap reflects a shared pathogenic mechanism, targeting it therapeutically could potentially 3

address multiple diseases. Hyperphosphorylation and aggregation of tau are pathological 4

hallmarks of primary tauopathies, such as frontotemporal dementia (FTD) and progressive 5

supranuclear palsy (PSP), as well as secondary tauopathies, which include common 6

neurodegenerative diseases like Alzheimer's disease (AD) and possibly Huntington's disease and 7

Parkinson's disease 1-6 . Tau abnormalities are also observed in experimental models of autism 9,8

epilepsy 10 , stroke 11 , and traumatic brain injury 12 , where tau reduction has shown beneficial effects 9

– even in the absence of neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs), the prominent intraneuronal tau aggregates 10 

characteristic of AD and other tauopathies 1-3 . This suggests that non-aggregated soluble tau species 11 

may mediate the pathological effects of tau. Tau is normally a soluble protein enriched in neuronal 12 

axons but is also found in neuronal dendrites, cell bodies 13 , and non-neuronal cells 14,15 . Tau is 13 

phosphorylated at many sites under normal conditions but becomes hyperphosphorylated in 14 

disease, suggesting that phosphorylation regulates its pathophysiological activities 15,16 . Genetic 15 

mutations in tau causing FTD with Parkinsonism linked to chromosome 17 17-20 , the relationship 16 

between tau pathology and brain dysfunction in AD 21 , and the beneficial effects of tau reduction 17 

observed in experimental AD models 22,23 collectively provide strong evidence that tau 18 

abnormalities are pathogenic. 19 

20 

Tau was originally identified as a protein essential for microtubule (MT) assembly 24 , and disease-21 

associated mutations or post-translational modifications (PTMs) were thought to impair this 22

Mitochondrial dysfunction is a prominent feature of tauopathies and other conditions associated 9

with tau abnormalities 7,8 . Tau interacts with mitochondrial proteins, and recent studies in hiPSC-10 

derived neurons suggest that the mitochondrial interactome of tau is extensive 34 . Overexpression 11 

studies in cell culture and animal models have linked pathogenic tau to disruptions in various 12 

mitochondrial processes 35-39 . However, an unresolved question remains: do these effects reflect 13 

tau’s normal physiological function 15 ? Addressing this question is essential for effectively 14 

targeting tau in therapeutic development, as a deeper understanding of its normal function in 15 

relation to its pathological effects is critical. 16 

17 

During oxidative phosphorylation (OxPhos), electrons enter the electron transport chain (ETC) 18 

from C-I in the form of NADH. These electrons are transferred through carriers such as co-enzyme 19 

Q10 (CoQ) and cytochrome C, eventually reaching complex IV, where they react with O 2 to form 20 

H2O. This forward electron transport (FET) process generates a proton gradient needed for ATP 21 

synthesis, converts NADH to NAD +, and produces small amounts of reactive oxygen species (ROS) 22

as a byproduct 40 . In contrast, reverse electron transport (RET) generates large amounts of ROS and 1

converts NAD + back to NADH, thereby reducing the mitochondrial and cellular NAD +/NADH 2

ratio 41-43 . RET occurs under conditions of high CoQH 2 levels or elevated mitochondrial membrane 3

potential (Δp), which provide the energy required to drive electrons backward against the redox 4

potential gradient of the ETC, moving them in reverse from CoQH 44 . RET has been implicated in 5

various disease states 42,43,45,46 , but its normal physiological role and regulatory mechanisms in 6

health and disease remain poorly understood. Using in vivo models in Drosophila and mice, as 7

well as hiPSC-derived neuronal systems, we have uncovered a conserved role of tau as a regulator 8

of RET. This discovery provides new insights into tau’s physiological function and its relevance 9

to tauopathies. 10

The hyperactivation of RET observed in tauopathy may reflect either a toxic gain-of-function by 1

pathogenic tau, unrelated to its normal role, or a physiological function of tau that becomes 2

deregulated in tauopathy. To distinguish between these possibilities, we examined tau knockout 3

(KO) and knockdown (KD) conditions. In hiPSC-derived neurons with tau KD by lentiviral 4

delivery of shRNA, tau protein levels were significantly reduced (Extended Data Fig. 2i), and these 5

neurons displayed normal ROS and NAD +/NADH ratio compared to control neurons (Fig. 1k,l). 6

In contrast, mitochondrial RET activity was significantly impaired in tau KD neurons (Fig. 1k,l), 7

while FET activity remained unaffected (Extended Data Fig. 2j,k). This effect was further 8

confirmed by treating neurons with diethyl-succinate (DES), a substrate known to promote RET 9

in mammalian cells 59 and flies 43 . Control neurons treated with DES exhibited increased ROS 10 

production, whereas tau KD neurons failed to generate ROS under the same conditions (Fig. 1m). 11 

12 

Similarly, brain tissues from a well-characterized tau KO mouse model 60 showed normal ROS 13 

levels and the NAD +/NADH ratio (Fig. 1k,l), However, when purified brain mitochondria were 14 

analyzed, RET activity was reduced while FET activity remained unchanged (Fig. 1k,l; Extended 15 

Data Fig. 2j,k). Comparable findings were observed in tau KO flies 61 (Fig. 1k,l; Extended Data 16 

Fig. 2j,k). These results indicate that tau-deficient mitochondria have compromised RET activity, 17 

which becomes evident when mitochondria are fueled by RET-specific substrates in vitro . Under 18 

normal conditions, mitochondria predominantly rely on FET, with RET contributing minimally to 19 

cellular ROS production and NAD +/NADH ratio. This explains the relatively unchanged ROS 20 

levels and NAD +/NADH ratio in tau KO brain tissues or tau KD hiPSC-derived neurons. Taken 21 

together, these findings demonstrate a conserved physiological role for tau in regulating RET 22 

activity across species. 23

Tau actively promotes RET in a phosphorylation-dependent manner 2

The mechanism by which tau regulates RET could be either direct or indirect. To explore this, we 3

treated mitochondria isolated from 6-month-old WT mouse (C57/BL6) muscle with soluble 4

recombinant human tau (2N4R form), pretreated with glycogen synthase kinase 3 b (GSK-3 b)5

under conditions conducive to protein import. This recombinant tau was recognized by the PHF-1 6

p-tau antibody (p-S396/S404), but not by antibodies specific for other phosphorylation sites, 7

including p-S262, AT-100 (p-T212/S214), or CP13 (p-S202) (Extended Data Fig. 3a). These results 8

are consistent with GSK-3 b being a kinase generating the PHF-1 epitope 62 . Notably, while a non-9

phosphorylatable tau mutant (tau-S404A) failed to induce RET, an equivalent amount of GSK-3 b10 

treated tau-WT robustly promoted RET-ROS production (Fig. 2a). Since phosphorylation at S404 11 

primes tau for phosphorylation at S396 63 , tau-S404A is likely deficient in S936 phosphorylation. 12 

These findings suggest that tau promotes RET in a PHF-1 site-dependent manner. 13 

14

resembling that of the inner mitochondrial membrane protein NDUFS3 - while the outer membrane 19 

protein Tom20 was completely digested (Fig. 2c). In contrast, p-tau was sensitive to PK digestion 20 

when not mixed with mitochondria (Fig. 2d), confirming that its resistance to PK digestion was 21 

due to mitochondrial import rather than intrinsic properties of the protein. Furthermore, after 22

Tau directly interacts with NDUFS3 and alters its RET-related interactions within C-I 2

To understand the molecular mechanisms by which tau promotes RET, we made several 3

predictions: 1) tau/p-tau should be present inside mitochondria; 2) tau/p-tau may directly interact 4

with C-I proteins; and 3) tau/p-tau may induce conformational changes in C-I that influence RET. 5

We tested these predictions, focusing on PHF-1 tau, which is important for RET. 6

7

First, we analyzed mitochondrial fractions and subfractions from rTg4510 mouse brains. We 8

observed p-tau inside mitochondria, with a distribution pattern encompassing the inner 9

mitochondrial membrane (IMM) where the C-I marker NDUFS3 is localized (Fig. 3a,b). p-tau was 10 

also present in mitochondria purified from normal mouse brains, with levels increasing with age 11

Inhibiting the mitochondrial entry of tau diminishes its RET-promoting effect 2

To further substantiate the intramitochondrial action of tau in promoting RET, we explored ways 3

to inhibit its mitochondrial entry. Although tau has been reported to localize to mitochondria and 4

interact with various mitochondrial proteins, the mechanism by which it enters mitochondria 5

remains unknown. Proteins such as voltage-dependent anion channel (VDAC1) and molecular 6

chaperones Hsp70 and Hsp90 - known to facilitate mitochondrial protein import 73, 74 - have been 7

shown to interact with tau 75-77 . We found that mitochondria purified from HEK293 cells treated 8

recombinant PHF-1 tau and exhibited lower RET activity than control mitochondria after treatment 10 

with PHF-1 tau (Extended Data Fig. 9a,b), despite similar amounts of total PHF-1 tau in the 11 

reaction and unaltered basal RET activity of inhibitor-treated mitochondria (i.e., without PHF1 tau 12 

import) (Extended Data Fig. 9c,d). These findings demonstrate that PHF-1 tau needs to enter 13 

mitochondria to promote RET. 14

Discussion 1

Despite significant efforts to develop treatments for neurodegenerative diseases, therapeutic 2

success has been limited, largely due to an incomplete understanding of underlying disease 3

mechanisms. In tauopathies, while considerable progress has been made in elucidating tau’s role 4

in microtubule-related function and dysfunction, the normal physiological function of tau in the 5

adult brain - particularly in relation to disease - remains uncertain. This knowledge gap has 6

hindered efforts to uncover the core mechanisms of tau-induced neurotoxicity and develop 7

effective therapies. Here, we identify a previously unrecognized physiological function of tau as a 8

regulator of RET through its direct interaction with the mitochondrial C-I protein NDUFS3. 9

Several lines of evidence strongly support tau’s intramitochondrial role in promoting RET: 10 

blocking tau’s mitochondrial entry diminishes RET activation, and mutations in NDUFS3 that 11 

disrupt tau interaction reduce RET activity. Using AlphaFold-based structural predictions 12 

combined with experimental validation, we provide mechanistic insights into tau’s interaction with 13 

C-I and regulation of RET. Furthermore, RET activation is shown to be dependent on p-tau -14 

widely implicated in tau pathogenesis, and is deregulated across tauopathy models, human iPSC-15 

derived neurons, and patient brain samples. Crucially, therapeutic inhibition of RET mitigates tau-16 

induced neurotoxicity in multiple models, without observable detrimental effects on normal 17 

animals. These findings suggest that RET inhibition holds promise as a therapeutic strategy not 18 

only for tauopathies but potentially for other brain diseases characterized by aberrant tau 19 

phosphorylation and mitochondrial dysfunction. 20

A key finding of this study is the identification of a feedforward loop between RET and tau 1

phosphorylation (Extended Data Fig. 20 ). While phospho-tau promotes RET activation, RET-2

induced changes in ROS and NAD +/NADH ratios further enhance tau phosphorylation, creating a 3

signaling. These insights begin to unravel the molecular mechanisms underlying RET-induced tau 7

hyperphosphorylation and highlight site-specific effects of tau phosphorylation on RET. For 8

example, phosphorylation at the PHF-1 site is particularly critical for tau’s interaction with 9

NDUFS3 and RET promotion, while mutations at the AT8 (S202) site have little impact on RET. 10

Our findings demonstrate that RET inhibition provides broad therapeutic benefits in tauopathy 12 

models. The RET inhibitor CPT effectively disrupts the pathological loop between RET and tau 13 

phosphorylation, ameliorating neurotoxicity across species. In fly and mouse models of tauopathy, 14 

CPT treatment rescues behavioral deficits, reduces neuroinflammation, and mitigates 15 

neurodegeneration. In hiPSC-derived neurons carrying pathogenic tau mutations, CPT protects 16 

against stress-induced cellular abnormalities, including ROS overproduction, mitochondrial 17 

dysfunction, synaptic deficits, and impaired mitophagy. Notably, RET inhibition reduces p-tau 18 

levels without affecting total tau, suggesting that targeting RET may avoid potential adverse effects 19 

associated with complete tau depletion. 20

The therapeutic potential of RET inhibition extends beyond tauopathies. Mitochondrial 1

dysfunction is a hallmark of aging and age-related diseases 91 , and RET is emerging as a driver of 2

aging and aging-related pathology 43 . Our findings resonate with the geroscience hypothesis, which 3

posits that targeting aging mechanisms may prevent or mitigate multiple age-related diseases 92,93 .4

Evidence supporting this hypothesis includes the association of C-I subunit abnormalities 5

(including NDUFS3) with longevity and neuroprotection in experimental models 94, 95 ; lifespan 6

extension by partial knockdown of NDUFS3 and select C-I subunits in worms 96 and flies 97 ;7

neuroprotection by mild inhibition of C-I in mouse AD models 98 ; and association of NDUFS3 with 8

late onset AD in human genome-wide association studies, with NDUFS3 RNAi being protective 9

against AD related toxicity 99 . These observations suggest that mild inhibition of NDUFS3 may 10 

confer therapeutic benefits by inhibiting pathological RET without compromising essential 11 

mitochondrial functions. 12

transition in disease. Under normal conditions, tau may act through RET to mediate stress 15 

adaptation, particularly during aging. However, persistent stress stimuli or aging-related changes 16 

can lead to supraphysiological levels of RET and p-tau, triggering a vicious cycle that drives 17 

neurotoxicity (Extended Data Fig. 20 ). The observation that RET and tau phosphorylation are 18 

upregulated during aging and stress suggests that tauopathies may arise from the deregulation of 19 

normal physiological processes. Our findings also suggest that therapeutic strategies targeting tau 20 

phosphorylation must account for site-specific effects. Not all phospho-tau sites contribute equally 21 

to RET regulation or tau toxicity, as evidenced by the differential effects of mutations at the PHF-22

The identification of tau as a modulator of RET has broader implications for neurodegenerative 4

diseases. Aberrant tau phosphorylation and mitochondrial dysfunction co-occur in numerous brain 5

disorders, including AD, frontotemporal dementia (FTD), and Parkinson’s disease. Our results 6

suggest that RET may serve as a common pathogenic mechanism linking tau abnormalities to 7

mitochondrial dysfunction across diseases. For example, in AD models, endogenous tau acts 8

downstream of APP to activate RET, and RET inhibition mitigates APP-related neurotoxicity. This 9

highlights the potential of RET-targeted therapies to address both primary tauopathies and 10 

secondary tauopathies associated with other disease contexts. 11 

12 

Future studies should explore whether other FTD-related tau mutations and disease-associated tau 13 

variants similarly promote RET. Additionally, the cell-type specificity of RET and tau 14 

phosphorylation in different neurodegenerative diseases remains an open question. It is possible 15 

that certain cell types are more vulnerable to RET-induced changes in ROS and NAD +/NADH 16 

metabolism, or that disease-specific factors selectively upregulate RET and phospho-tau. 17 

Addressing these questions will deepen our understanding of tau’s role in disease and refine 18 

therapeutic strategies. 19

for 20 sec before being removed. If a mouse failed to locate the platform within 60 sec, it was 21 

gently guided to the platform and allowed to sit on the platform for 20 sec before being removed. 22

Fear conditioning test 8

This test was conducted in a sound attenuated chamber with a metal grid floor capable of delivering 9

an electric shock, and freezing was measured with an overhead camera and Anymaze software 10

(CS) and was presented for 30 sec. During the final 2 sec of this noise, mice received a mild foot 13 

shock (0.5mA), which served as the unconditioned stimulus (US). After 1 minute, another CS-US 14 

pair was presented. The mouse was removed 30 sec after the second CS-US pair and returned to 15 

its home cage. Twenty-four hours later, each mouse was returned to the test chamber, and freezing 16 

behavior was recorded for 5 min (context test). Percent of time freezing was calculated as test 17 

freezing time/5 min – baseline freezing time/ 2 min. 18

Extended Data Fig. 20 | Schematic diagram summarizing the main findings of this study. 18 

Stress stimuli like heat stress will activate RET. This may serve some adaptive function initially, 19 

but chronic stress will cause aberrant RET activation, leading to tau hyperphosphorylation. p-tau 20 

further promotes RET, forming a vicious cycle that causes cognitive deficits. In tau KO animals, 21 

this feedback loop is broken, and the animals are spared from stress induced behavioral deficits. 22
