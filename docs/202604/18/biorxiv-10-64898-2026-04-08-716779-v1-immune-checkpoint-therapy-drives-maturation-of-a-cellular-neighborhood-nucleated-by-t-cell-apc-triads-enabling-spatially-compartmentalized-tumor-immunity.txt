Summary 24 

Spatially organized immune hubs of T cells and antigen-presenting cells (APCs) have been linked 25 

to immune checkpoint therapy (ICT) efficacy, yet the mechanisms underlying their function 26 

remain unclear. Using CODEX multiplex imaging, we longitudinally characterized the dynamic 27 

evolution of intratumoral cellular neighborhoods (CN) defined by “triad” interactions of CD4 ⁺ and 28 

CD8 ⁺ T cells with two distinct myeloid APC populations: cDC1s and IFN γ–activated 29 

macrophages. We termed this CN the immunity-promoting CN (IP-CN) and tracked its progressive 30 

development during tumor rejection induced by α-CTLA-4/ α-PD-1 therapy. A coordinated IFN γ31 

and TNF α signaling signature accompanied the IP-CN assembly. Over time, the IP-CN underwent 32 

functional maturation, forming specialized sub-neighborhoods that compartmentalized 33 

proliferating T cells at the tumor periphery versus cytotoxic T effector cells interacting with tumor 34 

cell targets. Our findings reveal a spatiotemporal mechanism by which the IP-CN sustains and 35 

amplifies cytotoxic T cell responses, demonstrating how T cell–APC neighborhoods orchestrate 36 

tumor immunity. 37

INTRODUCTION 39 

Advancements in our understanding of the mechanisms employed by the immune system 40 

to control tumor outgrowth have led to groundbreaking discoveries in cancer immunotherapy 1, 2, 3, 

41  

> 4, 5

. This progress is particularly underscored by the clinical success of Immune-Checkpoint Cancer 42 

Therapy (ICT) targeting the Cytotoxic T-lymphocyte Associated Antigen-4 (CTLA-4) and 43 

Programmed Death-1 (PD-1) immune checkpoint pathways, which have revolutionized cancer 44 

treatment, sometimes providing durable responses in patients with different types of cancer 6, 7, 8 .45 

However, a significant fraction of tumor-bearing individuals fail to respond to therapy 9 and thus, 46 

a deeper understanding of the mechanisms underlying successful cancer immunotherapy is 47 

critically needed. 48 

Previous work in our lab has shed light on this issue by defining mechanisms of successful 49 

tumor rejection 10, 11, 12 . We demonstrated that optimal immune responses to developing and 50 

established tumors required that (a) tumor cells express antigens for both CD4 + and CD8 + T cells; 51 

(b) the host was able to induce tumor-specific CD4 + and CD8 + T cells; and (c) both requirements 52 

were met even in tumors that lacked the expression of MHC-II proteins 13 . Furthermore, using 53 

CyTOF and scRNAseq analysis of tumor-infiltrating CD45 + cells, we showed that successful ICT 54 

induced a profound remodeling of the lymphoid compartment, which was marked by CD4 + and 55 

CD8 + T cell activation and proliferative responses 14 . This work also revealed that the 56 

myeloid/macrophage compartment also concomitantly underwent major remodeling such that 57 

intratumoral CX3CR1 + macrophages expanded during tumor progression, but rapidly disappeared 58 

following ICT, whereas IFN γ-induced iNOS + macrophages increased during tumor rejection 14 .59 

Recent studies highlight the critical role of immune cell spatial organization in determining 60 

outcomes following immunotherapy 15, 16, 17, 18 . Most notably, tertiary lymphoid structures (TLS) 61 

are well-established niches for cellular crosstalk associated with a favorable prognosis 19, 20, 21 .62 

Analogous to TLS, additional forms of immune neighborhoods composed of T cells and antigen-63 

presenting cells (APCs) have also been reported 22, 23 . However, our understanding of how these 64 

structures are formed and change over time remains limited. Importantly, most of our current 65 

insights derive from spatial analyses performed at single time points or under conditions that result 66 

in only partial therapeutic responses. As a result, the spatiotemporal mechanisms governing the 67

functional role of these T cell-APC hubs during successful immunotherapy remain largely 68

To address this deficiency, we applied a longitudinal spatial-proteomic approach herein to 70 

investigate the changes, over time, that occur in immune cell organization induced by ICT in a 71 

fully responsive mouse tumor model. Mice bearing syngeneic orthotopic T3 tumors were treated 72 

with control monoclonal antibodies (mAb) or the dual ICT combination of α-CTLA-4 and α-PD-73 

1 (herein referred to as α-CTLA-4/ α-PD-1). Tumors were harvested at multiple time points and 74 

subjected to CODEX multiplex imaging 24, 25 , allowing us to profile, at single-cell resolution, 75 

surface and intracellular proteins in fresh-frozen tissue sections. We found that T3 tumors are 76 

organized into seven distinct cellular neighborhoods (CNs) and that α-CTLA-4/ α-PD-1 promotes 77 

dramatic changes in their trajectories. Specifically, tumor rejection was orchestrated through the 78 

assembly, expansion, and maturation of an immunity-promoting CN (IP-CN) composed of CD4 ⁺79 

and CD8 ⁺ T cells forming three-cell clusters (triads) with cDC1s or IFN γ–activated macrophages. 80 

We observed that these triads form early in the α-CTLA-4/ α-PD-1-induced rejection process and 81 

increase in frequency as the IP-CN expands, indicating that continuous T cell/APC engagement is 82 

an important feature nucleating the response. We also observed that the IP-CN supports the 83 

intratumoral induction of T cell proliferation and effector function. But over time, these functions 84 

become spatially compartmentalized into specialized sub-neighborhoods, with proliferating T cells 85 

enriched with triad-forming APCs at the tumor tissue periphery as cytotoxic T cells progressively 86 

move inward, targeting cancer cells at the tumor boundary CN. Overall, this study provides a 87 

spatiotemporal mechanism for the central role that the IP-CN has in organizing tumor immunity. 88 

Our findings establish maturation of immune neighborhoods and functional compartmentalization 89 

as novel principles underlying successful ICT. 90

reveal a spatial hierarchy that emerges during tumor rejection. In control mAb-treated tumors, the 604

CN1, with no corresponding increase in the tumor bulk CN2. These findings suggest a sequential 615 

mechanism of tumor rejection in which effector CD8 ⁺ T cells from IP-CN6 preferentially target 616 

the tumor boundary CN1, where they can progressively eliminate layers of tumor cells as the IP-617 

CN6 expands during α-CTLA-4/ α-PD-1 therapy and reorganizes the TME. 618

DISCUSSION 619 

In this study, we applied longitudinal spatial proteomic profiling to define the immune dynamics 620 

underlying tumor rejection following dual α-CTLA-4/ α-PD-1 immunotherapy. By preserving 621 

tissue architecture, this approach extends our prior CyTOF and scRNA-seq analyses of 622 

disaggregated tumors 14 . Using the T3 tumor model with well-defined rejection kinetics, we 623 

analyzed multiple cohorts across different time points, enabling comprehensive characterization 624 

of the rejection process and the development of a spatiotemporal mechanistic model for the 625 

evolving role of an intratumoral CN composed of CD4 ⁺ and CD8 ⁺ T cells engaging in triad 626 

interactions with cDC1s or IFN γ–activated macrophages (Figure 8E) . In this model, progressively 627 

growing tumors have T cells that are scarce, poorly infiltrate tumor tissue, and rarely engage 628 

cDC1s or IFN γ–activated macrophages, resulting in minimal formation of APC-CD4-CD8 cellular 629 

triads. Following α-CTLA-4/ α-PD-1 therapy, however, the TME undergoes coordinated 630 

reorganization, enabling the dynamic evolution of IP-CN6, which progresses through three 631 

functionally distinct phases. During the formation phase , CD4 ⁺ T cells first increase in frequency 632 

and spatially redistribute, enabling co-localization with CD8 ⁺ T cells and APCs and initiating 633 

assembly of IP-CN6. At this stage, T cells exhibit a burst of proliferation followed by early strong 634 

induction of TNF α and IFN γ, consistent with reciprocal activation of cDC1s and macrophages. 635 

During the expansion phase , CD8 ⁺ T cells markedly increase and surpass CD4 ⁺ T cells in 636 

frequency, coinciding with a peak in T cell cytotoxicity and substantial tumor cell elimination. 637 

This step is accompanied by continued T cell and APC engagement and an increase in triad 638 

frequency. Finally, during the maturation phase , spatial compartmentalization becomes evident: 639 

Ki67 ⁺ proliferating T cells localize preferentially near triad-forming APCs—often at the tumor 640 

periphery—while GZMB ⁺ cytotoxic T cells move inward from the tumor boundary CN1 and target 641 

remaining tumor cells. Concomitantly, this transition is associated with a reorganization of inter-642 

neighborhood interactions, establishing IP-CN6 as a dominant structural and functional hub within 643 

the tissue. 644 

Our findings extend the prevailing model of tumor rejection, from the previous model involving a 645 

simple, quantitative increase in cytotoxic activity to a complex process in which the IP-CN6 646 

functions as a lymphoid-like organ that dynamically organizes antitumor immunity. Moreover, our 647 

findings provide critical temporal resolution to our understanding of the role of multicellular 648

entire span of the antitumor immune response, from initiation to complete elimination. In contrast, 651

of immune neighborhoods and functional compartmentalization as novel principles underlying 654

Mechanistically, our results indicate that the efficacy of α-CTLA-4/ α-PD-1 relies on coordinated 656 

crosstalk between T cells and diverse APCs. The formation of two distinct triad types, prior to the 657 

expansion of CD8 ⁺ T cells, indicates that immune triads may be involved with the rapid 658 

proliferation and increase in cytotoxicity observed from d11 to d13. We hypothesize that initial 659 

tumor cell killing generates antigen release that is captured by APCs, enabling further CD8 ⁺ T cell 660 

expansion. This model aligns with established roles of cDC1s in cross-presentation and MHC-II– 661 

dependent CD4 ⁺ T cell help³ ⁸. Triads function by promoting physical proximity of T cells and 662 

APCs, facilitating chemokine and/or cytokine production and their biologic activities 31 . TNF α663 

signaling via TNFR1 has been shown to induce cDC-1 maturation, as well as to enhance co-664 

stimulatory activity³ ⁹,⁴⁰ . TNF α has also been shown to enable cDC1s licensing of CD8 + T cell 665 

cytotoxicity³ ⁹. In addition, IFN γ-dependent modulation/activation of myeloid cell function is well 666 

known 38 . IFN γ-polarized macrophages have been shown to directly kill certain tumor cells, 667 

enhance T cell cytotoxicity by functioning as APCs and secreting pro-inflammatory cytokines ⁴¹, ⁴². 668 

These mechanisms would provide a positive feedback loop in which activated T cells and myeloid 669 

APCs reinforce each other's functions to sustain a productive immune response. 670 

However, not all macrophage subsets contribute positively. CX3CR1 ⁺ macrophages in tumors 671 

from control mAb-treated mice represent immature myeloid populations with limited co-672 

stimulatory and antigen-presenting capacity, as shown by others 39, 40, 41 . Similarly, CD206 ⁺673 

macrophages are often considered to express immunosuppressive functional activities 42 . Yet, 674 

emerging evidence indicates that CD206 ⁺ macrophages can efficiently capture antigens and 675 

stimulate antigen-specific CD8 ⁺ T cells 43 . This would be consistent with our observation that 676 

CD206 ⁺ macrophages are present in IP-CN6 and may support T cell activity, though their function 677 

remains unclear in our model. 678

While organized networks of T cells and APCs have been previously described in other mouse 679 

models, their temporal evolution has not been demonstrated. In the Kras/Trp53 (KP) lung 680 

adenocarcinoma model, T and B cells form organized structures in response to immunogenic 681 

epitopes, yet these tumors remain refractory to ICT 22 . Similarly, Hickey et al. observed in a model 682 

of adoptive T cell therapy that organized CD8 ⁺ T cell zones drove upregulation of PDL1 on 683 

macrophages, reduced tumor proliferation, but ultimately failed to eliminate tumors 44 . By contrast, 684 

we observed that following α-CTLA-4/ α-PD-1, IP-CN6 drives rapid and complete tumor rejection, 685 

without enforced antigen expression or requiring B cells, as we previously showed that T3 tumors 686 

do not elicit a B cell response 14 .687 

Analogous to these observations, in human cancers, Chen et al. identified “immunity hubs” 688 

enriched in stem-like CD8 ⁺ T cells and activated DCs in lung cancer, correlating with responses 689 

to anti-PD-1 23 . However, their analyses were limited to pre-treatment samples, leaving unknown 690 

the temporal trajectory of these structures. Shiao et al. identified an 'immune district' in breast 691 

cancer patients that expanded in response to pembrolizumab and radiation therapy, characterized 692 

by effector T cells, B cells, and MHCII + macrophages 17 . Yet, the single-arm design makes it 693 

difficult to differentiate anti-PD-1’s specific effect. 694 

In conclusion, this study reveals a spatiotemporal mechanism that shows how a neighborhood of 695 

T cells and myeloid APCs emerges, expands, and functionally evolves in response to α-CTLA-696 

4/ α-PD-1. Our longitudinal CODEX dataset highlights the power of spatial proteomics to identify 697 

dynamic immune organization and provides a valuable resource to obtain mechanistic insights. 698 

The current study will thus provide a critical dataset to compare the effects of mono-ICT to dual 699 

ICT, and between ICT and cancer vaccine therapy. Future spatial transcriptomic analyses will 700 

also resolve how the two distinct triad configurations contribute to CD8 ⁺ T cell programming and 701 

will aim to generate a multi-omics approach to refine our observations. A particularly important 702 

focus of our future research is to explore whether neighborhoods similar to IP-CN6 arise in 703 

additional tumor models across diverse mouse strains, including those in which MHC-II is 704 

expressed directly by the tumor cell and whether such structures can be induced by other 705 

immunotherapies 12, 45, 46 . Defining which tumor contexts and therapeutic strategies elicit these 706 

immune architectures will be critical for developing the next generation of cancer 707 

immunotherapies. 708

Cellular neighborhood (CN) analysis 960 

To identify CNs in T3 tumors, we applied the “Sliding Window Mapping Method” developed by 961 

the Nolan Lab 20 , which uses a custom k-nearest neighbor approach available via GitHub 962 

(github.com/nolanlab/NeighborhoodCoordination). We set the window size to 20 neighbors, 963 

consistent with parameters from the original study. Each window was represented as a vector 964 

containing the frequency of 13 defined cell types. Following window generation, CNs were 965 

identified using the MiniBatchKMeans clustering algorithm (scikit-learn v1.2.1), with the number 966 

of clusters (K) set to 20 and neighborhood size to 7. This configuration was chosen based on prior 967 

CODEX studies to balance resolution and biological interpretability. Higher values of K tended to 968 

split known CNs without revealing new biological patterns, while smaller values merged distinct 969 

tumor, boundary, and immune compartments. Similarly, increasing the neighborhood size beyond 970 

7 did not yield novel CNs but instead divided cell types into redundant or overly similar groups. 971 

We then calculated the log2 fold change (log2 FC) of cell-type enrichment within each 972 

neighborhood relative to the tissue background. The log2-transformed fold change values were 973 

then visualized as a heatmap using the sns.clustermap function from Python's Seaborn library 52 .974

ARCADIA overview 1084 

ARCADIA is a dual variational autoencoder (VAE) framework that integrates scRNA-seq data 1085 

with spatial proteomics data 32 . ARCADIA first identifies and matches archetypes between 1086 

modalities, defined as common extreme phenotypic states, rather than relying on feature-level 1087 

linkage for integration. It then specifically establishes "anchors", which are high-confidence cell 1088 

state archetypes that guide the alignment of all cells between modalities. 1089 

First, we denote N cells and G genes in scRNA-seq data ( XRNA ) and M cells and P protein features 1090 

for CODEX data ( XProtein ). We describe z RNA and z Protein as low-dimensional latent representations 1091 

for each data modality, and ARCADIA entangles these latent spaces using a multi-objective 1092

function that ensures gross- and fine-grained details are matched properly across modalities. The 1093 

generative process for gene expression counts X ng RNA is modeled by a zero-inflated negative 1094 

binomial distribution, ZINB( μng , θg, πng ), where mean expression μ, gene dispersion θ, and dropout 1095 

probability π are parameterized by neural networks. Simultaneously, protein expression X mp Protein 

1096 

is modeled by a Gaussian distribution N(μmp , σ²p ), where mean expression μ and variance σ² are 1097 

parameterized by separate neural networks. 1098 

To learn entangled latent representations, the framework optimizes a joint objective function that 1099 

combines reconstruction loss (Evidence Lower Bound or ELBO) with geometric alignment 1100 

constraints, incorporating known biological states and extracted anchors. The total loss function is 1101 

defined as L total = Σ (λ0 * L ELBO + λ1 * L struct ) + λ2 * L anchors + λ3 * L cross-modal , evaluated for both 1102 

scRNA-seq and CODEX and where hyperparameters λ0 , λ1 , λ2 , and λ3 are positive weights 1103 

controlling the relative contribution of each objective component as implemented in the original 1104 

study 32 . We minimize this total loss using stochastic gradient descent to optimize for the encoder 1105 

and decoder parameters. Additional mathematical details are available in 32 .1106 

The objective function consists of four key components. First, Reconstruction Fidelity (L_ELBO) 1107 

uses the Evidence Lower Bound to ensure accurate reconstruction of the original features for each 1108 

modality. Second, Structure Preservation (L_struct) maintains the internal structure of major cell 1109 

types within each modality, ensuring that distinct cell types and major patterns remain separated. 1110 

Third, Cross-Modal Alignment (L_cross-modal) uses Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) to 1111 

align the global distributions and major patterns of cell types across the two modalities. Finally, 1112 

Anchor Alignment (L_anchors) penalizes latent distances between biologically matched archetype 1113 

anchors. This term is responsible for retaining subtle intra-cell type patterns, particularly those 1114 

driven by the spatial environment that cause phenotypic differentiation within cells of the same 1115 

type. 1116
