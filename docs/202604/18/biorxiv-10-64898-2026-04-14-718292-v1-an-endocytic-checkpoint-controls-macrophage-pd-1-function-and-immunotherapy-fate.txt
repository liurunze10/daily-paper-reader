SUMMARY (150 WORDS) 1

Responses to PD1 blockade span durable tumor control to hyperprogressive disease (HPD), yet 2

innate immune mechanisms governing these extremes remain undefined. Here we integrate a 3

macrophage systems atlas (>12,500 transcriptomes) with single-cell profiles from >1,000 anti– 4

PD1 treated patients, to identify CCDC88A (GIV ) as a macrophage-intrinsic determinant of 5

durable response versus HPD. GIV loss increases PD1 surface retention, suppresses 6

phagocytosis, and accelerates tumor growth across murine models, human macrophages, and 7

patient-derived organoids. Myeloid-specific GIV deletion converts PD1 blockade from tumor-8

restraining to tumor-accelerating by reprogramming macrophages toward HPD-like states. 9

Mechanistically, GIV engages a conserved TIR-like [TILL] motif within the PD1 cytoplasmic tail to 10 

drive dynamin-dependent endocytosis, coupling innate immune signaling logic to checkpoint 11 

receptor trafficking. Pharmacologic disruption of this axis phenocopies GIV loss, revealing an 12 

endocytic vulnerability that undermines checkpoint efficacy and triggers accelerated growth at 13 

relapse. These findings define PD1 routing, rather than ligand-binding, as a macrophage-encoded 14 

checkpoint governing antitumor immunity. 15 

16

INTRODUCTION 1

Immune checkpoint inhibitors ( ICI s) have reshaped cancer therapy, delivering durable tumor 2

regression in a subset of patients across diverse malignancies 1. Yet clinical responses remain 3

strikingly heterogeneous. While some patients achieve long-term durable remission, others fail to 4

respond or, more alarmingly, develop secondary resistance and progress despite treatment. 5

When progression is accompanied by accelerated tumor growth after ICI exposure, the 6

phenomenon is termed hyperprogressive disease ( HPD )--a paradoxical acceleration of tumor 7

growth rate following therapy 2. HPD occurs in ~6% to 43% of patients 3 and is defined by a 50% 4

8

to >2-fold 5 increase in tumor growth rate after treatment relative to baseline. It occurs more 9

frequent when treated with single-agent checkpoint blockade and is associated with high 10 

metastatic burden and poor prognosis 4. Despite its clinical impact, the biological mechanisms and 11 

actionable biomarkers that distinguish durable benefit from accelerated progression remain poorly 12 

defined 6-8 (Figure 1A ). 13 

Programmed cell death protein 1 ( PD1; CD279 ) is a central inhibitory immune checkpoint 14 

that regulates T cell function 9,10,11,12 and maintains immune homeostasis in various physiological 15 

states, including infection and autoimmunity 13,14 . Engagement of PD1 by its ligands PD-L1 16 

(CD274) and PD-L2 (CD273) enforces peripheral tolerance and also suppresses anti-tumor 17 

immunity 15,16 . Therapeutic antibodies targeting the PD1–PD-L1 axis have revolutionized 18 

oncology 17,18,19 . However, as with other ICIs, only a fraction of patients derive durable benefit, and 19 

clinical outcomes have largely plateaued despite combination strategies. This has fueled renewed 20 

interest in understanding how PD1 transmits inhibitory signals at the molecular level, beyond 21 

ligand binding alone. 22 

Although PD1 blockade was originally framed as a T cell–centric intervention 20-24 , this 23 

paradigm cannot fully explain resistance, relapse, or HPD 25 . PD1 is also expressed in innate 24 

immune cells 26-30 , including tumor-associated macrophages ( TAMs ), which are increasingly 25 

recognized as upstream determinants of tumor progression and immunotherapy fate 31-36 and 26

potential drivers of HPD 37 . PD1 expression on TAMs inversely correlates with proinflammatory 1

polarization and phagocytic capacity 26,38 . Furthermore, genetic studies 39 have implicated myeloid-2

PD1, rather than T cell PD1, as a critical regulator of anti-tumor immunity. 3

Emerging clinical and experimental evidence from T cell studies indicates that PD1 4

endocytosis, rather than surface blockade per se , is required to unleash cytolytic immune 5

activity 40 . Bivalent antibodies that promote receptor clustering and internalization (e.g., nivolumab) 6

outperform monovalent antibodies (e.g., pembrolizumab) or PD-L1 targeting antibodies 40-43 . While 7

this paradigm has been explored in T cells, the role of PD1 trafficking in macrophages, particularly 8

its surface retention and endocytic control, remains largely unexplored. This is despite the known 9

ability of endocytic inhibitors to enhance phagocytosis and antibody-dependent cellular 10 

cytotoxicity (ADCC) 44 for immunoglobulin (IgG)1-based biologics. 11 

Here, we reframe durable response and the emergence of secondary resistance 12 

presenting as HPD as biological extremes to interrogate the full spectrum of TAM programs that 13 

govern immunotherapy outcome. By integrating systems immunology, single-cell tumor profiling, 14 

and functional models, we identify CCDC88A (GIV/Girdin) as a macrophage-intrinsic endocytic 15 

adaptor that controls PD1 surface availability. Our findings establish PD1 trafficking—not ligand 16 

engagement alone—as a macrophage-encoded checkpoint that determines therapeutic success 17 

or failure. Moreover, we reveal a clinically relevant vulnerability: commonly used drugs that inhibit 18 

endocytosis may impose a biphasic risk by transiently enhancing checkpoint engagement while 19 

ultimately undermining durability through sustained retention of PD1 on the macrophage surface. 20

RESULTS 1

Identification of macrophage states associated with extremes of checkpoint response. 2

We sought to define TAM programs that govern response to PD-1 blockade. Rather than 3

contrasting average responders with non-responders, we hypothesized that the mechanistic logic 4

of immunotherapy fate would be most discernible at clinical extremes. We therefore deployed a 5

two-step, outcome-extreme discovery strategy ( Figure 1B-I ) designed to resolve macrophage 6

states that actively enable durable benefit versus those that drive therapeutic failure. 7

In Step 1, we overlaid our machine-learning–derived macrophage continuum model—the 8

Signatures of Macrophage Reactivity and Tolerance (SMaRT) 45 , constructed from >12,500 9

transcriptomes—onto single-cell tumor datasets from the CPI1000+ cohort (>1,000 anti–PD-1– 10 

treated patients) 46 . The SMaRT model organizes macrophage behavior along the full continuum 11 

between the extremes of reactivity and tolerance 47 . This cross-scale alignment enabled outcome-12 

anchored mapping of macrophage states rather than lineage abundance. Intersection analyses 13 

revealed discrete gene programs enriched within responder versus non-responder TAM clusters, 14 

defining responder-associated (rTAM) and non-responder (nrTAM) signatures ( Figure 1B ; Figure 15 

S1A ; Data S1 ). 16 

Pathway enrichment analyses demonstrated that rTAMs programs are enriched for innate 17 

immune and pathogen-sensing programs, including TLR and NOD-like receptor signaling, as well 18 

as interferon-mediated antiviral responses and heterotrimeric G protein–related molecular 19 

functions ( Figure 1C ; Figure S1B–C ). In contrast, nrTAMs were dominated by ribosomal 20 

biogenesis, translational control, and RNA-binding functions, consistent with metabolically active 21 

but immunologically inert macrophages ( Figure S1D–F ). Clinically, high rTAM expression robustly 22 

predicted improved overall and progression-free survival following PD1 blockade, whereas nrTAM 23 

enrichment predicted poor outcomes ( Figure 1D–G ), validating the outcome relevance of this 24 

systems framework. 25

In Step 2, we refined these programs using an experimental extreme-phenotype model. 1

We leveraged a study 48 that transcriptionally profiled macrophage education in vitro using 2

conditioned media from non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) cell lines that display either durable 3

response–like (DR-like) or HPD–like growth in mice ( Figure 1H ). Unsupervised clustering of rTAM 4

genes across these polarized conditions resolved discrete modules segregating DR-associated 5

from HPD-associated macrophage states ( Figure 1I ). By intentionally leveraging phenotypic 6

extremes—clinical and experimental—this strategy amplifies biological signal over noise, 7

enabling resolution of macrophage decision circuits that would be obscured in intermediate states. 8

Together, this outcome-extreme systems approach defines a macrophage effector axis 9

that tracks immunotherapy fate and establishes a conceptual and analytical framework to uncover 10 

macrophage-encoded checkpoints governing response durability versus hyperprogression. 11 

12 

CCDC88A (GIV) couples the responder macrophage state to PD1 surface control 13 

Among rTAM genes, CCDC88A (encoding GIV ; Gα-interacting vesicle-associated protein, also 14 

known as Girdin) emerged as the sole candidate that simultaneously (i) tracks with durable clinical 15 

response in patients, (ii) integrates signals downstream of innate immune receptors, including 16 

multiple TLRs (including TLR4 49 ) and NOD-like receptors (i.e., NOD2 50 ), and (iii) directly 17 

modulates heterotrimeric Gα protein signaling 51-56 . As the prototypical non-receptor Guanine 18 

nucleotide Exchange Modulator (GEM 51 ), GIV links diverse receptor classes to G protein 19 

signaling 57 —unlike canonical GPCR-restricted signaling 49,58,59 —and is highly expressed in 20 

macrophages 49 , where it is a key determinant of polarization within the SMaRT framework 60 .21 

These properties nominate GIV as a putative mechanistic bridge between macrophage state, 22 

innate immune signaling, and PD1 pathway outcomes. 23 

To test this hypothesis in vivo , we used mice with myeloid-specific deletion of 24 

CCDC88A 49,61 (see Methods ) to create a syngeneic LL/2 lung cancer model ( Figure 2A ). Loss of 25 

GIV resulted in significantly increased tumor burden, reflected by elevated tumor volume and 26

weight at harvest ( Figure 2A ). Tumors from GIV-KO mice exhibited altered distribution of PD1-1

positive immune cells, restricted to tumor periphery ( Figure 2B ). Critically, a macrophage-intrinsic 2

To establish robustness and cross-species reproducibility, we validated this phenotype 6

across three independent macrophage model systems: (i) GIV-depleted RAW 264.7 murine 7

macrophages (~85–90% knockdown; Figure 2E ); (ii) thioglycolate-elicited primary peritoneal 8

human THP1 macrophages with CRISPR-mediated GIV loss (>90% depletion; Figure 2G ). 10 

Across all systems, GIV depletion consistently increased macrophage surface PD1, as assessed 11 

by flow cytometry, immunoblotting, and immunofluorescence ( Figure 2D–G ), demonstrating a 12 

conserved and macrophage-intrinsic role for GIV in restraining PD1 surface accumulation. 13 

Together, these data establish GIV as an essential regulator of surface PD1 levels on 14 

macrophages across in vivo , primary, and immortalized cell-line models. By linking responder 15 

macrophage state to a concrete receptor-localization phenotype, GIV emerges as a decisive 16 

rTAM-intrinsic determinant of antitumor immunity and sets the stage for mechanistic dissection of 17 

how PD1 trafficking governs immunotherapy response. 18 

19 

Macrophage GIV licenses phagocytosis and tumor clearance in human organoid models 20 

Because macrophage PD1 functions as a “don’t-eat-me” checkpoint 26 , its accumulation on the 21 

cell surface is predicted to suppress phagocytosis. Consistent with this, loss of GIV impaired 22 

macrophage phagocytic function: GIV-deficient primary peritoneal macrophages showed 23 

markedly reduced tumor cell uptake in vitro (Figure 2H ; Figure S3A–C ). Similarly, GIV-deficient 24 

macrophages (F4/80 ⁺) engulfed fewer fluorescently (CFSE)-labeled tumor cells in vivo (Figure 25 

2I ). 26

We next asked whether this defect translates into impaired clearance of patient-derived 1

tumors in a human-relevant system. To address this, we established a human macrophage–tumor 2

organoid co-culture assay in which CFSE-labeled patient-derived lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) 3

organoids (PDOs) were incubated with WT and GIV-KO THP1-derived macrophages ( Figure 3A ). 4

PDOs were rigorously benchmarked against matched donor tissue to confirm preservation of 5

tumor identity, architecture, and lineage markers (TTF1 62,63 , CK7) 64 (Figure S3D–F ). To preserve 6

continuity with other functional readouts and prioritize genetic interpretability over patient-specific 7

immune heterogeneity, we used THP1 macrophages rather than donor-matched immune cells. 8

GIV loss resulted in significantly increased residual tumoroid burden after 4 h of co-culture, 9

reflected by right-shifted size-distribution histograms and increased residual tumoroid area 10 

(Figure 3C–E ). By contrast, adjacent normal lung organoids were cleared equivalently by WT and 11 

GIV-KO macrophages, with no significant difference in residual organoid area ( Figure 3E ). 12 

Because PDO generation efficiency, histopathologic integrity, and marker expression were 13 

indistinguishable across co-culture conditions ( Figure S3D–F ), the observed differences in tumor 14 

clearance are attributable to macrophage genotype rather than tumor-intrinsic variability. 15 

These findings were in keeping with high-resolution time-lapse imaging in reductionist 2D 16 

assays, where CFSE-labeled MDA-MB-231 breast cancer cells were incubated with WT and GIV-17 

KO THP1-derived macrophages on fibronectin-coated plates ( Figure S3A ). WT macrophages 18 

underwent rapid tumor cell engulfment, often completing phagocytosis after a single productive 19 

encounter, whereas tumor cells survived multiple failed interactions with GIV-deficient 20 

macrophages ( Figure S3B-C ). 21 

Together, findings across three orthogonal systems — reductionist 2D assays, in vivo 22 

phagocytosis, and human tumor organoid co-culture systems — establish GIV as a macrophage-23 

intrinsic checkpoint for tumor clearance. The restriction of this phenotype to malignant, but not 24 

normal, organoids underscores that GIV-dependent macrophage function is context-aware and 25 

tumor-specific, positioning GIV as a functional executor of TAM-mediated antitumor immunity. 26

Myeloid GIV favors PD1 blockade–induced durable response; its loss unleashes hyper 2

We next asked whether loss of macrophage GIV alters the therapeutic trajectory of PD1 blockade 4

in vivo . We treated tumor-bearing WT and myeloid-specific GIV-KO mice with anti–PD1 in a 5

syngeneic LL/2 lung cancer model (Figure 4A ). At the end of treatment (week 3), both genotypes 6

appeared to respond: anti–PD1 slowed tumor growth and reduced tumor burden relative to 7

untreated controls ( Figure 4B-C ; AUC analyses in Figure S4A–B ), despite the higher baseline 8

tumor burden in GIV-KO mice ( Figure 4B-C ). Thus, initial tumor shrinkage alone suggested 9

‘success’ in both genotypes. 10 

Because early response is a poor surrogate for durable benefit 65 — a meaningful and the 11 

most desirable immunotherapy clinical trial endpoint 66,67 — we explicitly separated initial sensitivity 12 

from long-term outcome. In an early-response paradigm with harvest at day 15 ( Figure 4D ;13 

Figure S4C ), WT and GIV-KO mice showed comparable early responses, with similar reductions 14 

in tumor volume and weight ( Figure 4F–I ; Figure S4D ). These data establish that macrophage 15 

GIV and GIV-dependent surface control of PD1 expression are dispensable for the initial response 16 

to PD1 blockade. 17 

Despite similar early responses, tumor architecture and macrophage organization 18 

diverged sharply. WT tumors displayed deep macrophage penetration into the tumor core, 19 

whereas GIV-KO tumors showed peripheral macrophage confinement, a spatial pattern 20 

associated with ineffective clearance ( Figure 4E ). This divergence predicted radically different 21 

fates. After treatment cessation ( Figure 4J ), WT mice maintained durable tumor control, marked 22 

by liquefied tumor beds encased in fibrotic capsules—hallmarks of immune-mediated clearance 23 

(Figure 4K ). In stark contrast, GIV-KO mice underwent rapid tumor rebound, developing large, 24 

solid tumors consistent with hyperprogressive disease ( Figure 4K ). Relapse analyses confirmed 25 

significantly increased tumor volume and weight in GIV-KO mice ( Figure 4L ), with a ~2-3-fold 26

effector macrophage and NK cell gene signatures in GIV-KO tumors ( Figure 5F ), consistent with 25

PD1 and GIV proteins formed complexes in THP1-derived macrophages. Inhibiting dynamin-23

PD1•GIV complexes ( Figure 6B ; Figure S6A–B ), consistent with accumulation at the plasma 25 

membrane and arrested clathrin-mediated endocytic intermediates. Orthogonal validation in a 26

A conserved TILL motif couples GIV to PD1 to control receptor trafficking 10 

We next asked how GIV physically engages PD1 to control receptor routing. Domain mapping 11 

revealed that GIV’s C-terminus and the PD1 cytoplasmic tail each harbor a short, conserved TIR-12 

like loop (TILL) motif ( Figure 7A–B ), originally described in cytokine receptors 80 and shared with 13 

TIR-containing innate immune receptors 49 . Because TILL-motifs mediate homotypic 14 

interactions 49 , this convergence in sequence suggested that PD1 co-opts an evolutionarily 15 

conserved innate-immune interaction logic, i.e., a putative TILL-motif to engage GIV. 16 

Biochemical reconstitution confirmed this mechanism of direct coupling. Recombinant 17 

His–GIV C-terminus bound the GST–PD1 tail, but not GST alone, and mutation of key residues 18 

within GIV’s TILL motif disrupted PD1 binding ( Figure 7C ). Reciprocal mutagenesis of the PD1 19 

tail similarly impaired GIV association, including substitutions that mirror recurrent somatic 20 

mutations in human cancers (COSMIC) clustering within a membrane-proximal cytosolic patch 21 

encompassing the TILL region ( Figure 7D–E ; Figure S7A–B ). These data define a bilateral 22 

TILL•TILL interface as the molecular determinant of specific GIV•PD1 coupling. 23 

To test the functional sufficiency of this mode of coupling, we used cell-permeable 24 

synthetic peptides (CPPs) encoding the TILL-motif (TILL-sequence fused to the C-terminus of 25 

the Antennapedia-CPP 81 , to acutely perturb this interaction (Figure 7F ). These peptides, 26

DISCUSSION 1

This study identifies PD1 endocytosis, not ligand blockade alone, as a macrophage-intrinsic 2

immune checkpoint that determines immunotherapy durability. We show that the endocytic 3

adaptor GIV/Girdin binds PD1 through a conserved TIR-like loop (TILL) interaction, enabling 4

clathrin-mediated internalization that limits surface PD1 on TAMs. This routing preserves 5

macrophage phagocytosis and safeguards durable responses to PD1 blockade. Genetic or 6

pharmacologic disruption of this pathway traps PD1 at the plasma membrane, collapses 7

macrophage effector function, accelerates tumor growth, and converts checkpoint therapy into 8

hyperprogressive disease. Thus, macrophage endocytic routing represents a tractable axis for 9

improving the durability of cancer immunotherapy. 10 

11 

TAM states dictate immunotherapy fate 12 

By integrating a macrophage systems model with single-cell tumor transcriptomes, we define TAM 13 

subtypes that stratify durable response versus secondary resistance with accelerated tumor 14 

growth at relapse. These states are governed by process-level wiring—innate sensing, endocytic 15 

competence, and phagocytic capacity, rather than lineage abundance. GIV serves as a nodal 16 

regulator coupling these programs, providing a unifying explanation for TAM heterogeneity. This 17 

framework reconciles prior context-dependent observations, including the evolving biology of 18 

TREM2 ⁺ macrophages and their context-specific role in tumors. 19 

20 

A TIR-like loop embeds PD1 in innate immune logic 21 

We identified a previously unrecognized TIR-like loop (TILL) motif within the PD1 cytoplasmic tail, 22 

revealing that PD1 co-opts conserved innate immune interaction logic. PD1 engages GIV through 23 

a TILL•TILL interface analogous to TLR–adaptor signaling, reframing PD1 as embedded within 24 

innate immune architecture rather than a purely adaptive checkpoint. Our findings suggest that 25 

GIV-mediated PD1 endocytosis may represent a physiological brake-release mechanism, 26

allowing macrophages to recalibrate between tolerance and inflammation in response to 1

contextual cues. In cancer, failure of this routing mechanism locks macrophages into a tolerant, 2

non-phagocytic state, undermining antitumor immunity. 3

Because GIV binds both TLR4 and PD1 via the same motif, these interactions are likely 4

mutually exclusive, positioning GIV as a molecular switch that integrates innate activation with 5

checkpoint resolution. Moreover, spatial proximity of the TILL motif to PD1 K233 (K235 in mice) 6

further suggests crosstalk between GIV binding and receptor stability that is dictated by dynamic 7

ubiquitination at that site 83,84 .8

Somatic mutations within the PD1 cytoplasmic tail—particularly those clustering near the 9

TILL region—may be acquired or functionally redeployed in macrophages through tumor material 10 

uptake, nanotubes 85 or cell fusion 86 , generating “tumor-educated” macrophages with altered 11 

checkpoint trafficking. Our findings suggest that such mutations could phenocopy GIV loss by 12 

stabilizing PD1 at the macrophage surface, thereby promoting immune tolerance and contributing 13 

to tumor progression and immunotherapy resistance. 14 

This mechanistic convergence suggests a model in which GIV occupancy integrates 15 

innate activation signals with checkpoint receptor fate, coordinating inflammatory tone, receptor 16 

turnover, and immune effector function. Disruption of this coordination—genetically or 17 

pharmacologically—uncouples innate sensing from checkpoint resolution. 18 

19 

GIV as an endocytic adaptor for PD1 20 

Our findings establish GIV as a bona fide endocytic adaptor for PD1. GIV directly binds dynamin 21 

and is known to selectively gate clathrin-mediated endocytosis of defined cargoes—promoting 22 

uptake of receptors such as transferrin and E-cadherin while restraining EGFR and β-integrin. 23 

Engagement of PD1 by GIV drives PD1 internalization, and notably, the short TIR-like loop (TILL) 24 

motif alone is sufficient to confer this endocytic control, independent of GIV’s other signaling 25

modules. This sufficiency was unexpected given GIV’s large, multimodular architecture and its 1

broad roles in cytoskeletal and signaling networks 51-56,87 .2

Mechanistically, GIV may facilitate PD1 endocytosis through two non-exclusive 3

mechanisms. First, the GIV-binding region in PD1 cytosolic tail contains a membrane-proximal 4

hydrophobic patch that may serve as an electrostatic–hydrophobic “Velcro,” stabilizing PD1 at the 5

plasma membrane ( Figure 8B-C ), analogous to mechanisms described for PD-L1 88,89 (Figure 6

S7C ). Such patches anchor proteins via a two-pronged mechanism: hydrophobic interactions with 7

lipid tails and electrostatic attraction to anionic membrane lipids (e.g., PIP2, phosphatidylserine) 8

> 90

. This dual-site mechanism enables precise control over where and when proteins bind to 9

membranes, thereby affecting signal-coordinated processes like endocytosis 90 . GIV binding likely 10 

disrupts this stabilization, licensing PD1 for endocytic uptake. Second, because GIV oligomerizes 11 

through its coiled-coil domain 91 , GIV•PD1 engagement may promote receptor clustering, a well-12 

recognized prerequisite for efficient tumor cell internalization, which in turn promotes tumor 13 

immunity 40 . Together, these observations integrate biochemical, structural, and functional data 14 

into a coherent model in which GIV couples PD1 to the endocytic machinery to control checkpoint 15 

receptor fate. 16 

17 

Endocytic inhibition as a double-edged sword in cancer care 18 

Our findings reveal a critical paradox in cancer care: Endocytic inhibition can both enhance and 19 

undermine anti-tumor immunity, depending on the context. Endocytic inhibitors have been widely 20 

explored as adjuvants to improve the efficacy of IgG1-based biologics (e.g., cetuximab, 21 

trastuzumab, avelumab) by prolonging receptor–antibody engagement and enhancing ADCC 44 .22 

Our data demonstrate that the same strategy is deleterious in the context of PD1 blockade, where 23 

sustained inhibition of endocytosis traps PD1 at the macrophage surface and converts 24 

immunotherapy into hyperprogressive disease. 25

This distinction has immediate clinical relevance. Psychotropic and antiemetic drugs that 1

impair dynamin-dependent endocytosis are widely prescribed in oncology, often without 2

consideration of immunotherapy interactions. In light of our mechanistic findings, 3

pharmacoepidemiologic evidence warrants caution: concomitant use of such agents during PD-1 4

blockade may blunt the development of durable antitumor immunity and worsen survival. 5

Together, these observations establish endocytic inhibition as a double-edged sword, 6

underscoring the need for temporal precision and pathway awareness when deploying such 7

agents alongside IgG1-based biologics vs immunotherapy. 8

9

Limitations 10 

This study opens several mechanistic avenues that extend beyond the scope of the present work. 11 

GIV is a multimodular scaffold, and we focused on its endocytic adaptor function; how its 12 

additional domains couple PD1 to tyrosine kinase pathways or heterotrimeric G protein signaling 13 

remains to be defined. Notably, GIV potently suppresses the cAMP–PKA axis 51,59,92 , an emerging 14 

driver of protumorigenic TAM states 93-95 , raising the possibility that PD1 trafficking intersects with 15 

metabolic and transcriptional checkpoints downstream of cAMP signaling. We did not resolve how 16 

GIV dynamically partitions between innate immune receptors (e.g., TLR4, NOD2) and PD1, or 17 

how crosstalk between these pathways are temporally coordinated during immune activation. The 18 

proximity of the GIV-binding site to PD1 K233 (K235 in mice) highlights an unexplored interface 19 

between endocytic routing and ubiquitin-mediated control of receptor stability 83,84 . Whether GIV 20 

biases PD1 toward degradation versus recycling remains an open question. Because PD1 and 21 

PD-L1 are co-expressed in myeloid cells, the possibility of cis interactions 96,97 at the plasma 22 

membrane—and GIV’s role in such signaling—remains unexplored. Finally, as GIV is also 23 

expressed in T cells 98 , its endocytic functions may extend to PD1 positioning in adaptive immunity. 24

DETAILED EXPERIMENTAL METHODS 

Cell culture 

Thioglycolate-elicited murine peritoneal macrophages (TGPMs) were isolated from 8-12-wk-old C57BL/6 mice by peritoneal lavage with 10 ml of ice-cold RPMI (Roswell Park Memorial Institute Medium) per mouse, 4 days after intraperitoneal injection of 2.5 ml of aged, sterile 3% thioglycolate broth (BD Difco, NJ, USA) and cultured as described previously 107 . Cells were passed through a 70 μm filter to remove tissue debris, counted, centrifuged, and resuspended in RPMI-1640 containing 10 % FBS and 1% penicillin/streptomycin. Cultures were maintained at 37°C in a humidified 5% CO 2 incubator. Cells were plated at the required density, and the medium was replaced after 4 hours to remove non adherent cells. Cells were allowed to equilibrate overnight before various stimulations and/or treatments, as indicated in the figure legends.

Lewis lung carcinoma (LL/2), HeLa, THP-1 monocytes, and RAW264.7 cells were obtained from American Type Culture Collection (ATCC). RAW264.7 and THP-1 cells were maintained in RPMI media, while LL/2 and HeLa cells were maintained in DMEM (Dulbecco's Modified Eagle Medium); all supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% penicillin/Streptomycin. All macrophages were grown in serum that was heat inactivated (at 56°C for 30 minutes). 

Collection of human lung specimens for organoid isolation 

Adult lung organoids—from both lung adenocarcinomas and adjacent normal tissues--were generated, as outlined previously 108 , from fresh surgical resections, prospectively collected by a cardiothoracic surgeon. Prior to collection of the lung specimens, each tissue was sent to a gross anatomy room where a pathologist cataloged the area of focus, and the extra specimens were routed to the research laboratory in Tissue-2-Go (HUMANOID TM 

Cat# HUM202403) for cell isolation. Deidentified lung tissues obtained during surgical resection, which were deemed excess by clinical pathologists, were collected using an approved human research protocol (IRB#101590; PI: Patricia Thistlewaite). Isolation and biobanking of organoids from this lung tissue were carried out using an approved human research protocol (IRB# 190105; PI: Ghosh) that covers human subject research at the UC San Diego HUMANOID Centre of Research Excellence (CoRE). For all de-identified human subjects’ information, including age, gender, and previous history of the disease, was collected from the chart following the rules of HIPAA and described in Table 1 .

Isolation and culture of human whole lung-derived organoids 

A previously published protocol 109,110 was modified 108 to isolate lung organoids from three human subjects. Briefly, normal human lung specimens were washed with PBS containing 4× penicillin-streptomycin and minced using surgical scissors. The resulting tissue fragments were resuspended in 2 ml of wash buffer (Advanced DMEM/F-12, 10 mM HEPES, 1× Glutamax, 1× penicillin-streptomycin) containing 2 mg/ml Collagenase Type I (Thermo Fisher, MA, USA) and incubated at 37°C for approximately 1 hr. During incubation, the tissue was mechanically dissociated every 10 minutes using a 1 ml micropipette and monitored under a light microscope. Once 80–100% of the cells were released from connective tissue, digestion was halted by adding 10 ml of wash buffer supplemented with 2% fetal bovine serum. The suspension was then passed through a 100 μm cell strainer and centrifuged at 200 rcf. Residual erythrocytes were lysed using 2 ml of red blood cell lysis buffer (Invitrogen, MA, USA) at room temperature for 5 min, followed by the addition of 10 ml of wash buffer and another centrifugation step at 200 rcf. The resulting cell pellets were resuspended in cold Matrigel (Corning,NY, USA) and seeded in 25 μl droplets on a 12-well tissue culture plate. The plate was inverted and incubated at 37°C for 10 min to allow complete polymerization of the Matrigel before the addition of 1 ml Lungbrew (HUMANOID TM cat# HUM2020-250) per well. Organoids were maintained in a humidified incubator at 37°C/5% CO 2, with a complete media change performed every 3 days. After 7 and 10 days, when the desired confluency was reached, organoids were harvested using PBS with 0.5 mM EDTA and centrifuged at 200 rcf for 5 minutes. They were then dissociated in

Mice

Sex as a biological variable 

Sex was not considered as a biological variable in this study. Both male and female mice were used. 

Establishment of syngeneic ectopic tumor model in mice and drug treatment 

Mice were injected subcutaneously on the right hindlimb flank with 0.5 x 10 6 Lewis lung cancer cells suspended in 0.1 ml of sterile phosphate buffered saline (PBS) supplemented with 20% Matrigel. Drug treatments started when tumors reached approximately 50 mm 3 and mice were sacrificed at a maximum tumor volume of 2000 mm³, which was considered the study endpoint. Mice were weighed prior to the start of drug treatment, and tumor dimensions were measured every alternate day using a digital caliper (Vernier, Silverline Digital) for a total of three weeks. Tissue histology 

Histological processing of excised tumors was performed at the Histology Core Facility of the La Jolla Institute for Immunology, La Jolla, California. Tissues were initially fixed in zinc formalin for 24 hours, followed by transfer to 70% ethanol and storage at room temperature until sectioning. Samples were then sectioned for hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining, and adjacent unstained sections were processed for immunohistochemical (IHC) staining using markers of interest. Stained sections were scanned and imaged using a Leica light microscope (Leica Microsystems, Wetzlar, Germany), and the resulting images were analyzed using ImageJ/Fiji software (NIH, Bethesda, MD) 112 .

Immunohistochemical and immunofluorescent staining of tumor tissue sections

Homology modeling of GIV•PD1 interaction via TIR-like BB-loop (TILL) sequences 

In the absence of resolved structures of the cytoplasmic tail of PD1 and GIV’s C-terminus, we built homology models of the GIV(TILL)•PD1(TILL) complex using as template a previously published 49,122 model of GIV’s TILL motif bound to TLR4’s BB loop via a homotypic interface. Homology modeling was performed in ICM (Internal Coordinate Mechanics) software and the details of how the original GIV•TLR4(TIR) model was rigorously vetted and experimentally validated can be found in 49 . Briefly, the GIV-TILL and PD1-TILL peptides were built ab initio ,tethered to the respective Pro-Gly positions, and its conformations were extensively sampled (> 108 steps) by biased probability Monte Carlo (BPMC) sampling in internal coordinates, with the TLR4 TIR domain represented as a set of energy potentials precalculated on a 0.5 Å 3D grid and including Van der Waals potential, electrostatic potential, hydrogen bonding potential, and surface energy. Following such grid-based docking, the peptide poses were merged with full-atom models of the TLR4 TIR domain, and further sampling was conducted for the peptide and surrounding side chains of the TLR4 residues. Co-complexed GIV-TILL and PD1-TILL peptide-only models are displayed here.

DETAILED COMPUTATIONAL METHODS 

RNA Sequencing and Data Processing. 

RNA sequencing libraries were generated at the UCSD IGM Genomic Center using the Illumina TruSeq Stranded Total RNA Library Prep Gold with TruSeq Unique Dual Indexes (Illumina, San Diego, CA). Samples were processed following the manufacturer’s instructions, except for modifying the RNA shear time to five minutes. The resulting libraries were multiplexed and sequenced with 100 base pairs (bp) Paired-End (PE100) to a depth of approximately 40 million reads per sample on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000. Samples were demultiplexed using bcl2fastq v2.20 Conversion Software (Illumina, San Diego, CA). Raw FASTQ files were trimmed, filtered, and mapped to the human genome for downstream quantification analyses. Low-quality sequences were trimmed or removed with Trimmomatic. STAR (version 2.6.0a) was used to align reads on the reference genome ( Mus musculus genome build GRCm38.94). The resulting transcriptome-aligned sequences were used for expression quantification by using RSEM (version 1.3.3) with “–forward-prob 0” option. TPM scores for each sample were used through the RSEM tables (RSEM gene. results tables). We used log2(TPM) if TPM >1, else TPM - 1 values for each sample as the final gene expression value for downstream expression analyses. Datasets are uploaded to NCBI GEO for both whole tumors (GSE314811) and TAM (GSE314813) datasets. Access codes to datasets are provided in “ Cover Letter ” and the datasets will be publicly released at the time of publication. 

Differential Expression Analysis 

Differential gene expression analysis was performed using raw count data processed through DESeq2 123 . Genes with an absolute log2 fold change ≥ 1 and an adjusted p-value ≤ 0.05 (Benjamini-Hochberg method) were considered differentially expressed genes (DEGs). Pathway enrichment analysis of DEG lists was conducted using the ShinyGO 124 database and its integrated algorithms. Volcano plots representing differentially expressed genes were created using the matplotlib package.

StepMiner analysis 

StepMiner is an algorithm designed to detect stepwise transitions in time-series gene expression data 125 . It fits step functions to expression profiles by identifying the sharpest changes in signal, corresponding to gene expression switching events. The algorithm evaluates all possible step positions and calculates the average expression on either side of each step to define constant segments. An adaptive regression approach is then used to select the step position that minimizes the sum of squared errors between the observed and fitted data. The selected step is used as the StepMiner threshold . This threshold is used to convert gene expression values into Boolean values. A noise margin of 2-fold change is applied around the threshold to determine intermediate values, and these values are ignored during Boolean analysis. Composite gene signature analysis using Boolean Network Explorer (BoNE) 

Boolean network explorer (BoNE) 126 provides an integrated platform for the construction, visualization and querying of a gene expression signature underlying a disease or a biological process in three steps: First, the expression levels of all genes in these datasets were converted to binary values (high or low) using the StepMiner algorithm. Second, Gene expression values were normalized according to a modified Z-score approach centered around StepMiner threshold (formula = (expr - SThr)/3*stddev). Third, the normalized expression values for every gene were added together to create the final composite score for the gene signature 127 . As a modified Z-score, the composite score of any gene signature typically ranges from negative to positive values, reflecting the dynamic range of each gene in the signature. These composite scores represent the overall activity or state of biological pathways associated with the genes and, hence, can identify differences between control and query groups within any given dataset. However, composite scores can neither be directly compared between various gene signatures on a dataset (because they are not normalized according to the number of genes in each signature), nor can the same signature be compared across datasets (which are individually normalized according to intra-dataset sample distribution or have other inherent differences). The samples were ordered based on the final signature score.

In silico deconvolution of bulk RNA-seq (tumor) data was performed using the Granulator R package 128 to estimate immune cell compositions. Cell-type abundance estimates were normalized using the immune cell signature matrix developed by Monaco et al 129 .

Survival Analysis 

Kaplan–Meier survival analysis and log-rank tests for statistical significance were performed using KM-plotter (https://kmplot.com/analysis/) 130 . Briefly, datasets from immunotherapy treated patients with different tumor types including lung, melanoma, bladder cancer, head and neck, esophageal adenocarcinoma, hepatocellular cancer and glioblastoma were analyzed using the "Immunotherapy" subsystem 131 . The KM Plotter uses a default algorithm to automatically select the best cutoff value for separating samples into high and low expression groups when generating Kaplan-Meier survival plots. This automated selection is based on finding the cutoff that yields the most statistically significant separation between the groups, as determined by a Cox regression analysis 132 .
