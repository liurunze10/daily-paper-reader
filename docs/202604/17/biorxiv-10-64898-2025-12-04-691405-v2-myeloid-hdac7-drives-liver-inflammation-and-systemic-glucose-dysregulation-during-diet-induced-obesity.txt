Abstract 43 

Objectives: Histone deacetylase 7 (HDAC7), a classical HDAC family member, promotes 44 LPS-inducible glycolysis and inflammatory mediator production in macrophages, innate 45 immune cells that contribute to pathology in metabolic diseases. Here, we investigated myeloid 46 HDAC7 functions in obesity-driven metabolic disease. 47 

48 

Methods: We used gain- and loss-of-function genetic approaches in mice to investigate 49 myeloid HDAC7 functions in hepatic inflammation and metabolic disease, as well as 50 associations with hepatic gene signatures characteristic of advanced chronic liver disease 51 (CLD). 52 53 

Results: Transgenic expression of Hdac7 in myeloid cells increased liver inflammation and 54 liver mRNA levels of Ccl2 and Il1b , key inflammatory mediators linked to CLD. Liver 55 glycogen levels were also decreased, another feature of CLD. Transgenic expression of Hdac7 56 in myeloid cells mimicked the hepatic inflammatory phenotype that was observed in mice fed 57 a high fat, high cholesterol, and high sucrose (HFHCHS) diet, an obesity model that mimics 58 some features of metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease. In myeloid Hdac7 59 transgenic mice fed a HFHCHS diet, relative weight gain was increased, fasted glucose levels 60 were elevated and glucose tolerance was dysregulated by comparison to control mice. 61 Conversely, fasted blood glucose levels were reduced and glucose tolerance was improved in 62 myeloid Hdac7 -deleted mice on a HFHCHS diet. HDAC7 mRNA levels were also elevated in 63 the livers of people with advanced CLD and spatial transcriptomics revealed that myeloid 64 HDAC7 directs hepatic gene signatures characteristic of advanced CLD. 65 

66 

Conclusions: Myeloid HDAC7 contributes to hepatic inflammation and systemic glucose 67 dysregulation in a mouse model of obesity and liver inflammation. 68

Introduction 72 

Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD) is a common cause of 73 chronic liver disease (CLD) that is associated with substantial morbidity and mortality 1.74 MASLD progressing to metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis (MASH) is the major 75 cause of cirrhosis, especially in developed countries 2. MASLD represents a spectrum of 76 conditions, ranging from fatty liver (steatosis) without significant inflammation to MASH, 77 where liver injury, inflammation, and/or fibrosis accompany steatosis 3. The thyroid hormone 78 receptor-β agonist Resmetirom 4 and the glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) receptor agonist 79 semaglutide 5 are approved in the USA as therapies for patients with moderate to advanced liver 80 fibrosis. Other therapies are currently under clinical evaluation 6, including tirzepatide, a dual 81 agonist for GLP-1 and glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide receptors 7. However, 82 these therapies are likely to be effective in only a sub-set of MASH patients. The development 83 of more effective treatments requires a better understanding of the complex underlying 84 mechanisms that drive MASH and the employment of approaches that target multiple 85 inflammatory and fibrotic pathways. 86 87 Macrophages are key innate immune cells that sense and respond to endogenous and exogenous 88 danger signals, enabling them to detect perturbations in homeostasis 8. These cells are extremely 89 heterogeneous, exhibiting diverse phenotypes depending on the specific environmental 90 conditions encountered 9. Liver macrophages, comprising both tissue-resident Kupffer cells and 91 monocyte-derived macrophages, play important roles in liver homeostasis and in responding 92 to environmental signals. Kupffer cells clear endotoxin and phagocytose bacterial pathogens, 93 dying cells and other debris in the hepatic parenchyma 10, 11 , and also release inflammatory 94 cytokines upon activation via the pattern recognition receptors, such as toll-like receptor (TLR) 95 412 . Recruited monocytes can also differentiate into macrophages in the liver, either promoting 96 wound healing and tissue repair when the injury-inducing agents have been removed 13, 14 or 97 perpetuating inflammation and pathology during active disease 15, 16 . A failure to resolve 98 macrophage activation leads to dysregulated production of inflammatory mediators that 99 contribute to pathological conditions, including liver fibrosis 10 . For example, elevated TNF 100 levels in inflamed livers contribute to fibrogenesis 17 , the chemokine CCL2 promotes lipid 101 accumulation in hepatocytes leading to obesity-induced liver steatosis 18 and MASH 102 progression 19 , and inflammasome-mediated IL-1β production is associated with collagen 103 deposition in MASH patients 20 . Signalling molecules that connect glycolysis and inflammation such 112 as the glycolytic enzyme pyruvate kinase isoform 2 (PKM2) have been implicated in the 113 pathogenesis of MASH. For example, elevated levels of PKM2 in both serum and liver were 114 associated with MASLD severity 27 . PKM2 exists in two forms, a tetramer that is associated 115 with its glycolytic function and a dimeric form that drives inflammatory responses. Tetrameric 116 PKM2 polarises macrophages towards a regulatory or anti-inflammatory phenotype, 117 ameliorating liver inflammation and steatosis 28 . In contrast, nuclear accumulation of dimeric 118 PKM2 in macrophages was reported to promote macrophage polarisation towards a pro-119 inflammatory phenotype, leading to MASH pathogenesis 29 . Significantly, a group of proteins 120 called the class IIa histone deacetylases (HDACs) promote dimerization and activation of the 121 pro-inflammatory form of PKM2 in macrophages 30 .122 123 HDACs remove an acetyl moiety from the ɛ-amino group of lysine residues of histones, with 124 this generally associated with repression of gene transcription 31 . However, thousands of non-125 histone proteins are also regulated by lysine acetylation 32, 33 , so HDACs control many cellular 126 processes beyond gene expression. Class IIa HDACs are a subfamily of the classical HDACs 127 that share a conserved C-terminal deacetylase domain and a less conserved N-terminus that 128 interacts with a broad range of cellular proteins 34 , allowing this family to regulate various 129 biological processes in physiological and pathological conditions (reviewed in 35, 36 ). One class 130 IIa HDAC with a particularly important function in metabolic and inflammatory processes in 131 macrophages is HDAC7 (reviewed in 37, 38 ). In human and murine macrophages, HDAC7 132 orchestrates LPS-induced glycolysis and the production of key inflammatory mediators IL-1β 133 and CCL2 30 , two inflammatory mediators associated with MASLD/MASH. Interestingly, 134 HDAC7 primarily functions in macrophages responding to submaximal concentration of 135 LPS 39 , suggesting that this class IIa HDAC may be particularly important in driving chronic 136 low-grade inflammation. Myeloid Hdac7 overexpression promotes liver inflammation in mice 145 We previously showed that HDAC7 drives production of the pro-inflammatory mediators 146 CCL2 and IL-1β in TLR4-activated macrophages 30, 39 and that acute LPS-induced systemic 147 inflammation was amplified in MacHDAC7 mice that selectively over-express HDAC7 in 148 myeloid cells 30 . However, whether myeloid HDAC7 drives inflammatory responses and/or 149 pathological changes in the liver has not been examined. To determine if myeloid HDAC7 150 might contribute to chronic and low-grade liver inflammation, we utilised MacHDAC7 151 transgenic mice 30 that overexpress an alternatively spliced, inflammatory form of mouse 152 HDAC7 lacking the first 22 amino acids, HDAC7-u 41 , in myeloid cells. In MacHDAC7 mice, 153 

Csf1r promoter elements direct myeloid-specific expression of the transcriptional activator 154 GAL4-VP16, which in turn drives both HDAC7-u and cyan fluorescent protein (CFP) 155 expression in myeloid cells. Littermate MacBlue mice that express GAL4-VP16-dependent 156 CFP expression were used as controls in these studies 42 . We first evaluated the expression of 157 inflammatory mediators and markers of liver fibrosis in naïve MacBlue and MacHDAC7 mice. 158 As expected, mRNA levels of Hdac7 were significantly elevated in the livers of MacHDAC7 159 mice compared to those of MacBlue control mice ( Figure 1A ). Thus, the effect of myeloid-160 specific Hdac7 overexpression could be detected at the whole-organ level, even in the context 161 of endogenous Hdac7 expression in hepatocytes and other non-myeloid cells. Consistent with 162 the elevated systemic levels of inflammatory mediators observed upon LPS challenge in 163 MacHDAC7 mice 30 , mRNA levels of the inflammatory genes Ccl2 and Il1b were increased in 164 livers of these mice in a basal state ( Figure 1B-C ). Of the fibrotic genes that were examined, 165 only Mmp9 was increased ( Figure 1D ), with no difference being observed between the two 166 genotypes in mRNA levels of other inflammatory or fibrotic genes such as Crp , Fstl1 , Col1a1 167 and Act2 (Figure S1 ). Thus, myeloid Hdac7 overexpression increases levels of specific 168 inflammatory mediators in the liver, suggesting that it may prime liver inflammation and/or 169 accelerate liver disease under certain conditions. 170 171

Myeloid Hdac7 overexpression drives neutrophilia and perturbs haematopoiesis 213 

The immune profiling above was performed on mice aged 30-34 weeks, so it was conceivable 214 that the observed neutrophilia was an age-assocated phenomenon. To investigate this, we 215 assessed immune profiles of peripheral blood from 6-8 week old mice. Here we found that 216 MacHDAC7 mice exhibited pronounced leukocytosis (p < 0.01) and neutrophilia (p < 0.05) 217 compared with MacBlue controls, whereas there were no significant differences in monocytes, 218 eosinophils, basophils and lymphocytes ( Table 2 ). Young adult MacHDAC7 mice (6-10 week 219 old) also showed marked splenomegaly versus MacBlue control mice (~3‑fold increase in 220 spleen weight; p < 0.0001), with this accompanied by a substantial expansion of total 221 splenocytes and splenic immune subsets, including B220⁺ B cells, CD3⁺ T cells, and CD11b⁺ 222 myeloid cells (p < 0.0001) ( Table 3 ). Thus, MacHDAC7 mice exhibit marked splenomegaly 223 with broad immune‑cell expansion and circulating leukocytosis dominated by neutrophils, 224 indicative of a systemic immune‑primed state. 225 226 

Myeloid Hdac7 promotes hepatic inflammation but not fibrosis in mice 227 

Given that HDAC7 drives inflammatory responses in primary macrophages 30, 39 , we postulated 228 that myeloid Hdac7 overexpression alone may amplify diet-induced liver inflammation. Interestingly, this 231 difference was not apparent in mice fed a HFHCHS diet, suggesting that myeloid Hdac7 alone 232 primed liver inflammation. CCL2 and the accompanying infiltration of inflammatory 233 monocytes contributes to MASH in animal models 43, 44 . We therefore assessed circulating 234 CCL2 levels. Glucose metabolism is dysregulated in MacHDAC7 mice fed a HFHCHS diet 263 

Insulin resistance is a common metabolic syndrome of MASLD 45 . Thus, we measured fasted 264 blood glucose levels to assess whether myeloid Hdac7 overexpression regulates hepatic 265 glucose metabolism. When placed on a chow diet, MacHDAC7 mice consistently had lower 266 fasted blood glucose levels than the control MacBlue mice, with these effects being significant 267 at wk 4 and 16 ( Figure 4A ). To assess glucose clearance, we performed intraperitoneal glucose 268 tolerance tests (GTTs) at wk 12, finding that the MacHDAC7 mice cleared injected glucose 269 more rapidly than the MacBlue control, albeit the difference was not statistically significant 270 (Figure 4B ). However, these phenotypes were reversed when mice were fed a HFHCHS diet, 271 with higher blood glucose levels and slower glucose clearance observed in MacHDAC7 mice 272 (Figure 4C-D ). This suggests that myeloid HDAC7 has context-dependent roles in regulating 273 glucose metabolism. 274 275 In a fasting state, glucose is mainly produced in the liver via glycogenolysis and 276 gluconeogenesis, with the former converting glycogen into glucose 46 . Myeloid HDAC7 expression is associated with gene expression signatures linked to 353 

advanced CLD 354 

Our data support a role for HDAC7 in mediating glucose dysregulation in both gain- and -loss 355 of function mouse models, as well as liver inflammation in a gain-of-function mouse model. 356 We therefore considered that elevated HDAC7 expression in CLD may contribute to metabolic 357 dysregulation and/or disease progression. We thus assessed HDAC7 mRNA levels in liver 358 biopsies of people with early or advanced CLD 51 . Here we found that HDAC7 mRNA levels 359 were elevated in livers from people with advanced CLD by comparison to those with early 360 disease ( Figure 6A ), suggestive of a link between HDAC7 and human CLD severity. To further 361 investigate this link in myeloid cells, we performed spatial transcriptomic analysis on livers 362 from MacBlue and MacHDAC7 mice fed a chow diet to identify myeloid HDAC7-associated 363 transcriptional changes relevant to CLD. We first identified cells co-expressing eCFP and 364 

Hdac7 mRNAs in livers, then performed differential gene expression analysis on 365 

eCFP +/Hdac7 + cells from MacBlue versus MacHDAC7 mice. A heatmap of the 53 366 differentially expressed genes is shown in Figure 6B . This analysis confirmed elevated Il1b ,367 

Ccl2 and Mmp9 mRNA levels in hepatic myeloid cells from MacHDAC7 mice ( Figure 6B ), 368 consistent with our previous findings in whole liver tissue ( Figure 1B-D ). When compared 369 with our previously published dataset of genes associated with early and advanced human 370 CLD 51 , we found that eight genes associated with advanced CLD in human were also 371 differentially expressed in eCFP + cells in livers of MacHDAC7 mice. These include Vcan ,372 

Thbs1 , Mmp9 and Rab8b (Figure 6B ), several of which have been implicated in CLD 373 progression and/or pathology 52-54 . Spatial co-expression analysis revealed that these genes 374 were co-expressed with HDAC7, with limited expression in other hepatic cells ( Figure 6C-F ). 375 This suggests that myeloid HDAC7 expression is sufficient to direct gene expression programs 376 associated with advanced CLD. In summary, data presented in this study implicate myeloid 377 HDAC7 in driving hepatic inflammation and expression of genes linked to advanced CLD, as 378 well as systemic glucose dysregulation in a mouse obesity model. 379

Discussion 380 

In this study, we investigated potential roles of myeloid HDAC7 in metabolic diseases, 381 examining obesity, glucose dysregulation, hepatic inflammation and fibrosis. Although levels 382 of steatosis and fibrosis were variable in our studies ( Figure 3H-K, S4C-F, Table S1 ), 383 HFHCHS fed mice did develop diet-induced obesity ( Figure 2A ) and liver inflammation 384 (Figure 3F-G ). We therefore focused our studies on indicators of metabolism and 385 inflammation. We found the overexpression of myeloid Hdac7 promoted hepatic inflammation 386 (Figure 3 ) and expansion of leukocytes, particularly neutrophils ( Tables 1-2 ), in a basal state, 387 sharing some overlap with phenotypes observed in control MacBlue mice under HFHCHS. We 388 also observed that weight gain of MacHDAC7 mice fed either a chow or HFHCHS diet was 389 reduced by comparison to MacBlue mice ( Figure 2A ), which may relate to the energetic burden 390 required for immune cell expansion. These findings complicate interpretation of the effects of 391 HDAC7 on HFHCHS diet-induced weight gain, though we note that the relative increase in 392 body weight (HFHCHS versus chow diet) was significantly increased in MacHDAC7 versus 393 MacBlue mice ( Figure 2B ). Of note, in the HFHCHS diet model, glucose dysregulation was 394 exacerbated in myeloid HDAC7 gain-of-function mice and improved in myeloid HDAC7 loss-395 of-function mice. This key finding thus extends the functions of HDAC7 in macrophages 396 beyond cellular metabolism 30, 39, 40 to whole body glucose metabolism. 397 398 MacHDAC7 mice on a HFHCHS diet had increased hyperglycaemia at multiple time points 399 (Figure 4C ), as well as impaired glucose tolerance ( Figure 4D ), by comparison to MacBlue 400 control mice. Conversely, hyperglycaemia ( Figure 5A ) and glucose clearance ( Figure 5B )401 were improved in myeloid Hdac7 -/- mice on a HFHCHS diet. Collectively, these data implicate 402 myeloid HDAC7 as a contributor to glucose dysregulation during obesity. Interestingly, 403 MacHDAC7 mice on a chow diet had reduced levels of fasted blood glucose ( Figure 4A ) and 404 liver glycogen ( Figure 4E ), suggesting that glycogen metabolism is disrupted in these mice. 405 Under fasting conditions, glycogenolysis is initiated by glucagon and epinephrine-dependent 406 cascades 55 . Glycogen phosphorylase is a key enzyme controlling glycogenolysis, converting 407 glycogen to glucose-1-phosphate, which is subsequently converted to glucose-6-phosphate that 408 feeds glycolysis 56 . A previous study found that glycogen phosphorylase activity is controlled 409 by post-translational modifications. Specifically, acetylation at K470 inhibits its enzymatic 410 activity, with this inactivation mechanism being enhanced by insulin 57 . Ethics statement 484 

All animal studies were approved by The University of Queensland Animal Ethics Committee. 485 Experimental procedures followed the approved projects 2021/AE00629, 2021/AE00630 and 486 2021/AE001048. Mice were bred and housed on a 12 h-controlled day/night cycle with food 487 and water available ad libitum in the Queensland Biosciences Precinct animal house (Institute 488 for Molecular Bioscience, The University of Queensland). Animals 492 

6-10 wk old male MacHDAC7 mice and control MacBlue mice (total of n=14-15 per group) 493 were fed a HFHCHS diet containing 20% kcal fat, 20 g/kg cholesterol, 222 g/kg fructose and 494 107 g/kg sucrose (Specialty Feeds, SF21-160, containing sodium caseinate) for 24 wk. The 495 control groups (n=3 per genotype) were fed a chow diet for the same time. These experiments 496 were carried out as two independent trials. Alternatively, 8-12 wk old male Hdac7 -/- and wild 497 type control Hdac7 +/+ mice (n=12 per genotype) were fed a HFHCHS diet (Specialty Feeds, 498 SF18-074, same nutrient composition to SF21-160 except that it contains casein) for 36 wk. 499 Body weight was measured weekly throughout the trials. Fat and lean mass was measured 500 using a Minispec LF50 NMR Analyser (Bruker) one week before the end of the study. At the 501 termination of the study, mice were fasted for 6 or 12 h before being euthanised with isoflurane. 502 Blood was collected via cardiac puncture followed by perfusion with 0.9% saline solution 503 (Baxter). Liver, spleen, kidney and epididymal white adipose tissue were collected, weighed, 504 snap frozen, then fixed in 10% neutral buffered formalin or processed for RNA extraction. 505 Relative tissue weight gain for mice of each genotype was calculated by normalizing each 506 HFHCHS‑fed mouse to the mean chow‑fed tissue weight using the formula: [(HFHCHS tissue 507 weight − chow mean)/chow mean] × 100%. 508 509
