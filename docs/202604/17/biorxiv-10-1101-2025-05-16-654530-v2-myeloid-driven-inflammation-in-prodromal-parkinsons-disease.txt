Abstract 23 We hypothesized that prodromal Parkinson’s Disease (PD), characterized by REM Sleep 24 Behavior Disorder and hyposmia, is an inflammatory disorder initiated in the gut that evolves 25 into a neurodegenerative process manifest as classical PD. To test this hypothesis, we 26 performed single-cell RNAseq analysis of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) and blood from 118 27 individuals, comparing healthy subjects with prodromal PD and manifest PD to patients with the 28 central nervous system (CNS) autoimmune disease, multiple sclerosis (MS). Surprisingly, we 29 identified increased numbers of immune cells in the CSF of patients with prodromal PD. Single-30 cell RNA sequencing revealed increases in CSF-specific microglia-like macrophages with 31 enhanced JAK-STAT and TNF α signaling signatures in prodromal PD. CSF macrophages 32 exhibited similar transcriptional profiles to dural macrophages and gut muscularis macrophages 33 from α-synuclein-expressing PD model mice. Finally, shared memory T cell clones were found 34 in humans between gut and CSF T cells. These findings uncover a myeloid-mediated TNF α35 inflammatory process in the CNS of patients with prodromal PD, with an immunological linkage 36 between the gut and the CNS. 37 38

Introduction 39 Inflammation involving both innate and adaptive immune responses is a common feature 40 across a wide range of central nervous system (CNS) diseases. This most often arises as a 41 secondary response to neurodegeneration or tissue damage, although in certain adaptive T cell 42 -mediated conditions, such as multiple sclerosis (MS), immune activation itself can play a more 43 primary pathogenic role. Parkinson's Disease (PD) is a neurodegenerative disorder, 44 predominantly in older populations, characterized by the progressive loss of dopaminergic and 45 other neurons and the accumulation of α-synuclein aggregates in the brain. 1 Both genetic and 46 pathologic evidence support the notion that CNS inflammation contributes to PD pathogenesis. 2-47 6 Specifically, the presence of TNF α in PD microglia 7 and the recent discovery of autoreactive α-48 synuclein T cells in patients with early PD 8 raises the hypothesis that autoreactive T cells drive 49 early CNS inflammation in prodromal PD, as is seen in autoimmune, relapsing-remitting MS, 50 with later, secondary neurodegeneration manifesting as classic PD. 9-14 However, pathological 51 studies reveal only a minimal presence of CD8 + T cells in the substantia nigra of PD patients 52 early in disease pathogenesis 53 We previously identified myeloid cells in the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) that express 54 microglia-associated cell surface proteins, including APOE, C1Q, and TREM2, that are 55 presumably derived from a monocyte lineage. While first observed in higher numbers in the 56 CSF of patients with HIV, 15 they are also observed in the CSF of healthy subjects. These CSF 57 microglia-like macrophages share gene expression patterns with border-associated 58 macrophages located along the brain vasculature at the interface of the brain parenchyma. 16,17 59 Surprisingly, the frequency of this population of microglia-like macrophages was markedly 60 decreased in the CSF of patients with autoimmune, relapsing-remitting MS as compared to age-61 matched healthy controls. 18 With highly effective B cell depletion therapy, this population re-62 emerged in the CSF of MS patients, with associated increases in myeloid TNF α expression in 63 blood, suggesting an important role in autoimmune disease pathogenesis. 18 64 One early clinical manifestation of α-synucleinopathy is Rapid Eye Movement (REM) Sleep 65 Behavior Disorder (RBD), a parasomnia that can appear years or even decades before the 66 onset of typical symptoms of PD or Dementia with Lewy Bodies (DLB). 19 Thus, RBD serves as 67 an early indicator of α-synuclein-related neurodegeneration. There is strong epidemiologic data 68 indicating a link between autoimmune disorders, such as inflammatory bowel disease and PD, 69 adding further support for a prominent role for the immune system in mediating the inception 70 and progression PD pathology. 20,21 In addition, mounting evidence implicates the gut as an early 71 site of PD-related pathology, including α-synuclein accumulation and intestinal inflammation, 72

Single-cell immune atlas of PBMCs and CSF in prodromal and early stage of PD 93 To profile the immunological changes in prodromal and across PD stages, we generated a 94 single-cell RNAseq immune cell atlas from paired peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) 95 and CSF cells. Participants were categorized into four groups: RBD (n=36), PD without RBD 96 (described as PD; n=15), PD with RBD (described as PD-RBD; n=18), 27 patients with 97 relapsing remitting MS, age-matched healthy controls (HC; n=15 for PD, n=7 for MS) 98 (Supplementary Tables 1-3) who were separately compared to a previously published cohort of 99 age matched healthy controls. Clinical and demographic information was collected, and PBMCs 100 and CSF samples were obtained from all participants. The demographic and clinical 101 characteristics of our study population were similar to other cohorts (Supplementary Table 2). 102 The mean ages of the subjects in the four groups ranged from 64 to 89 years; 77% were males. 103 Of the RBD group, 19 (53%) were hyposmic, as defined by an UPSIT score below the 15 th 104 percentile for age and gender, 24 7 (19%) evidenced reduced dopamine transporter (DaT) 105 binding in the striatum, and 25 (69%) showed Synuclein Aggregating Activity (SAA) in their CSF. 106

Pronounced immunological changes in prodromal PD 143 To identify PD-specific immune alterations, we analyzed our single-cell immune atlas to 144 assess both shifts in cell frequencies and absolute cell numbers and alterations in gene 145 expression. To evaluate changes in cell frequencies, we used a Bayesian model 28 to compare 146 RBD, PD, and PD-RBD groups against age-matched healthy controls (Figs. 2a-c). RBD exhibits 147 heterogeneity in features such as hyposmia and CSF SAA status, both of which are known to 148 be associated with PD onset. 29 Using available demographic, clinical and imaging data, we 149 further stratified RBD individuals based on prodromal PD likelihood using the Movement 150 Disorders Society (MDS) online Prodromal PD Calculator. 25 This analysis revealed a significant 151 increase in the CSF Mac population in the RBD high-probability group (Figs. 2b,c). Additionally, 152 the absolute numbers of CSF Mac, CD4 + Tnaive, Tcm, and Tem cells in CSF were increased as 153 compared to healthy controls (Fig. 2d) while there was a decrease in the relative proportion of 154 CD4 + Tcm cells and CD8 + T effector memory/terminally differentiated effector memory 155 (em/emra) cells in the RBD group (FDR<0.05 and <0.1 respectively). As previously described, 30 156 we observed an increase in CD4 + T naive cells in the peripheral blood of prodromal PD 157 (Supplementary Fig. 6, FDR <0.05). 158 We next examined gene expression changes within each cluster (Supplementary Fig. 2a). 159 Compared to PBMCs, we observed more pronounced gene expression changes in CSF, 160 especially in CD4 + T cells, CD14 + monocytes, and the CSF Mac population. Notably, when 161 stratifying RBD patients by MDS Prodromal PD probability score, the RBD high-probability 162 group exhibited the highest number of differentially expressed genes (DEGs). These findings 163 suggest that both cell frequency and gene expression changes are most pronounced in the 164 RBD high-probability group, with relatively smaller differences observed in PD and PD-RBD. 165 Furthermore, in CSF, CD4 + T cells and CSF Mac demonstrated coordinated changes in both 166 cell numbers and gene expression. 167 To interpret these gene expression changes, we calculated scores for each cell population 168 across conditions using Hallmark gene sets from the Molecular Signatures Database (MSigDB) 169 (Fig. 2e, Supplementary Figs. 2b-f, Supplementary Tables 4,5). Inflammatory signature in MS versus RBD CSF 180 A major paradox in the field of autoimmunity revolves around the TNF α pathway, 181 presumably relating to the different genetic architectures of human autoimmune diseases. 31,32 182 Specifically, while anti-TNF α therapy is an effective treatment for Inflammatory Bowel Disease 183 (IBD) and rheumatoid arthritis (RA), it paradoxically induces exacerbations in patients with 184 MS. 33,34 Moreover, studies have shown an increased incidence of PD among persons with IBD 185 but a highly significant decrease in the incidence of PD in IBD patients on anti-TNF α186 therapy. 20,21 Based on these observations, we hypothesized that the CSF Mac population in 187 prodromal PD would be characterized by elevated TNF α signaling and exhibit a distinct gene 188 expression profile compared to CSF from patients with MS. We analyzed a new single-cell 189 dataset of CSF cells from 29 MS patients and 7 previously published age-matched healthy 190 individuals. As recently reported in a different MS dataset, 18 we observed a marked reduction in 191 the CSF Mac population that was confirmed with a recent meta-analysis of other MS datasets 192 (Fig. 2c). 17,18,35 Gene set enrichment analysis revealed a pattern opposite to that observed in 193 RBD, with strong enrichment of gene sets in T cells, B cells, and dendritic cells, while 194 enrichment in CSF macrophages was minimal (Fig. 2e, Supplementary Table 6). Notably, TNF α195 signaling via NF-κB showed a negative change in MS CSF Mac (Mean change =-0.716, 196 

p adj =4.75x10 -5 ). Furthermore, we applied (1) a detailed analysis pipeline specialized for 197 circulating CD4 + T cells 36,37 and (2) TCR-based estimation of T cell activity. 38 Both approaches 198 consistently suggested that T cells did not show evidence of global T cell activation in PD or 199 RBD (Supplementary Note and Supplementary Fig. 6-12). 200

Genetic prioritization of cell populations highlighted myeloid population in CSF 244 To determine whether integrating known genetic information with our immune cell atlas 245 could aid in prioritizing relevant cell populations in PD, we analyzed both polygenic and rare 246 variant components. First, we used scDRS 42 to integrate GWAS data associated with PD, 4247 RBD, 43 and LBD 44 traits, evaluating the enrichment of polygenic signals in individual cell types 248 (Supplementary Figs. 5a,b). The analysis revealed the strongest polygenic signal in CSF Mac 249 for LBD. Note that LBD 44 is a mixed proteinopathy that demonstrates genetic, clinical, and 250 pathological features in common with both PD and Alzheimer’s disease, and approximately half 251 of patients with RBD will go on to develop dementia with Lewy Bodies rather than motor 252 Parkinson’s disease, 45,46 consistent with it not being a pure synucleinopathy. For PD, significant 253 associations were observed in cDC2 and pDC in blood and pDC in CSF. No significant 254 associations were detected for RBD, although polygenic signals appeared to accumulate in 255 myeloid populations within the CSF. 256 

Enhanced cell-cell communication in the immune landscape of RBD 257 Next, we examined cell-cell interactions in the CSF using CellPhoneDB. 47 The ligand— 258 receptor interaction prediction suggested that myeloid cells, including CSF Mac and cDC2, 259 would exhibit robust ligand—receptor signals and that there would be more interactions 260 between lymphocytes and myeloid cells than among lymphocytes alone (Fig. 4a). Focusing on 261 the RBD group, where we observed the most pronounced immunological changes, we 262 investigated cell-cell interaction pairs with substantial shifts in signaling dynamics. We found 263 that ligands associated with signaling were moderately elevated ( FDR <0.2) in lymphocyte 264 subsets such as CD4 Tcm, NK CD16, and memory B cells, whereas receptor signals were 265 enhanced ( FDR <0.2) in myeloid subsets including cDC2 and CSF Mac (Fig. 4b). Notably, the 266 strongest elevation in receptor mediated signals occurred among myeloid cells themselves, 267 suggesting the presence of autonomous feedback activation. To explore these observations 268 further, we examined autonomous ligand—receptor pairs in CSF Mac and identified several 269 upregulated pairs in RBD, including C3 –C3AR1 , CD55 –ADGRE5 , and LGALS9 –HAVCR2 270 (Tim3), which are associated with immunoregulatory functions (Fig. 4c). SORL1 ) and prion protein ( PRNP –ADGRG6 ). 273 It is also known that aggregated α-synuclein can induce the activation of myeloid cells. 48 274 These ligand—receptor pairs may modulate the activation state of CSF macrophages and 275 influence the CNS microenvironment. An Immunological Gut–Brain Axis in Prodromal PD 282 Building on our previous work demonstrating a gut–brain axis mediated by gut-derived T 283 cells, 50 we analyzed paired PBMC, CSF, and gut samples from individuals with RBD, PD-RBD, 284 MS, and HC using single-cell RNA and TCR-seq (Fig. 5a, Supplementary Figs. 4d-e). Across 285 tissues, shared TCR clones were predominantly observed among CD8 effector memory T cells, 286 with clonal overlap detected between PBMC and gut, as well as between PBMC and CSF. 287 Although less frequent, shared clones spanning the gut and CSF were also identified, 288 suggesting a continuous T cell circulation axis linking the gut, peripheral blood, and central 289 nervous system. 290 Finally, a recent report suggested that intestinal macrophages modulate synucleinopathy in 291 a murine α-synucleinopathy model. 23 Re-analysis of muscularis macrophages from these mice 292 showed that intestinal macrophages display increased TNF α signaling pathway activity similar 293 to CSF Mac in human RBD individuals (Fig. 5b). Taken together, these findings support a model 294 in which gut α-synuclein aggregation and intestinal macrophage activation are immunologically 295 linked to CSF and border-associated macrophages through shared TNF α-driven inflammatory 296 programs, forming a coordinated gut–brain immune axis in prodromal PD (Fig. 5c). 297 

Discussion 298 While emerging evidence suggests an underlying inflammatory component in PD, as 299 demonstrated by the presence of α-synuclein autoreactive T cells in the circulation of patients 300 with manifest and prodromal PD, pathological studies reveal only a minimal presence of CD8 + T301 cells in the substantia nigra of PD patients early in disease pathogenesis. 51 To elucidate early 302 mechanisms of disease pathogenesis in PD, we performed single-cell RNAseq analysis of 303 paired CSF and blood from subjects with different stages of PD and compared them to age-304 matched healthy subjects and patients with MS. We found a previously unrecognized increase 305 in CSF immune cells that is most pronounced in patients with prodromal PD (RBD). Single-cell 306 RNAseq analysis revealed increased frequencies of a population of microglia-like macrophages, 307 termed CSF Mac, exhibiting inflammatory responses characterized by increased expression of 308 IL6-JAK-STAT3 and TNFRSF1A and TNFRSF1B signaling pathways, with a relative lack of T 309

Of note, inhibition of the 315 JAK/STAT pathway has been implicated in exerting protective effects in PD. 52-54 We recently 316 observed that treatment of patients with MS with B cell depletion leads to an exponential 317 increase of this CSF Mac population with increased secretion of TNF α by circulating 318 monocytes. 18 Taken together, these data suggest fundamental differences in the activity of 319 TNF α pathways between prodromal PD and MS. Moreover, these experimental results are 320 consistent with large-scale epidemiologic investigations of patients with IBD, demonstrating an 321 increased risk of PD in patients with IBD, and that anti-TNF α agents substantially decrease the 322 incidence of new-onset PD in this population. 20 Our observations comparing PD and MS are in 323 line with a fundamental observation of human inflammatory diseases. Specifically, the 324 therapeutic efficacy found with blocking an inflammatory pathway in one autoimmune disease 325 can lead to flare-ups in other autoimmune diseases. Two prominent examples include anti-IL-17 326 blockade, which has strong efficacy in treating psoriasis but has the risk of exacerbating IBD 55 327 and, as noted anti-TNF α blockade, an effective treatment in IBD and rheumatoid arthritis, can 328 cause exacerbations in patients with MS. 34 These dichotomous responses are likely related to 329 the different genetic architectures of autoimmune diseases, where a risk haplotype in one 330 disease can be protective in another. 31 331 Compared to the significant changes we observed in CSF myeloid populations, relatively 332 minor changes in T cell populations were noted in prodromal PD. Specifically, our analysis 333 replicated previous studies 30 showing increases in circulating and CSF naïve CD4 + T cells in 334 prodromal PD and PD with reductions in the proportion of CD4 + Tcm cells and CD8 + Temra cells 335 in CSF. These results are in contrast to T and B cell populations in patients with early 336 relapsing/remitting MS, which display markedly inflammatory signals in CSF T cells and in 337 parenchymal lesions which are relatively absent in prodromal PD. While prior studies have 338 identified α-synuclein–reactive T cells in patients with PD 8,10,12 , we did not observe evidence of 339 global T cell expansion or activation in the CSF of prodromal PD. This finding aligns with α-synuclein mouse models in which 344 aggregated α-synuclein in the gut leads to activation of both myeloid cells and reactive T cells 56 .345 Furthermore, the accompanying paper demonstrates the presence of activated T cells and 346 myeloid cells with a TNF α signature in dura, suggesting that the meningeal compartment is a 347 key anatomical immune niche in PD. 48 In this framework based on observations in both animal 348 models and in humans with prodromal PD, we hypothesize the disease originates from α-349 synuclein aggregation in the gut perhaps triggered by the microbiome inducing TNF α secreting 350 macrophages in the muscularis layer of the gut leading to: 1) propagation of α-synuclein 351 aggregates via the vagus nerve to the brain 57 ; and/or 2) trafficking of α-synuclein reactive T cells 352 between gut and dural sinuses that then; 3) mediate a myeloid-driven inflammatory response 353 characterized by TNF signaling across intestinal and then meningeal compartments into the 354 CNS (figure S13). 355 In summary, our integrated analysis of human CSF and blood, together with observations 356 in mouse models, supports a role for myeloid-driven inflammation in early PD pathogenesis. We 357 identified an increase in CSF immune cells in prodromal PD, marked by expansion of CSF 358 macrophages with TNF α and JAK/STAT pathway inflammatory signatures, in contrast to the T-359 cell driven inflammation observed in MS. Although α-synuclein-reactive T cells have been 360 previously identified, we did not find evidence of global T cell activation in CSF. However, we 361 did detect shared T cell clones between gut and CSF, alongside shared macrophage 362 inflammatory responses in intestinal and meningeal compartments. Within this framework, TNF α363 pathway-associated myeloid populations emerge as a central effector in PD pathogenesis, 364 providing a mechanistic rationale for therapeutic targeting of this pathway in prodromal PD. 365 Based on these data, we have initiated a clinical trial of an anti-TNF α monoclonal antibody in 366 patients with prodromal PD (ClinicalTrials.gov: NCT06996652). 367 368
