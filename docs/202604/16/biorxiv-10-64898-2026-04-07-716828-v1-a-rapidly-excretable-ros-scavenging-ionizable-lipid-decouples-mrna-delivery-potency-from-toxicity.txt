Introduction 41 

Messenger RNA (mRNA) therapeutics have emerged as a powerful modality for vaccination, protein 42 

replacement, cancer immunotherapy, and genome editing. The clinical s uccess of mRNA vaccines 43 

and RNA interference therapies has been enabled largely by lipid nanoparticles (LNPs), which 44 

protect nucleic acid cargo from degradation and facilitate intracellular delivery [1 -3] . Despite these 45 

advances, the broader application of mRNA -based therapeutics remains constrained by vector -46 

associated immunogenicity and dose -limiting toxicities, particularly following systemic or repeated 47 

administration [4 -7] .48 

LNPs are multicomponent systems typically composed of an ionizable lipid, phospholipid, 49 

cholesterol, and polyethylene glycol (PEG) lipid [8, 9] . Among these constituents, the ionizable lipid 50 

plays a central role in nanoparticle assembly, endosomal escape, and intracellular cargo release 51 

[10, 11] . At the same time, accumulating evidence indicates that ionizable lipids are also the primary 52 

drivers of LNP -induced innate immune activation and inflamma tory responses [12 -14] . Commercial 53 

and clinically validated ionizable lipids, including DLin -MC3 -DMA and SM -102, have been 54 

associated with cytokine induction, oxidative stress, and prolonged tissue retention, which can 55 

compromise safety margins and limit dosing flexibility [8, 15, 16] .56 

Multiple strategies have b een explored to mitigate LNP -associated immunogenicity, including 57 

chemical modification of mRNA, incorporation of biodegradable linkages, and rational lipid design 58 

to enhance endosomal escape while reducing inflammatory signaling [14, 15] . Recent studies have 59 

highlighted that ionizable lipid structure governs not only delivery potency but also intracellular stress 60 

responses, including reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation and membrane destabilization [17 -61 

19] . However, a molecular design strategy that simultaneously addresses immunogenicity, oxidative 62 

stress, and tissue accumulation while maintaining delivery potency has remained elusive [20, 21] .63 

In addition to systemic delivery, local administration of mRNA therapeutics presents distinct safety 64 

challenges. In the eye, which is considered an immune -privileged tissue, even transient 65

Here, we report the identification and characterization of a novel ionizable lipid, HHES, that enables 72

and non -human primates. Importantly, HHES -based formulations display minimal hepatic toxicity, 79

Results 90 

Design, Synthesis, and Physicochemical Characterization of HHES Lipids 91 

To develop ionizable lipids that combine efficient mRNA delivery with improved biodegradability and 92 

intrinsic antioxidant functionality, we designed a new class of sulfur -containing lipids incorporating a 93 

hexyl 2 -hydroxyethyl sulfide (HHES) motif. The HHES unit was rationally introduced to enable 94 

oxidative conversion into more hydrophilic metabolites, thereby faci litating metabolic clearance while 95 

maintaining favorable delivery properties ( Fig. 1a ). 96 

A combinatorial lipid library was synthesized by coupling diverse amine head groups with 97 

brominated acid linkers of varying alkyl chain lengths, allowing systematic mod ulation of ionization 98 

behavior and membrane interaction. All HHES lipids were obtained with high purity and correct 99 

molecular composition, as verified by LC –MS and NMR analyses ( Supplementary Figs. 1 –9). 100 

Chromatographic and spectroscopic characterization confirmed successful synthesis of the 101 

designed structures with minimal impurities, supporting the robustness of the synthetic platform. 102 

The synthesized lipids were formulated into LNPs using a microfluidic mixing system. The optimized 103 

HHES LNP formulation c onsisted of HHES ionizable lipid, DOPE, cholesterol, and C16 -PEG 104 

ceramide at a molar ratio of 26.5:20:52:1.5. Under these conditions, all HHES LNPs formed uniform 105 

nanoparticles with narrow size distribution and high mRNA encapsulation efficiency (>90%) (Fi g. 1b; 106 

Supplementary Table 1 ). Measurement of ionization behavior using a TNS fluorescence assay 107 

showed an apparent pKa of 6.81, indicating suitable protonation characteristics for endosomal 108 

escape under acidic conditions (Supplementary Fig. 10 ). Cryogenic transmission electron 109 

microscopy revealed spherical particles with multilamellar internal organization, consistent with 110 

stable mRNA -loaded LNP formation ( Supplementary Fig. 11 ). 111

HHES Lipids Exhibit Intrinsic Antioxidant Activity Under Oxidative Stress 134 

Because t he HHES ionizable lipid contains a sulfur -bearing moiety designed to modulate ROS, we 135 

evaluated its impact on intracellular oxidative stress using a DCFH -DA assay. Cells were first 136 

exposed to H ₂O₂ (200 μM) for 16 h, followed by treatment with mRNA -loaded LNPs for an additional 137 

16 h . Treatment with SM -102 or MC3 LNPs resulted in a pronounced increase in DCF fluorescence 138 

relative to the H ₂O₂-only condition ( Fig. 2a ), suggesting that these ionizable l ipids further exacerbate 139 

oxidative stress, consistent with previously reported membrane -destabilizing effects of conventional 140 

ionizable lipids. In contrast, ROS levels maintained comparable to those of the H ₂O₂-only control 141 

after HHES LNP treatment . At 0.5 μg/mL, SM -102 and MC3 LNPs elevated intracellular ROS levels 142 

to approximately 20 .10 % and 7 .18 % DCF -positive cells, respectively, whereas HHES LNP -treated 143 

cells showed negligible ROS accumulation (~ 0.48 %) (Fig. 2b ). At the higher dose of 1.0 μg/mL, SM -144 

102 and MC3 further increased ROS levels to ~23 .16 % and ~18 .05 %, respectively, while HHES -145 

treated cells remained at ~ 1.95 %, retaining a markedly lower oxidative burden compared with both 146 

benchmark lipids ( Fig. 2c ). Notably, the gap between SM -102 and MC3 narro wed at the higher dose, 147 

whereas HHES maintained superior antioxidant activity across both concentrations tested. 148 

To confirm the oxidative responsiveness of the HHES motif at the molecular level, we examined 149 

chemical changes under oxidizing conditions. FT -IR analysis showed the emergence of a150 

characteristic S=O stretching band after H ₂O₂ treatment, consistent with oxidation of the thioether 151 

group to sulfoxide and sulfone derivatives, confirming that the HHES sulfur moiety acts as a direct 152 

ROS scavenger through oxidative conversion ( Supplementary Fig. 12 ). This ROS -suppressing 153 

activity was further validated in HepG2 cells, supporting the intrinsic antioxidant capacity of HHES 154

HHES Lipids and Their Metabolites Modulate Membrane Interaction and Endosomal 157 

Processing 158 

The interaction of LNPs with endosomal membranes critically influences the intracellular fate of 159 

mRNA cargo and subsequent cellular responses. Following cellular u ptake via endocytosis, LNPs 160 

are trafficked through early endosomes, where only a limited fraction — estimated at less than 5% 161 

of internalized particles — undergoes successful endosomal escape to release mRNA into the 162 

cytosol [28, 29] . Because endosomal escape efficiency is inherently low, the majority of internalized 163 

LNPs progr ess to late endosomes, where the increasingly acidic environment promotes degradation 164 

of ionizable lipids into smaller metabolites [30, 31] . These degradation products can interact with 165 

endosomal membranes, and the extent of membrane perturbation may influence downstream 166 

cellular stress responses, including inflammatory signaling [32] . The proposed intracellular 167 

processing mechanism is illustrated in Fig. 2d .168 

To characterize membrane interaction behavi or, we performed a time -resolved FRET -based lipid 169 

fusion assay using endosome -mimicking liposomes. HHES and SM -102 LNPs exhibited comparable 170 

lipid fusion kinetics, reaching maximum fusion efficiencies of ~2 2.26 % and ~2 0.33 %, respectively, 171 

both substantiall y greater than MC3 LNPs (~1 4.31 %) (Fig. 2e ). Because lipid fusion is associated 172 

with membrane destabilization events that facilitate endosomal escape, these results are consistent 173 

with efficient intracellular mRNA delivery for both HHES and SM -102 formulations. 174 

We next examined membrane interaction of the corresponding ionizable lipid metabolites, HHESm, 175 

SM -102m (heptadecan -9-ol), and MC3m (Dlin -MeOH), generated following degradation. In contrast 176 

to intact LNPs, metabolite -mediated fusion showed a markedly distinct trend: SM -102m reached 177 

~2 3.78 %, MC3m ~1 2.39 %, whereas HHESm exhibited substantially attenuated fusion activity of 178 

only ~6 .82 % (Fig. 2f ). The dramatically reduced membrane interaction of HHESm compared with 179 

SM -102m and MC3m suggests limite d post -degradation membrane perturbation. Given that 180 

excessive endosomal membrane disruption has been linked to cellular stress and inflammatory 181 

signaling, the attenuated membrane activity of HHES metabolites may contribute to the improved 182 

biocompatibility observed with HHES LNPs. 183 

Together, these findings indicate that HHES preserves membrane fusion capacity required for 184 

efficient mRNA delivery while minimizing membrane perturbation following lipid degradation, thereby 185 

decoupling delivery efficiency from st ress -associated downstream effects. 186

Rapid Biodistribution and Clearance of HHES Ionizable Lipids 188 

To investigate the in vivo biodistribution and clearance behavior of HHES LNPs, we performed 189 

quantitative PET/CT imaging following intravenous administration of ⁶⁴Cu -labeled LNPs in 190 

C57BL/6N mice. A ⁶⁴Cu -labeled PEG -lipid (⁶⁴Cu –NOTA –DSPE -PEG) was incorporated into the LNP 191 

formulati on to enable noninvasive tracking of nanoparticle distribution in vivo (Fig. 3a ). Following 192 

injection, HHES LNPs rapidly accumulated in the liver, reaching a peak hepatic signal of ~2 1.77 %193 

ID/g by 4 h post -injection, which progressively declined to ~1 4.03 % ID/g at 24 h and ~ 9.21 % ID/g at 194 

48 h, indicating efficient systemic clearance (Fig. 3b, c ). 195 

To further characterize the clearance kinetics at the molecular level, we quantified plasma and 196 

hepatic ionizable lipid concentrations by LC –MS/MS following intra venous administration of 197 

mRNA/LNPs in male BALB/c mice (6 –8 weeks old). Plasma pharmacokinetic analysis at 0.5 mg/kg 198 

revealed markedly accelerated elimination of HHES compared with MC3: initial plasma 199 

concentrations at 0.25 h post -injection were 2,905 ng/m L for HHES versus 17,327 ng/mL for MC3, 200 

and HHES levels declined to undetectable levels by 24 h, whereas MC3 remained detectable at 361 201 

ng/mL at the same time point ( Fig. 3d ). 202 

Hepatic pharmacokinetic analysis at 1 .0 mg/kg further confirmed the rapid clearance profile of HHES. 203 

MC3 exhibited a distinct hepatic accumulation phase, reaching peak concentration (Cmax = 85,774 204 

ng/mL) at 3 h post -injection, with a prolonged half -life of 44.7 h and total hepatic exposure (AUC ) of 205 

4,010,006 ng/mL·h. In contrast, HHES reached peak hepatic concentration of only 14,236 ng/mL at 206 

0.5 h post -injection — approximately 6 -fold lower than MC3 — and was rapidly eliminated, with a 207 

half -life of 13.4 h and AUC of 139,017 ng/mL·h, representin g a 3.3 -fold shorter half -life and 208 

approximately 29 -fold lower total hepatic exposure compared with MC3 ( Fig. 3e ). 209 

Together, these results demonstrate that HHES undergoes substantially faster systemic and hepatic 210 

clearance than MC3, consistent with its ra pid metabolic conversion to hydrophilic species. Notably, 211 

unlike conventional ionizable lipids such as MC3 and SM -102, which exhibit transient hepatic 212 

accumulation even when biodegradable functionalities are incorporated [15]  , HHES displayed no 213 

evident liver accumulation phase, instead undergoing rapid reduction in hepatic levels following 214 

initial uptake, further distinguishing it as a next -generation ionizable lipid with superior clearance 215 

properties. 216

Discussion 462 

The present study introduces HHES as a dual -function ionizable lipid that simultaneously addresses 463 

two persistent lia bilities of LNP -based mRNA therapeutics: vector -associated oxidative stress and 464 

prolonged tissue accumulation. The sulfur -bearing HHES motif confers intrinsic ROS -scavenging 465 

activity through direct thioether oxidation, as confirmed by FT -IR spectroscopy, w hile its hydrophilic 466 

metabolites exhibit markedly attenuated endosomal membrane perturbation compared with those 467 

of SM -102 and MC3. This mechanistic combination, in which preserved membrane fusion for 468 

endosomal escape is coupled with reduced post -degradati on membrane disruption, provides a 469 

molecular basis for the observed decoupling of mRNA delivery potency from inflammatory signaling, 470 

a challenge that has constrained the clinical application of existing ionizable lipid platforms .471 

A notable feature of HHES pharmacokinetics is the apparent paradox of accelerated hepatic 472 

clearance concurrent with enhanced protein expression. We propose that efficient initial hepatic 473 

uptake of HHES LNPs enables productive mRNA delivery, after which rapid oxidative metabolism 474 

of the lipid limits cumulative tissue burden without compromising translational output. This 475 

interpretation is consistent with the 3.3 -fold shorter hepatic half -life, 29 -fold lower total hepatic 476 

exposure, and absence of a hepatic accumulation phase relative to MC3, together with higher peak 477 

serum hEPO levels at equivalent mRNA doses. The safety implications of this clearance profile were 478 

borne out in both rat repeated -dose studies and cynomolgus monkey studies, where HHES 479 

maintained hepatic enzyme levels, inf lammatory markers, and hematological parameters within 480 

normal ranges across multiple administrations, a tolerability profile not achieved by benchmark 481 

ionizable lipids at comparable doses .482 

The immune -privileged and anatomically confined nature of the subretinal space places exceptional 483 

demands on delivery vector safety, as transient inflammatory responses can produce irreversible 484 

photoreceptor loss. The superior retinal tolerability of HHES LNPs relative to MC3 and SM -102, as 485 

evidenced by preserved retinal architecture, minimal microglial activation, and intact ERG responses, 486 

likely reflects the same attenuated metabolite -mediated membrane perturbation observed in vitro .487 

Critically, this safe ty advantage was not achieved at the cost of delivery efficacy: HHES LNPs 488 

supported SpCas9 -mediated indel formation at 42.62% and ABE8e -mediated base editing at 57.07% 489 

in RPE cells following subretinal injection. These efficiencies compare favorably with p reviously 490 

reported non -viral platforms [35 -37] . The editing efficiencies achieved here were sufficient to 491 

suppress laser -induced CNV in both SpCas9 and ABE8e cohort s, demonstrating clear therapeutic 492 

benefit in a clinically relevant model of neovascular AMD. The consistent therapeutic outcomes 493 

across two mechanistically distinct editing modalities underscore the versatility of the platform. RPE -494 

selective transfection was further validated in human ESC -derived RPE cells, which closely 495
