## Abstract 26 

Chronic pyelonephritis is characterized by persistent bacterial infection of the kidney and a 27 

dysregulated immune response that promotes disease progression. Macrophages are central 28 

regulators of antibacterial immunity in the urinary tract. However, the mechanisms underlying 29 

their functional reprogramming in chronic infection remain poorly understood. Here, we identify 30 

lactate as a key metabolic determinant of macrophage polarization in human pyelonephritis. 31 

Proteomic analysis of human kidney tissue revealed extensive metabolic remodelling, 32 

including upregulation of enzymes involved in glycolysis. Consistent with this, lactate levels 33 

were significantly elevated in urine and plasma of pyelonephritis patients. Concomitantly, we 34 

observed a pronounced accumulation of CD163 macrophages in infected kidneys, 35 

representing a distinct macrophage subset with immunomodulatory function. Correlation-36 

based network analysis revealed a strong association between CD163 and lactate 37 

dehydrogenase A, supporting a functional association between lactate metabolism and 38 

macrophage polarization. Mechanistically, exposure of murine bone marrow-derived 39 

macrophages to lactate induced intracellular protein lactylation and promoted polarization 40 

toward a CD163 phenotype, defining a metabolically imprinted macrophage state distinct from 41 

classical activation paradigms. Proteomic profiling demonstrated extensive remodelling of 42 

macrophage protein expression in response to lactate with significant alteration of mediators 43 

of phagocytosis, Toll-like receptor signalling, and interferon response. Together, these findings 44 

identify lactate as a potent metabolic driver of macrophage reprogramming and establish a 45 

foundation for investigating lactylation-dependent immune dysfunction in chronic 46 

pyelonephritis. 47

## Introduction 51 

Macrophages are highly plastic immune cells that dynamically adapt their phenotype and 52 

function in response to local tissue cues, thereby shaping the outcome of bacterial infections 1,2 .53 

In the urinary tract, macrophages orchestrate antibacterial defence, tissue repair, and 54 

resolution of inflammation through tightly regulated spatial and temporal programs 3-5 . However, 55 

persistent bacterial infections of the kidney, such as chronic pyelonephritis (PN), are frequently 56 

associated with dysregulated macrophage responses that favour immune suppression and 57 

disease persistence 6. Among macrophage subsets, CD163 ⁺ macrophages are associated with 58 

anti-inflammatory, scavenging, and tissue-protective functions 7,8 . While these programs are 59 

beneficial during resolution phases, their sustained activation during ongoing bacterial infection 60 

may impair effective host defence. Indeed, CD163 expression is linked to immunoregulatory 61 

macrophage states across multiple inflammatory and infectious contexts 9. However, the 62 

mechanisms that drive the accumulation and functional polarization of CD163 ⁺ macrophages 63 

in chronic kidney infection remain poorly understood. 64 

Emerging evidence indicates that metabolic cues within inflamed tissues act as 65 

instructive signals for macrophage polarization 10 . Lactate, long considered a metabolic waste 66 

product, has recently been recognized as a signalling metabolite capable of reshaping immune 67 

cell function through receptor-mediated pathways, metabolic rewiring, and protein 68 

lactylation 11,12 . Histone lactylation has been shown to directly regulate gene expression 69 

programs associated with macrophage activation states 11 . However, whether lactate-driven 70 

macrophage reprogramming contributes to immune dysfunction and disease persistence in 71 

PN is currently unknown. 72 

Lactate availability in macrophages is governed by the balance between glycolytic flux, 73 

mitochondrial pyruvate oxidation, and pyruvate-to-lactate conversion via lactate 74 

dehydrogenase A (LDHA) 13 . LDHA catalyses the conversion of pyruvate to lactate and thereby 75 

controls intracellular lactate pools that serve as substrates for protein- and histone lactylation. 76 

Importantly, modulation of LDHA activity alters lactate flux without directly impairing 77 

mitochondrial acetyl-CoA generation, which can be sustained through alternative metabolic 78 

pathways, such as amino acid and fatty acid metabolisms 13,14 . This metabolic architecture 79 

enables selective manipulation of lactate-dependent signalling while largely preserving global 80 

cellular viability and acetylation capacity. Our study identifies a lactate–dependent lactylation 81 

axis as a central mechanism linking metabolic tissue remodelling to macrophage-mediated 82 

polarisation in chronic PN. 83

## Results 85 

To characterize metabolic alterations in human PN, we performed LC-MS/MS–based 86 

proteomic analysis of kidney tissue from PN patients and control donors. Principal component 87 

(PC) analysis of the total kidney proteome indicated PN-specific changes (Figure 1A). 88 

Pathway-enrichment analysis revealed coordinated upregulation of the innate immune 89 

response, whereas mitochondrial proteins involved in small molecule catabolism and 90 

mitochondrial ATP synthesis were downregulated, indicating substantial metabolic remodelling 91 

in infected kidneys (Figure 1B). Notably, enzymes associated with glycolytic flux were 92 

upregulated, suggesting a shift toward enhanced aerobic glycolysis (Figure 1C–D). Consistent 93 

with this metabolic phenotype, lactate concentrations were significantly elevated in both urine 94 

and plasma samples from PN patients compared to healthy controls (Figure 1E–F). These 95 

findings indicate that PN is characterized by a lactate-enriched systemic and local 96 

microenvironment. 97

To define molecular and spatial immune cell alterations in PN, we performed further 98 

proteomic and microscopy analysis. Among immune-associated proteins, CD163 emerged as 99 

one of the most strongly upregulated markers in PN tissue (Figure 2A). Immunohistochemical 100 

analysis confirmed a significant increase in CD163 ⁺ macrophages in infected kidneys 101 

compared to controls (Figure 2B–C). To further characterize these cells, we performed 102 

multiplex fluorescence microscopy, enabling simultaneous visualization of multiple immune 103 

cell populations, including markers of pro-inflammatory effector (CD11b, CXCR4, IFN-γ, 104 

iNOS), monocyte-derived (CD14), homeostatic/scavenging (CD11c, CD206, CD209, IRF4), 105 

and antigen-presenting cells (HLA-DR). CD68 ⁺ macrophages were abundant in the infected 106 

renal tissue with abundant frequency expressing CD163 (Figure 2D). Moreover, we observed 107 

a broad upregulation of functional markers, such as the pattern recognition receptor DC-SIGN 108 

(CD209), by CD163 + CD68 + macrophages (Figure 2E), supporting the concept that lactate 109 

environment in PN induce a distinct macrophage state with increased functional plasticity and 110 

transcriptional activity. Thus, these data identify CD163 ⁺ macrophages as a distinct and 111 

prominent immune cell population in human PN. 112 

To investigate the relationship between macrophage polarization and metabolic 113 

remodelling, we performed correlation-based network analysis of the renal proteome. Proteins 114 

positively associated with CD163 abundance formed a coherent interaction network enriched 115 

for metabolic pathways (Figure 3A). Notably, lactate dehydrogenase A (LDHA), a key enzyme 116 

controlling lactate production, showed a strong positive correlation with CD163 expression 117 

across all analysed kidney samples, Pearson r >0.78 (Figure 3B). This association suggests 118 

that lactate metabolism is functionally linked to the emergence of CD163 ⁺ macrophages in the 119 

infected kidney. 120 

To determine whether lactate directly influences the macrophage phenotype, we 121 

performed in vitro experiments using murine bone marrow–derived macrophages (BMDMs). 122 

Cells were exposed to increasing concentrations of lactate to mimic the lactate-enriched 123 

environment in PN. Lactate treatment resulted in a significant increase in the proportion of 124 

CD163 ⁺ macrophages, accompanied by elevated intracellular protein lactylation as measured 125 

by flow cytometry (Figure 4A). Principal component analysis of ANOVA-regulated proteins 126 

quantified by LC-MS demonstrated clear separation of lactate-treated macrophages from 127 

classical pro-inflammatory (LPS/IFN-γ) and alternatively activated (IL-4/IL-13) cell states 128 

(Figure 4B). Proteomic analysis revealed extensive remodelling of these macrophages in 129 

response to lactate stimulation (Figure 4C). Differentially expressed proteins included 130 

regulators of immune cell identity, phagocytosis, TLR signalling, leukocyte migration, and 131 

cytokine production (Figure 4C). Upregulated proteins were enriched for factors of the 132 

response to interferon-α and -β, whereas regulators of the mitotic cell cycle and protein 133 

translation were decreased, indicative of terminally differentiated macrophages (Figure 4D). 134 

Together, these findings demonstrate that lactate induces a specific macrophage program, 135 

which is characterized by increased protein lactylation and polarization toward a CD163 ⁺136 

immunoregulatory phenotype. 137

## Discussion 139 

In this study, we identify lactate as a central metabolic determinant of macrophage polarization 140 

in chronic PN and provide evidence that lactate-driven protein lactylation promotes the 141 

emergence of CD163 ⁺ macrophages with altered immunoregulatory function. By integrating 142 

human proteomics and spatial imaging, our findings establish a direct link between metabolic 143 

tissue remodelling and macrophage-mediated immune dysfunction in chronic kidney infection. 144

Our data demonstrate that PN is associated with profound metabolic rewiring 145 

characterized by enhanced glycolysis and elevated lactate levels. These findings are 146 

consistent with previous studies showing that inflamed and hypoxic tissues accumulate lactate 147 

due to increased glycolytic flux and altered mitochondrial function 14,15 . Importantly, lactate is 148 

increasingly recognized as an active signalling metabolite that regulates immune cell 149 

behaviour rather than merely reflecting metabolic stress 12 . A key finding of our study is the 150 

enrichment of CD163 ⁺ macrophages in PN tissue and their association with lactate 151 

metabolism. CD163 ⁺ macrophages have been widely described as anti-inflammatory and 152 

tissue-remodelling cells, often linked to resolution of inflammation and fibrosis 8. However, in 153 

the context of persistent infection, such regulatory programs may become maladaptive and 154 

contribute to ineffective bacterial clearance. Our data extend this concept by identifying lactate 155 

as a potential upstream driver of CD163 ⁺ macrophage polarization in human PN. 156 

Mechanistically, we show that lactate directly induces intracellular protein lactylation 157 

and drives macrophage reprogramming toward a CD163 ⁺ phenotype. These findings support 158 

the description of histone lactylation as a novel epigenetic modification linking metabolism to 159 

gene regulation in macrophages 11 . Subsequent studies have further demonstrated that 160 

lactylation can regulate inflammatory gene expression and immune cell differentiation 12,16 . Our 161 

results expand this framework by linking lactylation to impaired antibacterial effector functions 162 

in macrophages, including alterations in pathways related to phagocytosis, reactive oxygen 163 

species production, and cytokine responses. 164 

Collectively, our findings position lactate not merely as a by-product of inflammation but 165 

as a central regulator of immune cell fate in chronic infection, which promote immune 166 

suppression and disease progression 17,18 . Thus, targeting lactate production, transport (e.g., 167 

monocarboxylate transporters), or lactylation pathways may represent a novel strategy to 168 

restore antimicrobial immunity in chronic PN. 169

were determined through Student’s t-testing. Proteins, identified with a p-value < 0.05 and at 354 

least 2-log 2(fold change) difference between groups, were considered significantly regulated 355 

and subjected to further enrichment analysis using the STRING database (v12.0). 356
