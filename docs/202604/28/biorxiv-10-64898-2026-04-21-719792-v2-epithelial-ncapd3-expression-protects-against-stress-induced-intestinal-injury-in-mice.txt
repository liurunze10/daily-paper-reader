Abstract 24 

Intestinal epithelial cells are central players in mucosal barrier integrity and host-microbe interactions. Genetic 25 studies have revealed that epithelial dysfunction is a key contributor to the pathogenesis of inflammatory bowel 26 disease. Non-SMC condensin II complex subunit D3 (NCAPD3) is essential for chromatin organization and 27 stability. NCAPD3 also promotes antimicrobial defense and autophagy responses in vitro . NCAPD3 expression 28 is decreased in intestinal epithelial cells from patients with ulcerative colitis; however, it is not known whether 29 loss of NCAPD3 expression drives intestinal barrier dysfunction or is a result of disease-associated 30 inflammation. To investigate this relationship in vivo , a tissue-specific approach was required, as global 31 constitutive knockout of NCAPD3 is embryonic lethal. Therefore, a transgenic mouse line with doxycycline-32 inducible expression of a short hairpin RNA targeting NCAPD3 restricted to villin-expressing cells was 33 generated (NCAPD3KD mice) to enable the study of NCAPD3 function in the intestinal epithelium. Treatment 34 of NCAPD3KD mice with 9-tert-butyl doxycycline resulted in ~75% reduction of NCAPD3 protein in EpCAM ⁺35 intestinal cells. Short-term epithelial NCAPD3 knockdown did not induce spontaneous colitis but was 36 associated with increased serum amyloid A and a trend towards increased intestinal permeability. Upon 37 dextran sodium sulfate or Salmonella enterica serovar Typhimurium ΔAroA challenge, NCAPD3KD mice 38 exhibited exacerbated weight loss, higher disease activity, increased histopathological damage, abnormal 39 colonic cytokines and chemokines, and significantly increased intestinal permeability. These results indicate 40 that NCAPD3 expression in the intestinal epithelium is required for optimal barrier maintenance and 41 antimicrobial defense under chemical or microbial stress. These findings support prior in vitro observations and 42 solidify NCAPD3 as a regulator of intestinal epithelial barrier function and mucosal host defense. 43 44

Introduction 59 The intestinal epithelium is a vital single-cell barrier between the host and the external environment, consisting 60 of a diverse range of specialized intestinal epithelial cells (IECs) (1). This dynamic interface allows the 61 absorption of nutrients and fluids, provides innate immune defenses, and helps maintain a homeostatic 62 balance with the gut microbiota (2). The epithelium provides a physical barrier, with intercellular tight junctions 63 responsible for maintaining a seal between adjacent cells (3). Additionally, IECs convey signals to mucosal 64 immune cells and help coordinate differential immune responses to commensal and pathogenic bacteria. 65 Defects in epithelial barrier integrity are a hallmark of inflammatory bowel diseases (IBD), such as ulcerative 66 colitis (UC) and Crohn’s disease (CD), and render the host susceptible to enteric infections (2). 67 

In the context of IBD, disruption and dysregulation of IEC function are key drivers of chronic intestinal 68 inflammation (4). Defects in epithelial barrier integrity, including altered tight junction architecture, impaired 69 mucus production, and reduced antimicrobial peptide secretion, lead to increased intestinal permeability and 70 inappropriate exposure of the mucosal immune system to luminal microbes and antigens. Increased exposure 71 of the host tissues to microbial products, from a so-called “leaky gut”, leads to chronic inflammation. Beyond 72 barrier failure, IECs in IBD display aberrant innate immune signaling, stress responses, and altered 73 interactions with the microbiota, all of which contribute to sustained inflammatory responses characteristic of 74 IBD. Importantly, IBD has a clear genetic component, with genome-wide association studies identifying over 75 250 risk loci, many of which are directly linked to epithelial cell function (5). Non-SMC condensin II complex subunit D3 (NCAPD3) is a core component of the condensin II 79 complex, which promotes chromatin condensation during mitotic initiation (6) and maintains genomic stability 80 (7). Beyond its canonical nuclear functions, NCAPD3 has been implicated in retrotransposon silencing (8), 81 mitochondrial redox (9), and antimicrobial defenses (10, 11). Consequently, the dysregulation of NCAPD3 has 82 been associated with multiple human pathologies. Loss-of-function mutations drive microcephaly (12), while 83 overexpression has been associated with cancer progression (13-19). Critically, reduced NCAPD3 expression 84 has been observed in intestinal epithelial cells isolated from UC patients (11). However, despite evidence from 85 cell-based and ex vivo analyses of intestinal epithelial cells, the role of NCAPD3 in maintaining intestinal 86 epithelial homeostasis in vivo remains uncharacterized. Specifically, it is unclear how loss or dysregulation of 87 NCAPD3 affects epithelial integrity or susceptibility to intestinal inflammation within an organismal context. 88 To directly define the role of NCAPD3 in the intestinal epithelium in vivo , we created an inducible, 89 intestinal epithelial cell-specific NCAPD3 knockdown mouse model. Using this model, we examined whether 90 epithelial NCAPD3 is necessary to maintain intestinal homeostasis or to protect the intestinal barrier during 91 inflammatory or infectious challenges. Specifically, we assessed the effects of NCAPD3 depletion on intestinal 92 permeability, susceptibility to chemically induced epithelial injury, and host defense against enteric bacterial 93 infection. Our studies provide evidence that NCAPD3 expression in the intestinal epithelium is essential for 94 optimal barrier integrity and antimicrobial defense under stress from chemical or microbial challenges. These 95 findings underscore the important role of NCAPD3 in maintaining intestinal health. 96

Discussion 90 

In this study, we generated and validated a novel inducible, intestinal epithelial cell-specific NCAPD3 91 knockdown mouse model and used it to investigate the in vivo role of NCAPD3 in intestinal barrier 92 homeostasis. Using a villin-rtTA driven shRNA system targeted to the Col1a1 safe harbor locus, we achieved 93 robust epithelial-specific reduction of NCAPD3 following induction with the non-antimicrobial tetracycline 94 analogue 9-TB-Dox. This in vivo mammalian model enables mechanistic investigation of the observation that 95 UC patients have reduced NCAPD3 expression specifically in intestinal epithelial cells (11). Our data support 96 the interpretation that this epithelial NCAPD3 reduction is not a secondary consequence of inflammation but 97 may contribute to epithelial dysfunction and disease pathogenesis. 98 Despite efficient (~75%) epithelial knockdown over 10 days, NCAPD3KD mice did not develop 99 spontaneous colitis. However, the trend toward increased intestinal permeability, elevated circulating flagellin 00 levels, and significantly increased SAA suggest that NCAPD3 loss subtly compromises epithelial barrier 01 integrity, even in the absence of overt histological inflammation. Increased intestinal permeability is 02 increasingly recognized as an early event in IBD, detectable even in quiescent disease and in individuals prior 03 to clinical onset (23-25). The observation that NCAPD3KD mice exhibit low-level permeability and systemic 04 inflammation without visible tissue injury supports the concept that NCAPD3 loss can contribute to epithelial 05 barrier weakening and predispose one to inflammatory conditions. While this study examined only short-term 06 knockdown, chronic NCAPD3 deficiency may exacerbate this basal inflammatory tone and potentially lead to 07 spontaneous disease. Extended knockdown studies will be required to determine whether long-term NCAPD3 08 reduction alone is sufficient to break the intestinal barrier and drive intestinal pathology. 09 The increase in SAA in NCAPD3KD mice, even at baseline, is notable. SAA is not merely a marker of 10 inflammation but an active participant in inflammatory processes, capable of inducing cytokine production, 11

In this context, elevated SAA in the 12 absence of overt injury may reflect ongoing low-level epithelial stress and induction of repair mechanisms 13 triggered by a subtle compromise to the barrier. Importantly, SAA levels were further amplified following DSS 14 exposure, consistent with an exaggerated systemic response when the compromised epithelium encounters an 15 additional insult. 16 When challenged with DSS or an enteric Salmonella infection, NCAPD3KD mice exhibited significantly 17 heightened disease activity, epithelial damage, inflammatory cytokine production, and intestinal permeability 18 compared to controls. The amplification of permeability and inflammation in these settings aligns with the 19 widely proposed “two-hit hypothesis” for IBD pathogenesis, where a genetically predisposed individual may 20 have epithelial vulnerability and mild barrier dysfunction that, when coupled with environmental triggers, can 21 lead to an uncontrolled inflammatory state (27). In this context, reduced NCAPD3 expression may represent a 22 susceptibility factor that does not independently cause disease but sensitizes the host to secondary insults. 23 During DSS-induced injury, NCAPD3 deficiency was associated with significantly increased epithelial 24 damage and elevation in the proinflammatory mediators MIP1 α/CCL3, MIP2/CXCL2, IL-9, IL-15, and IL-17. Notably, MIP1 α/CCL3 and 27 MIP2/CXCL2 are key chemokines in IBD that promote the recruitment of macrophages and neutrophils, 28 respectively, and their upregulation correlates with disease severity (29). Whilst it remains unclear whether 29 these changes reflect direct transcriptional dysregulation resulting from NCAPD3 loss or are secondary to 30 increased epithelial damage and permeability, this data suggests that NCAPD3 plays a role in restraining 31 inflammatory amplification following barrier disruption. Further studies are required to determine whether this 32 occurs through direct chromatin-mediated regulation of inflammatory gene programs or indirectly because of 33 worsened epithelial injury. 34 Consistent with our prior in vitro and Drosophila studies, epithelial NCAPD3 knockdown compromised 35 host defense during Salmonella infection. Deficits in 40 antimicrobial peptide production also have implications for IBD, as antimicrobial peptide levels can be reduced 41 even in the presence of pro-inflammatory signaling that typically upregulates their expression (32). As prior 42 enteric infections are known to increase IBD risk in genetically predisposed individuals (33), heightened tissue 43 damage during infection in the setting of reduced NCAPD3 may represent another mechanism linking epithelial 44 chromatin dysregulation to chronic inflammatory susceptibility. 45 Recent advances have highlighted broader roles for chromatin regulators in controlling transcriptional 46 programs governing epithelial differentiation and innate immunity (34).
