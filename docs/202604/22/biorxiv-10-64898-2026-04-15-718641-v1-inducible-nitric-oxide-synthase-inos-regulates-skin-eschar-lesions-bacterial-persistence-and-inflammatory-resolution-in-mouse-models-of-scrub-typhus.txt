26 Abstract 

27 Orientia tsutsugamushi (Ot ) is an obligately intracellular bacterium that causes scrub typhus, a 

28 potentially severe infectious disease characterized by systemic inflammation and multiorgan 

29 dysfunction. We recently reported a protective role for IFN-γ signaling in host defense against Ot 

30 infection; however, the underlying mechanisms remain obscure. Inducible nitric oxide synthase 

31 (iNOS, encoded by Nos2 ) is a key antimicrobial effector induced downstream of IFN-γ signaling. 

32 Here, we used transgenic mouse models to further investigate the biological functions of iNOS. 

33 We first revealed the requirement of iNOS for the restriction of Ot growth in cultured bone 

34 marrow-derived macrophages. Using an intradermal mouse model, we found that while tissues of 

35 Nos2 ⁻/⁻ and wild-type mice exhibited comparable bacterial burdens during acute infection phases, 

36 Nos2 ⁻/⁻ mice developed eschar-like lesions similar to those observed in Ifngr1 ⁻/⁻ mice, indicating a 

37 critical role for the IFN-γ/iNOS axis in regulating skin pathology in scrub typhus. Notably, Nos2 ⁻/⁻

38 mice displayed impaired bacterial clearance during the recovery phase (day 42), with persistent 

39 bacterial burdens in multiple organs accompanied by sustained immune activation and elevated 

40 inflammatory responses. Histopathological and biochemical analyses further revealed increased 

41 tissue damage and dysregulated physiological homeostasis in Nos2 -/- mice during recovery. 

42 Mechanistically, iNOS deficiency resulted in heightened myeloid cell activation and prolonged 

43 expression of proinflammatory mediators, suggesting a dual contribution of iNOS in both 

44 antimicrobial defense and inflammation resolution. Collectively, these findings provide new 

45 insight into IFN-γ-mediated defense mechanisms and imply the distinct roles of iNOS during 

46 different stages of scrub typhus.

49 Author summary 

50 Scrub typhus is a potentially severe infectious disease caused by the bacterium Orientia 

51 tsutsugamushi (Ot ), which is transmitted to humans through the bite of infected mites. Despite its 

52 global impact and expanding geographic distribution, the immune mechanisms that protect against 

53 this infection remain incompletely understood. In this study, we examined the role of inducible 

54 nitric oxide synthase (iNOS), an immune effector molecule that helps the host control infection. 

55 Using mouse models, we found that iNOS plays dual and stage-specific roles during Ot infection. 

56 Mice lacking iNOS developed dysregulated immune homeostasis during acute infection and 

57 exhibited skin lesions resembling the eschars observed in some patients with scrub typhus. In 

58 addition, these mice showed delayed bacterial clearance, prolonged inflammation, and increased 

59 tissue damage during the recovery phase. Our findings indicate that iNOS contributes not only to 

60 host antimicrobial defense but also to the control of excessive inflammation following infection. 

61 These results provide new insight into host defense mechanisms in scrub typhus and may help 

62 inform future therapeutic or preventive strategies.

70 71 Introduction 

72 Orientia tsutsugamushi (Ot ) is an obligately intracellular bacterium and the etiological agent of 

73 scrub typhus, an acute febrile illness that can progress to severe systemic inflammation and 

74 multiorgan dysfunction 1-3 . Scrub typhus was historically thought to be confined to the so-called 

75 “tsutsugamushi triangle” in the Asia-Pacific region; however, new cases have now been reported 

76 well beyond this area, including the Middle East and South America 4. It is estimated that there are 

77 more than one million new cases annually 5. A meta-analysis reported a pooled seroprevalence of 

78 10.73% among healthy individuals and 22.58% among febrile patients in endemic regions 6. More 

79 recently, molecular detection of Ot species has been documented in free-living Eutrombicula 

80 chiggers in North Carolina, USA 7. Consistently, in this region, antibodies reactive to Ot have also 

81 been detected in individuals presenting with skin eschars 8, the most pathognomonic clinical sign 

82 of scrub typhus, suggesting that scrub typhus may be underrecognized in the United States. The 

83 clinical presentation typically involves abrupt onset of fever with chills, along with nonspecific 

84 symptoms including headache, myalgia, sweating, and vomiting 9. Although scrub typhus is 

85 treatable with antibiotics such as doxycycline and azithromycin, diagnostic delay and antibiotic 

86 therapy failure can result in severe disease outcomes often associated with increased morbidity 

87 and mortality 10-12 . Currently, no licensed vaccine is available, largely due to the extensive antigenic 

88 diversity of Ot strains and an incomplete understanding of protective immunity 13-15 .

90 IFN-γ is a key immunoregulatory cytokine for host protection against intracellular pathogens 16,17 .

91 Upon binding to its receptor, IFN-γ activates the Janus kinase-signal transducer and activator of 

92 transcription 1 signaling pathway, leading to the induction of interferon-stimulated genes (ISGs) 

93 that suppress pathogen replication and enhance the expression of immune effector and signaling 

94 proteins. Ot infection elicits robust IFN-γ responses in scrub typhus patients and murine models 18-

95 25 . It is reported that scrub typhus patients exhibit markedly upregulated IFN-γ and Th1-associated 

96 cytokine CXCL10 18 . Using established inbred C57BL/6 and outbred CD-1 mouse models, our 

97 group has consistently demonstrated that Ot infection elicits robust IFN-γ responses, accompanied 

98 by the induction of CXCL10, across lymphoid and parenchymal organs 22,26 . More recently, we 

99 used a panel of transgenic mouse models and demonstrated a critical role of IFN-γ, but not IFN-

100 , in host protection against Ot infection 27 . We found that Ifngr1 -deficient mice were highly 

101 susceptible to infection and developed eschar-like lesions, a hallmark feature observed in a 

102 proportion of scrub typhus patients 27-29 . Although we have demonstrated a critical role of IFN-γ in 

103 scrub typhus, the mechanism underlying IFN-γ signaling-mediated control of Ot infection remains 

104 unclear. 

105 106 Inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS, encoded by Nos2 ), typically induced following STAT1 

107 activation, is a well-characterized antimicrobial effector molecule 30 . IFN-γ-STAT1 signaling 

108 directly and indirectly promotes iNOS transcription through cooperative interactions with 

109 additional transcription factors, including interferon regulatory factor-1 and NF-κB 31 . This results 

110 in the production of nitric oxide (NO) that contributes to pathogen clearance by exerting 

111 antimicrobial and immunomodulatory effects, particularly in activated M1 macrophages 32,33 . Ot ,

112 formerly called Rickettsia tsutsugamushi , is closely related to the Rickettsia genus; both infect 

113 endothelial and immune cells, leading to systemic inflammation, vascular dysfunction, and 

114 potentially severe multi-organ disease 34 . In Rickettsia rickettsii infection, NO-producing 

115 macrophages restricted bacterial growth in vitro , whereas pharmacological inhibition of iNOS 

116 abrogated this antimicrobial effect 35 . Similar findings have been reported in Rickettsia prowazekii 

117 infection, in which NO was shown to prevent bacterial invasion of host cells through direct 

118 bactericidal activity 36 . During Rickettsia parkeri infection, IFN-γ induces Nos2 expression in 

119 mouse primary macrophages, leading to the inhibition of bacterial growth in vitro 37 . In Ot infection, 

120 iNOS-expressing macrophages have been observed within infiltrative parenchymal nodules of 

121 multiple organs in a scrub typhus mouse model 38 . Consistently, in vitro studies demonstrated that 

122 IFN-γ inhibits Ot growth in mouse bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMDMs), whereas 

123 pharmacological inhibition of iNOS reverses the antibacterial effects of IFN-γ 38 . Our previous 

124 study also demonstrated that Ot infection can significantly increase Nos2 expression in mouse 

125 primary macrophages in vitro at 4 and 24 hours post-infection as compared with the mock control 24 .

126 Although these studies highlight the antimicrobial role of iNOS in vitro , its contribution to 

127 bacterial control and host immune homeostasis in vivo remains controversial. For example, both 

128 protective and nonessential roles for iNOS have been reported in mouse models of Mycobacterium 

129 tuberculosis infection 39,40 , highlighting the context-dependent and complex functions of iNOS in 

130 vivo . Given our demonstration of the critical role of IFN-γ in controlling Ot infection in vivo 27,41 ,

131 we sought to define the contribution of iNOS to Ot control and disease outcome in the mouse 

132 model of scrub typhus.

134 In this study, we employed our established intradermal scrub typhus mouse model in combination 

135 with transgenic mouse strains. Our results demonstrate that iNOS is required for restricting Ot 

136 growth in murine macrophages in vitro . Notably, Nos2 ⁻/⁻ mice developed eschars like those 

137 observed in Ifngr1 ⁻/⁻ mice, indicating a critical role for the IFN-γ/iNOS axis in preventing skin 

138 lesion formation. Although Nos2 ⁻/⁻ mice showed comparable bacterial burdens as compared to 

139 wild-type (WT) mice during acute infection, they exhibited higher inflammatory responses in 

140 organs and dysregulated systemic homeostasis relative to WT controls. More importantly, the 

141 absence of Nos2 resulted in delayed bacterial clearance during the recovery phase, leading to 

142 persistent inflammatory responses in various organs. Collectively, these findings define an 

143 essential role for iNOS in Ot infection and provide new insight into host protective immunity in 

144 scrub typhus.

284 Dynamic patterns of Ifng /Stat1 /Nos2 expression in mouse tissues following Ot infection 

285 We have previously reported that Ot -infected mice develop a type 1-skewed immune response, as 

286 evidenced by markedly elevated IFN-γ expression 22,23,46 . To characterize the temporal dynamics 

287 of iNOS-related signaling during infection, we intradermally infected mice with the Ot Karp strain 

288 and quantified Ifng , Stat1 , and Nos2 transcript levels in multiple organs at indicated time points 

289 (Figure 1 ). qRT-PCR analysis revealed significant upregulation of Ifng expression in the spleen, 

290 draining lymph nodes (dLN), lungs, and liver on 7 or 14 dpi. While Stat1 levels in the spleen and 

291 dLN were modestly increased, their levels in the lungs and liver were markedly upregulated. Nos2 

292 expression was also significantly upregulated across multiple tissues, with particularly strong and 

293 early induction in the liver. We also found that Nos2 upregulation in the lungs was relatively 

294 delayed and was primarily evident at 14 dpi. Notably, elevated Nos2 expression persisted in both 

295 the liver and spleen at 42 dpi, indicating that iNOS may contribute to host defense during the acute 

296 phase, as well as to immune regulation during the recovery phase of Ot infection.

434 435 Discussion 

436 iNOS, which is expressed in response to proinflammatory stimuli (e.g., LPS, IFN-γ and TNF-α), 

437 acts as a crucial mediator of immune activation, inflammation, and host defense 50 . As a hallmark 

438 functional marker of M1 macrophages, iNOS is primarily induced through the classical IFN-

439 γ/STAT1 pathway in synergy with pattern-recognition receptor signaling (e.g., TLRs) 51 . In 

440 infectious diseases, the role of iNOS has been investigated extensively, suggesting that iNOS can 

441 act as a double-edged sword 52-54 . While iNOS-derived NO mediates pathogen killing and exerts 

442 anti-inflammatory effects 30,33,35,55,56 , iNOS can also drive excessive inflammation, contributing to 

443 blood-brain barrier disruption in bacterial meningitis 57 , increased mortality and morbidity during 

444 influenza A infection 58 , and heightened susceptibility to fungal infections 59 .

445 446 The antibacterial role of iNOS has been well documented in studies of Rickettsia species, which 

447 are closely related to Ot . Activated, nitric oxide-producing macrophages restrict Rickettsia 

448 rickettsii replication, whereas pharmacologic inhibition of iNOS abolishes this growth restriction 35 .

449 Similarly, early Rickettsia prowazekii infection is suppressed in L929 cells treated with IFN-γ plus 

450 TNF-α, and this effect is reversed by iNOS inhibition 36 . More recently, IFN-I has been shown to 

451 induce iNOS expression in an IRF5-dependent manner, contributing to control of Rickettsia 

452 parkeri infection 60 . In sharp contrast, very little is known regarding the precise role of iNOS

454 patients and in different mouse models 18,22,46 . Elevated IFN-γ, in combination with 

455 proinflammatory cytokines such as TNF-α, can potently induce iNOS expression 61 . We previously 

456 demonstrated in vivo that TNF-α and IFN-γ signals are critical for host protection against Ot 

457 infection 27,45 , indicating downstream molecules such as iNOS may play a role in controlling Ot 

458 infection. In scrub typhus patients, iNOS expression is markedly increased, particularly in alveolar 

459 macrophages and damaged lung tissues 62 . Consistently, histochemical analyses reveal iNOS 

460 expression across multiple organs, where it colocalizes with activated macrophages/microglia in 

461 murine models of scrub typhus 38 . Collectively, these reports from human studies and experimental 

462 animal models support a critical role for iNOS in mediating host protective responses against Ot 

463 infection. 

464 465 In this study, we aimed to define the functional role of iNOS in Ot infection using our established 

466 intradermal infection model. We first characterized the kinetics of iNOS expression across organs

468 in multiple tissues (Figure 1). Consistent with the previous report 38 , Nos2 expression peaked 

469 around 14 dpi, with the highest levels observed in the liver. Notably, Nos2 expression remained 

470 elevated in the spleen and liver at 42 dpi, suggesting a potential role for iNOS in the recovery and 

471 resolution phase of infection. We next employed two complementary approaches ( Nos2 -/- cells and 

472 iNOS selective inhibitor treatment) to assess the anti-Ot function of iNOS in macrophages (Figure 

473 2). Our results demonstrated that iNOS is required for effective restriction of bacterial growth in 

474 activated macrophages. In line with these findings, immunostaining using an anti-TSA47

519 520 Although we found that iNOS was required for activated macrophage-mediated restriction of Ot 

521 in vitro (Figure 2), its apparently dispensable role in restricting Ot during the acute phase in vivo 

522 remains unclear. Based on our findings and recent studies, we propose several explanations. First, 

523 Ot may evade macrophage-mediated innate defense in vivo . Following intradermal infection, 

524 although macrophages can phagocytose bacteria, dendritic cells appear to be the primary carriers 

525 that transport bacteria from the skin to dLN 69 . This trafficking route may allow the pathogen to 

526 evade macrophage-mediated killing, effectively infect lymphatic endothelial cells, and ultimately 

527 disseminate into visceral organs. Second, optimal iNOS induction typically requires both 

528 pathogen-derived signals (e.g., LPS) and proinflammatory cytokines such as IFN-γ, TNF-α, and 

529 IL-1β. However, Ot lacks LPS 70 , a potent inducer of iNOS and NO production. As a result, NO 

530 levels in the skin and lymphatic tissues may be insufficient during early infection, enabling 

531 bacterial growth and immune evasion. Third, T cell-mediated immunity, particularly CD8 ⁺ T cells, 

532 plays a dominant role in controlling infection in vivo 71,72 . Although iNOS deficiency has been 

533 demonstrated to enhance Th1 responses during bacterial and viral infection 73-75 , we did not find 

534 major differences in T cell or NK cell activation between WT and Nos2 ⁻/⁻ mice during the acute 

535 phase (Figure 5), suggesting that iNOS may play a marginal role in shaping early lymphocyte 

536 responses in this context. Finally, Ot infection induces robust type 1 immune responses and 

537 upregulates numerous ISGs. Certain ISGs, such as members of the guanylate-binding protein 

538 family 41 , may serve as key antimicrobial effectors that compensate for the loss of iNOS signals 60 .

539 Further studies are needed to identify the critical ISGs responsible for bacterial control and to 

540 define their underlying mechanisms.

541 542 Our study has several limitations that warrant further studies. The intradermal inoculation used 

543 here mimics the natural route of Ot infection, but it is a non-lethal model in WT B6 mouse 

544 models 38,76,77 . It remains unclear as to how iNOS-mediated responses contribute to Ot clearance 

545 and/or cellular injury in lethal models (e.g., intravenous inoculation) 22,42 . Given the known 

546 differences in human and murine cells in their capacity to induce iNOS 37,78 , it will be important to 

547 examine the role of iNOS in the control of Ot growth in human cells (e.g., macrophages). Since 

548 Ot can replicate in endothelial cells, which produce relatively low NO concentrations via eNOS 

549 for vascular function and metabolic homeostasis 79,80 , it will be interesting to examine whether 

550 iNOS-derived NO can help control Ot infection in endothelial cells. Future studies are still needed 

551 to elucidate the mechanisms by which iNOS contributes to Ot clearance and disease outcomes. 

552 Collectively, our findings reveal the first in vivo role of an IFN-γ/iNOS axis in scrub typhus and 

553 offer new insights into host immune defense against Ot infection.
